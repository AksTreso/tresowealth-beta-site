The defense of returns requires a surgical understanding of the IFSC regulatory code. --- ## The Regulatory Architecture: The Tax Shield The primary allure of GIFT City for Saudi HNIs is the categorical exemption from the otherwise punitive Indian tax code. This is not achieved through loopholes, but through specific statutory provisions designed to make the IFSC globally competitive. ### 1. Section 10(4D) and Notification No. 72/2020: The Core Exemption The cornerstone of the GIFT City tax advantage is **Section 10(4D) of the Income Tax Act, 1961**, read with **CBDT Notification No. 72/2020/INCOME-TAX**. Under standard Indian domestic law, income earned by a foreign investor from Indian securities is subject to withholding tax, capital gains tax, and dividend distribution tax (though the latter was abolished domestically, structural friction remains). Section 10(4D) provides a blanket exemption to "specified income" of certain categories of funds located in the IFSC. **What this means for Saudi HNIs:** If a Category I or Category II AIF established in GIFT City complies with the stipulations of Notification No. 72/2020, its specified income—which includes interest, dividends, and long-term/short-term capital gains from investments made in India—is entirely exempt from Indian income tax. *Skeptical Caution:* This exemption is conditional. The AIF must be genuinely located in GIFT City, meaning its heart of operations and decision-making must reside within the IFSC. A shell entity with a GIFT City address but managed externally will not survive a tax audit. Saudi HNIs must conduct rigorous due diligence on the fund manager’s physical and operational substance within GIFT City. ### 2. Section 9A: The Pass-Through Paradigm The second critical pillar is **Section 9A** of the Income Tax Act, which governs the taxation of offshore funds. Historically, foreign investors feared that investing directly into Indian entities would create a "Permanent Establishment" (PE) in India, thereby subjecting their global income to Indian taxation. For GIFT City AIFs, Section 9A provides a safe harbor. It stipulates that funds located in the IFSC, managed by IFSC fund managers, will not constitute a PE in India, provided specific conditions regarding fund size, investor count, and remuneration of the manager are met. **The Crucial Interplay (Section 9A vs. 10(4D)):** The regulatory framework mandates an election. A GIFT City AIF can claim benefits under Section 9A *or* Section 10(4D), but not both concurrently. For Saudi HNIs, the optimal structural path is almost universally the Section 10(4D) route. Why? Because claiming Section 9A benefits fundamentally alters the tax mechanics. If a fund claims Section 9A, it operates as a pass-through vehicle for tax purposes. Conversely, if the fund claims Section 10(4D), the fund itself is exempt from tax on specified income. **Compliance Implications:** Saudi HNIs receive pass-through treatment only under specific structural conditions. If the AIF claims the Section 10(4D) exemption, the income is tax-free in India, but the character of the income is extinguished at the fund level. When repatriated to the Saudi investor, it is treated as a return of capital or tax-free distribution. Because Saudi Arabia imposes no personal income tax, the Section 10(4D) exemption effectively creates a zero-tax jurisdiction for the entire investment lifecycle. However, if the fund is structured to claim Section 9A benefits (pass-through), the Saudi HNI might technically be required to file tax returns in India to claim treaty benefits, an administrative burden that defeats the purpose of the GIFT City structure. The TresoWealth recommendation is unequivocal: Mandate that your chosen GIFT City AIF operates under the Section 10(4D) / Notification 72/2020 framework, explicitly waiving Section 9A benefits. --- ## The Saudi HNI Perspective: Structural Imperatives Saudi capital operates under distinct constraints and opportunities. The Kingdom's 0% personal income tax regime means that tax efficiency is not about utilizing foreign tax credits; it is about avoiding Indian tax liability entirely and eliminating administrative friction. ### Repatriation and the DTAA India and Saudi Arabia share a Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA). Under the standard DTAA, capital gains are taxable in the source country (India). However, for a GIFT City AIF claiming the Section 10(4D) exemption, the income is legally exempt from Indian tax. Therefore, the DTAA becomes a secondary safety net rather than the primary shield. Saudi HNIs must ensure that their corporate structuring—often utilizing holding companies in the DIFC or ADGM before routing capital to GIFT City—does not inadvertently trigger "Limitation of Benefits" (LoB) clauses. The ultimate beneficiary must be a bona fide resident of Saudi Arabia to claim treaty protection if the Section 10(4D) exemption is ever challenged. ### Currency and Exchange Control Risks GIFT City operates as a distinct financial zone with its own financial regulator (the IFSCA) and operates primarily in USD. This is a critical feature for Saudi HNIs. Capital is deployed in USD, and returns are repatriated in USD, entirely bypassing the Reserve Bank of India’s (RBI) stringent exchange control regulations that plague the domestic Indian market. *Skeptical Caution:* While the IFSC is insulated, the underlying assets are often Indian rupee-denominated. A 65% YoY growth in commitments does not immunize the portfolio from INR depreciation. The structural tax shield of Section 10(4D) is irrelevant if the underlying alpha is eroded by currency depreciation. Hedging strategies must be evaluated at the AIF level. --- ## Actionable Insights: The TresoWealth Due Diligence Framework Deploying capital into a GIFT City AIF requires more than a subscription agreement; it requires structural verification. Saudi HNIs must enforce the following due diligence protocols before committing capital: ### 1. Verify the Section 10(4D) Election Do not assume the AIF is tax-exempt. Request statutory documentation proving the fund has made the election under Section 10(4D) read with Notification No. 72/2020, and confirm it has explicitly opted out of Section 9A. This dictates whether your distributions are tax-free or subject to Indian withholding tax. ### 2. Audit the "Substance" of the Fund Manager The CBDT is increasingly scrutinizing GIFT City entities for being "shell operations." Verify that the fund manager maintains genuine economic substance in GIFT City: * Does the manager have a physical office within the IFSC? * Are investment committee meetings held within the IFSC? * Does the manager employ a minimum of 10 full-time employees (as mandated by IFSCA regulations)? * Does the manager incur the minimum statutory expenditure requirements in India? A failure in substance can result in the retroactive denial of the Section 10(4D) exemption, leaving the Saudi HNI exposed to standard Indian tax rates (up to 40% plus surcharges). ### 3. Scrutinize the "Specified Income" Parameters Section 10(4D) only exempts "specified income." If the AIF engages in non-specified activities—such as proprietary trading unrelated to the fund’s stated objective, or certain derivative strategies not covered under the notification—the income from those activities becomes taxable in India. The Private Placement Memorandum (PPM) must be cross-referenced with the boundaries of Notification No. 72/2020. ### 4. Assess the ₹10.14 Lakh Crore Concentration Risk The broader Indian AIF industry AUM reached ₹10.14 lakh crore (~$122 billion) as of March 31, 2024. A significant portion of this is concentrated in a few top-performing managers. Saudi HNIs must be wary of "capacity-constrained" strategies. If a GIFT City AIF is simply a mirror portfolio of an over-saturated domestic fund, the alpha is likely diluted. Ensure the GIFT City mandate offers distinct, uncorrelated deal flow, particularly in infrastructure, credit, or early-stage venture, rather than late-stage private equity where valuations are stretched. ### 5. Monitor the $100 Billion Target Trajectory The IFSCA has set an aggressive target of $100 billion in total AUM by March 2027. While this signals regulatory support and liquidity, it also introduces systemic risk. Rapid scaling often leads to relaxed underwriting standards. Saudi HNIs must demand granular portfolio transparency, resisting the temptation to rely solely on the brand equity of the fund sponsor. --- ## Conclusion: Protected Alpha in a Zero-Tax Corridor The GIFT City AIF framework represents one of the most robust tax-efficient gateways for Saudi capital to access the Indian growth story. The statutory shield provided by Section 10(4D) and Notification No. 72/2020, combined with the USD-denominated operational freedom of the IFSC, aligns perfectly with the wealth preservation and growth mandates of Saudi family offices. However, the $10.2 billion deployed between 2024 and 2026 proves that the secret is out. As capital flows compound, regulatory scrutiny will inevitably tighten. Relying on the mere existence of a GIFT City address is structurally reckless.
