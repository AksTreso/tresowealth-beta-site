GIFT City AIF Tax Guide for DIFC EAMs

Executive Summary: The Corridor Calculation
As of April 2026, the capital corridor between the Dubai International Financial Centre (DIFC) and the Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) has matured from a theoretical arbitrage play into a structurally significant conduit for foreign direct investment into India. For DIFC-based External Asset Managers (EAMs), the Indian International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) offers a regulated, tax-advantaged gateway to deploy capital into onshore Indian assets. However, the structural allure of GIFT City is matched only by the operational complexity of its regulatory architecture.

This TresoWealth guide deconstructs the tax and regulatory framework for DIFC EAMs establishing or managing Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) in GIFT City. We strip away the marketing rhetoric to examine the statutory mechanics, quantify the actual exposure, and identify the hidden compliance frictions that trap the unprepared.

1. The Macro Landscape: Capital Gravity and Scale

To understand the strategic imperative for DIFC EAMs, one must first quantify the capital gravity of both jurisdictions. The DIFC currently oversees $425 billion in Total Assets Under Management. This staggering pool of capital is perpetually seeking yield, diversification, and high-growth emerging market exposure. India remains a primary target for this capital, but onshore regulatory friction and tax drag have historically limited allocation efficiency.

GIFT City was engineered to solve this exact friction. As of the most current 2024-2026 data windows, GIFT City IFSC has accumulated $10.8 billion in Total Assets Under Management. While a fraction of the DIFC’s scale, the growth trajectory is steep and heavily concentrated in alternative investments. Furthermore, the IFSC has facilitated $3.35 billion in Total Foreign Direct Investment as of 2024 (Under IFSCA AIF Regulations), serving as the primary conduit for offshore capital bypassing onshore regulatory bottlenecks.

For DIFC EAMs, the calculus is straightforward: GIFT City provides the only legally ring-fenced structure that allows offshore capital to access Indian onshore assets while retaining offshore tax treatment. But executing this requires navigating a triad of overlapping regulations—IFSCA, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), and the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT).

2. The Structural Mechanics: IFSCA AIF Regulations

The International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) governs the establishment and operation of AIFs within GIFT City. For a DIFC EAM, the IFSCA AIF Regulations provide a bespoke framework distinct from the onshore Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) AIF regulations.

Key Structural Advantages:
* Sponsorship and Management: A DIFC EAM can act as the Investment Manager or Sponsor of a GIFT City AIF without requiring a fully staffed onshore presence in India. The EAM must simply register with IFSCA as a Foreign Portfolio Investor (FPI) or operate under the IFSCA AIF framework.
* Capital Mobility: GIFT City AIFs are treated as offshore vehicles for Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) purposes. This allows them to repatriate capital freely, a critical requirement for DIFC-based limited partners (LPs) who cannot tolerate lock-in risks.
* Onshore Deployment: A GIFT City AIF can invest in onshore India under the FPI route or the Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) route, depending on the asset class. The $3.35 billion in FDI facilitated under these regulations underscores the operational viability of this structure for private equity and venture capital strategies.

The Skeptical View: While IFSCA provides the structural blueprint, it does not grant tax immunity. The tax efficiency of a GIFT City AIF is entirely dependent on the manager's ability to strictly comply with the Income Tax Act, 1961, and RBI foreign exchange mandates. A failure in one regulatory pillar collapses the entire tax shield.

3. The Tax Architecture: Decoding the Incentives

The tax framework for GIFT City AIFs is governed by specific carve-outs within the Income Tax Act. For DIFC EAMs, two sections are paramount: Section 10(4D) and Section 9A.

A. Section 10(4D): The 10-Year Holiday

Section 10(4D) of the Income Tax Act, 1961, read with CBDT Notification S.O. 3902, provides the foundational tax incentive for GIFT City AIFs. It stipulates that specified income received by a GIFT City AIF from its investments is exempt from Indian income tax.

Operational Mechanics:
* The Holiday Window: The exemption is not perpetual. It applies for a consecutive period of 10 financial years, which the fund can elect to commence within a specified timeframe. Current data windows indicate that the 10-year holiday extends through 2034/2035 for funds established in the current cycle.
* Specified Income: The exemption covers interest, dividends, and long-term/short-term capital gains arising from investments made by the AIF.
* The Catch (S.O. 3902): CBDT Notification S.O. 3902 strictly defines the conditions. The AIF must be regulated by IFSCA, and critically, the fund must file its return of income within the stipulated timeline. Furthermore, the exemption is strictly limited to income distributed to unit holders; it does not shield the fund from withholding obligations if administrative protocols are breached.

TresoWealth Protective Insight: The 10-year holiday is a deferral mechanism, not a permanent structural shield. DIFC EAMs must model fund lifecycles against the 2034/2035 sunset. If a private equity fund anticipates a 12-year horizon with exits in year 11, the terminal return will be subject to full Indian capital gains tax. The structuring must account for this tail risk today, not in year 9.

B. Section 9A: The Fund Management Safe Harbor

Historically, the mere presence of an EAM making investment decisions for an offshore fund constituted a "business connection" in India, dragging the entire fund into the Indian tax net. Section 9A of the Income Tax Act, 1961, read with CBDT Notification S.O. 1790(E), provides a safe harbor from this Permanent Establishment (PE) risk.

Operational Mechanics:
Section 9A stipulates that if an eligible investment fund (including a GIFT City AIF) meets specific conditions, the activity of the EAM acting on its behalf shall not constitute a business connection in India.

The Strict Conditions:
1. The fund must have at least 25 investors (with no single investor holding more than 25% of the fund).
2. The EAM must be registered with SEBI or IFSCA.
3. The remuneration paid to the EAM must not be less than arm's length price.
4. The fund must maintain proper documentation and file prescribed statements with the Indian tax authorities.

TreoWealth Skeptical Analysis: Section 9A compliance is binary; there is no materiality threshold. If a DIFC EAM breaches the 25% concentration limit for a single LP, the safe harbor shatters for the entire fund. This triggers a catastrophic PE event, subjecting the fund's global income to Indian taxation. EAMs must build hard-coded compliance blocks in their fund administration software to prevent inadvertent breaches of the 25% threshold during capital call or redemption events.

4. The RBI Foreign Exchange Conduit

Tax efficiency is irrelevant if capital cannot move. The foreign exchange framework governing GIFT City AIFs is dictated by the Reserve Bank of India.

A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 12 / RBI/FMRD/FID/2023-24/12 is the critical regulatory reference governing Foreign Exchange regulations for GIFT City. This circular outlines the framework for Foreign Portfolio Investors and AIFs operating within the IFSC.

Key Provisions:
* Onshore Repatriation: The circular clarifies the mechanisms through which GIFT City AIFs can bring capital onshore to invest in Indian securities under the FPI framework, and subsequently repatriate the proceeds.
* Sponsor Capital: DIFC EAMs acting as sponsors must adhere to the minimum capitalization norms defined under this circular. The RBI mandates that capital brought into the IFSC must be strictly segregated and accounted for before it is deployed onshore.
* Banking Integration: GIFT City AIFs must maintain accounts with IFSC Banking Units (IBUs). Circular No. 12 dictates the reporting requirements for these IBUs to the RBI, ensuring that all capital flows are tracked in real-time.

TresoWealth Protective Insight: Do not assume DIFC banking norms apply. The RBI’s reporting infrastructure is notoriously rigid. Delays in filing transaction reports under Circular No. 12 can result in compliance holds, freezing the AIF’s ability to repatriate capital. EAMs must retain specialized IFSC compliance counsel to manage the daily/monthly RBI reporting cadence.

5. The Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements (DTAA) Factor

A critical, often overlooked advantage for DIFC EAMs is the interaction between GIFT City structures and the India-UAE Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA).

Because the UAE does not levy corporate income tax on qualifying income (prior to the implementation of the UAE Corporate Tax in 2023, and still largely exempt for qualifying fund managers), the tax-exempt income generated by a GIFT City AIF under Section 10(4D) flows through to the DIFC LPs without suffering Indian withholding tax (subject to Section 9A compliance).

However, post-2023 UAE Corporate Tax implementation, DIFC EAMs must ensure their fund vehicles qualify as "Exempt Persons" or "Qualifying Investment Funds" under UAE Federal Decree-Law No. 47 of 2022. If the GIFT City AIF is deemed a taxable entity in the UAE, the DTAA benefits erode, creating a double-taxation risk that negates the entire premise of the structure.

6. Actionable Playbook for DIFC EAMs

To successfully leverage the GIFT City AIF structure, DIFC EAMs must implement a rigorous, protective operational framework.

1. Stress-Test Section 9A Diligence:
Do not rely on side letters to manage the 25% concentration rule. Implement a hard lock in the fund's subscription agreement and transfer agency platform that automatically restricts additional capital calls from LPs approaching the 25% threshold. A single breach invalidates the safe harbor.

2. Model the 2034/2035 Sunset:
When drafting the fund's term sheet, explicitly model the exit timeline against the Section 10(4D) 10-year holiday. For funds targeting illiquid infrastructure or real estate, establish an extension mechanism that accounts for the reality that exits post-2035 will face a 10% to 15% tax drag on long-term capital gains.

3. Align RBI Reporting with Fund Administration:
Ensure your chosen IFSC fund administrator has a direct integration with an IBU that has a proven track record of complying with A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 12 / RBI/FMRD/FID/2023-24/12. Request audit reports of the administrator's RBI filing history before onboarding.

4. Substance Over Form:
The CBDT has aggressively challenged offshore structures that lack economic substance. A GIFT City AIF cannot be a mere shell. Ensure the IFSCA-registered entity maintains demonstrable substance—physical office space, qualified investment professionals in GIFT City, and local decision-making documentation. The EAM in DIFC can direct strategy, but the execution and risk management must be visible within the IFSC.

5. UAE Corporate Tax Alignment:
Prior to launching the GIFT City AIF, obtain a ruling or formal assessment from the UAE Federal Tax Authority confirming the vehicle’s exempt status. This ensures the Indian tax exemption under Section 10(4D) is not swallowed by a 9% UAE corporate tax liability.

However, this efficiency is contingent on absolute precision. The $10.8 billion currently managed in GIFT City, and the $3.35 billion in FDI generated, are the result of managers who respect the rigidity of the Indian regulatory apparatus. The Indian tax authorities do not interpret ambiguity in favor of the taxpayer.
