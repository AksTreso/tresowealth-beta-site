How Can Foreigners Invest in GIFT CITY AIFs?

Demystifying GIFT City AIFs: A Strategic Guide for Foreign Investors

India’s growth narrative remains a focal point for global capital allocation. However, traditional foreign investment routes into Indian alternative assets have historically been encumbered by regulatory friction, opaque tax structures, and capital lock-in realities. The Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City)—India’s first International Financial Services Centre (IFSC)—was engineered to neutralize these inefficiencies.

For foreign investors, specifically Non-Resident Indians (NRIs), Overseas Citizens of India (OCIs), and foreign portfolio investors (FPIs), Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) domiciled in GIFT City offer a structurally superior conduit for India exposure. Yet, the architecture is not without friction. At TresoWealth, our analysis cuts through the jurisdictional marketing to examine the precise regulatory mechanics, operational realities, and benchmarking frameworks that foreign capital must navigate when allocating to GIFT City AIFs.

---

The Regulatory Architecture: Who Can Invest and How?

The IFSC operates as a jurisdictional sandbox, physically within India but economically offshore. Foreign investors looking to access GIFT City AIFs must understand the interplay between the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) and the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI).

Eligible Investor Classes

Under the SEBI (Alternative Investment Funds) Regulations, 2012, as adapted by IFSCA, the following foreign entities can invest in GIFT City AIFs:

1. **Non-Resident Indians (NRIs) and OCIs:** Subject to compliance with the Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) guidelines.

2. **Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs):** Registered with SEBI, either in the IFSC or onshore.

3. **Foreign Companies and Trusts:** Corporate bodies incorporated outside India.

4. **Resident Indians:** Strictly prohibited from investing in IFSC-based AIFs, maintaining the offshore integrity of the centre.

The Regulatory Statutes

Investment flows are governed by:

  • **IFSCA (Fund Management) Regulations, 2022:** This is the master statute dictating AIF operations within GIFT City. It allows for greater operational flexibility compared to onshore SEBI AIF regulations, including the ability to launch multiple schemes under a single AIF umbrella.
  • **FEMA (Non-Debt Instrument) Rules, 2019:** Governs the pricing and repatriation of capital.
  • **RBI IFSC Banking Unit (IBU) Framework:** Dictates the banking infrastructure required for capital movement.

**TresoWealth Insight:** Do not assume IFSCA regulations are a complete departure from onshore SEBI rules. IFSCA has largely "adapted" SEBI regulations. While relaxations exist (e.g., lower minimum investment thresholds), compliance reporting and fiduciary duties remain rigorously aligned with onshore standards.

The Operational Playbook: Step-by-Step Allocation

Allocating capital to a GIFT City AIF requires a specific operational sequence. Missing a step in the banking or KYC chain is the primary reason foreign capital faces delayed deployments.

Step 1: Establish an IFSC Banking Unit (IBU) Account

Foreign investors cannot route capital from standard overseas bank accounts directly into a GIFT City AIF. The capital must be remitted to an IBU account.

  • **Actionable Step:** Open a USD-denominated IBU account with a bank operating within GIFT City (e.g., State Bank of India IFSC, HSBC IFSC, Yes Bank IFSC).
  • **Friction Point:** KYC norms for opening an IBU account can take 3 to 6 weeks, depending on the investor's jurisdiction of origin and the complexity of the Ultimate Beneficial Owner (UBO) structure.

Step 2: Fulfill SEBI KYC and CP Requirements

Foreign investors must complete Know Your Customer (KYC) and Central KYC (CKYC) norms.

  • **Actionable Step:** Submit notarized and apostilled identity and address proofs, along with UBO declarations if investing through a corporate vehicle.
  • **Skeptical Note:** The CKYC registry in India often rejects foreign documentation due to formatting discrepancies. Engage a SEBI-registered Registrar and Transfer Agent (RTA) with specific IFSC experience to pre-vet documents.

Step 3: Capital Commitment and Drawdown

The minimum commitment size for a GIFT City AIF is currently standardized at **$100,000** (compared to ₹1 Crore onshore).

  • **Actionable Step:** Execute the Subscription Agreement and Private Placement Memorandum (PPM). Capital is typically called in tranches (drawdowns) by the AIF manager. Ensure your IBU account maintains sufficient liquidity to meet drawdown notices, which usually require funding within 10 to 15 business days.

Step 4: Repatriation

GIFT City operates on a fully repatriable basis. Once the AIF realizes returns, capital and profits are credited back to the investor’s IBU account, free to be wired to any global jurisdiction, subject to the AIF's scheme tenure concluding.

The Tax Arbitrage: Analyzing the Fiscal Incentive

The primary driver for foreign capital entering GIFT City is the fiscal arbitrage, specifically the tax regime under the **IFSC Act, 2019**, and the Income Tax Act, 1961.

Key Tax Provisions:

1. **Section 80LA:** AIFs and Fund Managers set up in GIFT City are entitled to a 100% tax exemption on specified income for any 10 consecutive years out of the first 15 years of operation. This fundamentally alters the expense ratio dynamics for the fund.

2. **Capital Gains Tax:** For foreign investors without a permanent establishment in India, the tax liability on capital gains depends on the underlying asset and the Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA) between India and the investor’s tax residency country. However, because the AIF is domiciled in the IFSC, the structuring often mitigates the direct onshore capital gains triggers.

3. **GST Exemption:** Fund management services provided to AIFs in GIFT City are exempt from the 18% GST levied on onshore AIFs. This directly reduces the management fee burden.

**TresoWealth Skepticism:** The 10-year tax holiday is a powerful incentive, but it is not perpetual. Investors must model the fund’s lifecycle against the sunset clause. Furthermore, global tax frameworks—specifically the OECD’s Pillar Two Global Minimum Tax (15%)—may erode the tax arbitrage for large multinational investors in the near future. The structural efficiency of GIFT City must be the primary value driver, not merely the tax delta.

Benchmarking GIFT City Equity Funds: The Critical Flaw

One of the most overlooked aspects of evaluating GIFT City AIFs is benchmarking. If you are a foreign investor allocating USD, comparing a GIFT City equity AIF to the onshore Nifty 50 is an analytical error that will distort performance expectations.

What Benchmarks Should NRIs Use for GIFT City Investments?

When evaluating **Benchmarks for GIFT City Equity Funds**, investors must account for currency risk, offshore market dynamics, and the cost of capital.

**1. The Currency Mismatch (The INR vs. USD Trap)**

A GIFT City AIF investing in onshore Indian equities generates INR returns. However, the foreign investor’s functional currency is usually USD. The Nifty 50 is an INR-denominated index. If the Nifty 50 returns 12% in a year, but the INR depreciates by 4% against the USD, the actual USD return is closer to 7.5%.

  • **Actionable Benchmark:** Foreign investors must demand **USD-denominated benchmarks**. The standard metric should be the **Nifty 50 USD Index** or the **MSCI India Index (USD)**. These indices automatically factor in the INR/USD exchange rate, providing a true reflection of the foreign investor's economic reality.

**2. The Offshore Premium/Discount**

GIFT City AIFs often invest in onshore markets, but their fund structure is offshore. They compete with India-focused offshore funds domiciled in Singapore, Luxembourg, or the Cayman Islands.

  • **Actionable Benchmark:** Compare the GIFT City AIF’s performance against the **MSCI India NR USD** (Net Return USD). This is the global institutional standard for foreign capital accessing Indian equities. If a GIFT City AIF cannot outperform the MSCI India NR USD over a full market cycle, the active management premium is unjustified.

**3. Private Market Equivalents (For Cat II and Cat III AIFs)**

If the GIFT City AIF is a Category II fund investing in unlisted equities or private credit, public market indices are inadequate.

  • **Actionable Benchmark:** Use a **Public Market Equivalent (PME)** methodology, specifically the **MSCI Emerging Markets Private Equity Index (USD)**. Furthermore, for private credit AIFs, the benchmark should be the **SOFR + Spread** (e.g., SOFR + 400 bps), reflecting the true cost of offshore credit capital.

**TresoWealth Verdict:** Any GIFT City AIF manager presenting performance metrics or target returns against the Nifty 50 (INR) to a foreign investor is either naive or intentionally obfuscating currency drag. Demand USD-denominated, offshore-equivalent benchmarks during the due diligence process.

Structural Risks and Frictions: A Realist’s Perspective

While the regulatory intent is to create a seamless offshore hub, the operational reality of GIFT City presents specific risks that foreign capital must price in.

1. The Liquidity Illusion

GIFT City AIFs are not listed on exchanges. Secondary market transfers are heavily restricted and require manager approval. Foreign investors must treat this capital as entirely illiquid for the duration of the fund’s tenure (typically 5 to 7 years for Cat II, longer for Cat I). There is no put option, and early redemption mechanisms are virtually non-existent.

2. Regulatory Fluidity

IFSCA is a relatively young regulator. It frequently issues circulars that alter operational guidelines, from the definition of "accredited investors" to the permissible limits of leverage for Cat III AIFs. This regulatory velocity means that the rules governing the AIF at deployment may shift before the fund winds down.

3. The "Onshore-Offshore" Execution Gap

While the AIF is domiciled in GIFT City, the underlying assets are often onshore. Capital must move from the IFSC IBU account through the onshore banking system to settle trades. Despite the RBI’s intent to streamline this, execution delays and FX conversion frictions can result in settlement slippage, particularly during periods of high market volatility.

---

Conclusion: Strategic Allocation or Regulatory Arbitrage?

For foreign investors, GIFT City AIFs represent a paradigm shift in accessing Indian alternative assets. The combination of a $100,000 minimum ticket size, SEBI-regulated oversight, 80LA tax exemptions, and seamless repatriation creates a highly efficient vehicle for India exposure.

However, the efficacy of this allocation depends entirely on rigorous due diligence. Investors must strip away the jurisdictional hype and demand structural clarity. This means insisting on USD-denominated benchmarks—such as the MSCI India NR USD or Nifty 50 USD—to accurately measure performance net of currency depreciation. It means modeling portfolio returns against the sunset of the 10-year tax holiday and the looming realities of the global minimum tax.

GIFT City is no longer an experiment; it is a functional gateway. But capital must cross that gateway with a precise understanding of its operational bottlenecks and benchmarking imperatives.
