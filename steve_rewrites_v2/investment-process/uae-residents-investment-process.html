GIFT CITY AIF Investment Process for UAE Residents

The GIFT City AIF Investment Process for UAE Residents: A Structured Framework for Capital Deployment

For UAE-based High Net Worth Individuals (HNIs), accessing Indian growth narratives has historically meant navigating a labyrinth of structural inefficiencies—ranging from adverse taxation on mutual funds to rigid repatriation constraints. The Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) was designed to eliminate these frictions. However, the architecture of GIFT City is highly specific. It is not a mere regulatory loophole; it is a distinct offshore jurisdiction requiring precise execution.

At TresoWealth, we observe a troubling gap between the marketing of GIFT City and the operational reality of deploying capital into its Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs). A miscalculation in regulatory alignment or benchmark selection can erode the very alpha an investor seeks. This article outlines the rigorous investment process for UAE residents allocating capital to GIFT City AIFs, stripping away the rhetoric to examine the regulatory mechanics, performance benchmarks, and structural pitfalls.

7 Misunderstandings That Cost NRIs Money in GIFT City

Before deploying capital, UAE investors must confront the structural realities that are frequently obscured by aggressive fund distribution. Based on TresoWealth’s analysis of cross-border capital flows, there are **7 things most NRIs misunderstand that cost them money**:

1. **Assuming GIFT City equates to zero Indian taxation:** While Category I and II AIFs in GIFT City enjoy a 100% tax holiday on capital gains for investors (under Section 10(4D) of the Income Tax Act), this does not absolve the investor of global tax liabilities in the UAE or their home jurisdiction, nor does it apply universally to all fund categories (e.g., certain Category III AIF structures).

2. **Conflating UAE Tax Residency with DTAA benefits:** Holding a UAE resident visa does not automatically guarantee Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA) benefits. A Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) from the UAE Ministry of Finance is mandatory, and the investor must satisfy the "tie-breaker" rules under Article 4 of the India-UAE DTAA.

3. **Ignoring the cost of capital conversion:** Funding an INR-denominated GIFT City AIF from AED or USD exposes the investor to currency drag. Failing to hedge or structuring the subscription in USD (where available) can reduce net IRR by 150-300 basis points annually.

4. **Treating AIF drawdowns like mutual fund SIPs:** AIFs utilize capital calls. Committing INR 10 Crore does not mean deploying INR 10 Crore on day one. Uncalled capital must remain highly liquid, yet investors often lock these funds in low-yield instruments, dragging down the blended portfolio return.

5. **Underestimating FATCA/CRS friction:** UAE banks are highly sensitive to downstream Indian investments. Incomplete FATCA/CRS declarations at the GIFT City banking stage routinely trigger frozen accounts and delayed capital calls.

6. **Mispricing the exit timeline:** GIFT City AIFs are illiquid. There is no secondary market. Investors misunderstanding the 5+2 year lock-in often face liquidity crunches, attempting premature redemptions that incur steep penalties (often 2-4% of NAV).

7. **Benchmarking against domestic Nifty 50 TRI:** Evaluating a GIFT City long-only equity AIF against a purely domestic, INR-denominated benchmark ignores the offshore risk premium, management fee drag, and the specific mandate of the IFSC fund.

The Regulatory Framework: Precision Over Assumptions

The regulatory perimeter for GIFT City AIFs is governed by a triad of authorities: the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA), the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI), and the Reserve Bank of India (RBI).

For a UAE resident, the process is underpinned by the IFSCA (Fund Management) Regulations, 2022. Unlike domestic Indian AIFs, which are constrained by RBI’s Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS) limits for repatriation, GIFT City AIFs are treated as offshore vehicles. Once the funds are routed from a UAE bank to an IFSC Banking Unit (IBU), they fall under the FEMA (Non-Debt Instrument) Rules, 2019, as amended for IFSC.

Crucially, the tax pass-through status for Category I and II AIFs ensures that capital gains are not taxed at the fund level in India. For UAE investors, this means the income retains its character. If the AIF realizes long-term capital gains (LTCG) on listed equities, and the fund is compliant with the 10(4D) exemption, the UAE resident faces 0% tax in India. However, this demands rigorous auditing. The AIF must be listed on a recognized stock exchange in the IFSC (such as the NSE IFSC or India INX), and the investor's jurisdiction must not be on the FATF grey list—currently a non-issue for the UAE, but a vital structural check.

The GIFT City AIF Investment Process: A Step-by-Step Execution

To mitigate operational risk, UAE investors must follow a structured deployment protocol.

Step 1: Regulatory Profiling and TRC Acquisition

Prior to initiating any subscription, the investor must secure a TRC from the UAE Federal Tax Authority. Without this, the Indian AIF is legally obligated to apply the highest marginal tax rate on any taxable distributions. The investor must also complete the CKYC (Central KYC) process specifically for IFSC entities, which operates distinctly from the domestic Indian CKYC registry.

Step 2: IBU Account Setup and Capital Repatriation

The investor wires USD/AED from their offshore bank to a designated IBU in GIFT City. Under FEMA regulations, the remittance must be explicitly tagged as an "Investment in IFSC AIF." The IBU converts the foreign currency to INR (if the AIF is INR-denominated) at the prevailing market rate. It is imperative to maintain a record of the conversion rate, as this establishes the cost of acquisition for future capital gains calculations in the investor's global tax jurisdiction.

Step 3: Subscription and Capital Commitment

The investor executes the Subscription Agreement and the AIF's Private Placement Memorandum (PPM). Here, the TresoWealth protective stance is critical: scrutinize the "Co-investment" and "Defaulting Investor" clauses. If a UAE resident fails to honor a capital call within the stipulated timeframe (usually 10-15 banking days), the AIF manager can declare the investor in default, forfeit up to 100% of their committed capital, and charge penalty interest.

Step 4: Drawdowns and Deployment

The AIF manager will issue Capital Call notices as investment opportunities are sourced. The investor must ensure that the uncalled capital is housed in a liquid, low-risk instrument in the UAE, ready for immediate SWIFT transfer to the IBU.

Step 5: Repatriation of Proceeds

Upon the realization of investments, the AIF distributes proceeds to the investor's IBU account. Under the IFSC framework, the IBU freely converts the INR back to USD/AED for repatriation to the UAE bank, free from the onerous repatriation netting requirements that plague domestic NRI investments.

Benchmarks: What Should NRIs Use for GIFT City Investments?

A fundamental error made by foreign investors is evaluating GIFT City AIFs against domestic benchmarks. This is structurally flawed. **What benchmarks should be used for GIFT City investments?**

GIFT City AIFs operate with an offshore risk premium, distinct management fee layers, and often, a specific sectoral or strategic mandate. When analyzing **benchmarks for GIFT City equity funds**, investors must differentiate between pure-play long-only funds and absolute-return strategies.

For GIFT City Equity Long-Only AIFs, the benchmark must reflect the offshore, USD-denominated reality of the investor's base currency. Therefore, the appropriate benchmark is the **MSCI India Total Return Net Index (USD)**, not the Nifty 50 TRI. The MSCI India USD index captures the INR depreciation risk that directly impacts a UAE resident’s net return.

For Long/Short or Absolute Return AIFs, a blended benchmark is required—typically a spread over the US Fed Funds Rate or the SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate), plus a premium of 400-600 basis points, depending on the volatility target of the fund.

An Abu Dhabi-based NRI invested INR 5 Crore into a GIFT City long-only equity fund in 2021. By 2023, the fund’s NAV had appreciated by 22%. However, the INR had depreciated against the USD by 5.5% over the same period. When the investor repatriated the funds to AED, the net return was severely eroded.

**TresoWealth Structuring:** The flaw was not in the AIF, but in the currency management. For subsequent commitments, we structured the investment into a USD-denominated GIFT City Category III AIF that systematically rolled over INR put options (USD calls) to hedge the currency exposure. While the hedging cost averaged 1.8% annually, it insulated the investor's base currency return, ensuring that the 22% gross alpha was not destroyed by macroeconomic forex drift.

Actionable Insights for UAE Investors

1. **Demand USD-Denominated Share Classes:** Where available, opt for USD-denominated AIFs within GIFT City. This forces the fund manager to handle the currency conversion and allows for clearer tracking of the MSCI India USD benchmark.

2. **Audit the PPM for Section 9A Compliance:** Ensure the AIF is structured to be a "Foreign Portfolio Investor" under the IFSCA framework. If the AIF breaches the 25% limit for a single corporate group, it risks being reclassified as a Foreign Direct Investment (FDI), fundamentally altering the exit tax dynamics.

3. **Scrutinize the High-Water Mark:** GIFT City AIFs typically charge a 1.5-2% management fee and a 15-20% performance fee over a hurdle rate. Ensure the hurdle rate is calculated on a compounding basis, not a simple basis, and that the high-water mark is perpetual, preventing managers from double-charging performance fees on recovered losses.

4. **Validate the Custodian:** Only utilize AIFs where the custodian is a Tier-1 IBU (e.g., HSBC IFSC, Standard Chartered IFSC). Smaller custodians may lack the SWIFT infrastructure for rapid capital call processing, causing UAE investors to default on drawdown notices due to settlement delays.

Conclusion

GIFT City represents a paradigm shift for UAE residents seeking Indian market exposure, offering an unprecedented combination of tax neutrality, simplified repatriation, and regulatory parity with global offshore centers. However, the architecture demands a skeptical and highly structured approach.

Assuming that GIFT City is merely a tax-free gateway to the Nifty is a guaranteed method of capital erosion. Success requires meticulous regulatory profiling, an uncompromising approach to currency and benchmark selection, and a deep structural audit of the AIF’s PPM.
