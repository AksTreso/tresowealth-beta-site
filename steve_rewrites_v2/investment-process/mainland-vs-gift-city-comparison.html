Mainland vs GIFT CITY AIF Comparison

Mainland vs. GIFT City AIFs: A Structural Breakdown for Foreign Capital

For foreign High Net Worth Individuals (HNIs) seeking alpha in India’s high-growth alternative investment landscape, the structural choice between a Mainland Alternative Investment Fund (AIF) and a GIFT City AIF is no longer a binary regulatory decision—it is a critical determinant of net IRR.

The momentum is undeniable. In 2025, AIF registrations continued to rise, growing by 20% with over 140 new fund registrations in GIFT City alone, driven primarily by foreign capital seeking regulatory arbitrage. However, at TresoWealth, we view surges in popular structures with inherent skepticism. The allure of GIFT City’s tax exemptions often obscures the operational friction, setup costs, and benchmarking complexities that can erode returns if not meticulously managed.

This analysis deconstructs the Mainland versus GIFT City AIF framework, providing foreign investors with a precise, unvarnished comparison to protect capital and optimize post-tax yields.

The Regulatory Architecture: SEBI vs. IFSCA

The foundational difference between the two structures lies in their regulatory jurisdiction, which dictates everything from fund setup timelines to compliance overhead.

**Mainland AIFs** are governed by the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) under the AIF Regulations, 2012. Foreign capital enters via the Foreign Portfolio Investment (FPI) route or the Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) route, subject to Reserve Bank of India (RBI) Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) guidelines. This structure involves multi-regulatory oversight: SEBI for fund registration, RBI for foreign exchange compliance, and the Income Tax Department for taxation.

**GIFT City AIFs** operate within the International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) under the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) regulations. IFSCA acts as a unified regulator, meaning a single window clearance replaces the SEBI-RBI dichotomy. Furthermore, GIFT City is treated as a foreign jurisdiction for FEMA purposes, allowing offshore investors to deploy capital without triggering the stringent Indian foreign exchange controls that plague Mainland structures.

**The Skeptical Takeaway:** While IFSCA’s single-window clearance theoretically reduces time-to-market, the practical reality of setting up a GIFT City fund requires navigating a nascent regulatory ecosystem. Compliance interpretability still lags behind SEBI’s well-established, albeit cumbersome, frameworks.

Taxation and Repatriation: The Core Arbitrage

The primary driver for the 20% year-over-year surge in GIFT City registrations is the stark tax differential. For foreign HNIs, the difference in post-tax returns between the two structures can exceed 400 basis points.

Mainland Taxation

Under the Mainland structure, foreign investors are subject to the Indian Income Tax Act.

  • **Equity Investments:** Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) are taxed at 12.5% (for gains exceeding ₹1.25 lakh), and Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) at 20%.
  • **Unlisted/Debt Investments:** Tax rates escalate significantly, often reaching up to 25% on LTCG plus applicable surcharges and cess.
  • **Withholding Tax:** Dividends are subject to a 10% withholding tax (plus surcharge and cess) under the India-Mauritius or India-Singapore DTAA, assuming TRC (Tax Residency Certificate) compliance.
  • **Repatriation:** Subject to RBI approval and potential lock-in periods depending on the sectoral FDI caps.

GIFT City Taxation

GIFT City operates under the Special Economic Zone (SEZ) Act, offering a 100% tax holiday on specified incomes for 10 consecutive years out of the first 15 years.

  • **Capital Gains & Dividends:** Zero tax on capital gains and dividend income generated by the fund within the IFSC.
  • **Distribution:** No distribution tax on income passed to foreign investors.
  • **Repatriation:** Completely frictionless. Capital and returns can be repatriated in freely convertible foreign currency (USD, EUR, etc.) without RBI prior approval, as the fund is denominated in foreign currency.

**The Skeptical Takeaway:** The 10-year tax holiday is a sunset clause. For long-duration Category II or III AIFs (typically 7-10 year lives with extensions), investors must model the risk of the fund exiting investments after the tax holiday expires, which would subject the tail-end returns to standard Indian tax rates.

Operational Friction and Setup Economics

GIFT City’s tax arbitrage is not free. The operational economics of an IFSC fund demand a higher Minimum Commitment and absorb greater fixed costs.

| Parameter | Mainland AIF | GIFT City AIF |

| :--- | :--- | :--- |

| **Regulator** | SEBI / RBI | IFSCA |

| **Fund Currency** | INR | USD / Foreign Currency |

| **Minimum Commitment** | ₹1 Crore (approx. $120,000) | $100,000 (approx. ₹83 Lakhs) |

| **Setup Timeline** | 60–90 days | 45–75 days |

| **Annual Setup/Compliance Cost** | $40,000 – $60,000 | $70,000 – $100,000 |

| **Withholding Tax on Returns** | 10% - 15% (DTAA dependent) | 0% |

The higher compliance and setup costs in GIFT City are driven by mandatory physical presence requirements (fund managers must operate out of GIFT City), higher IFSCA licensing fees, and the cost of maintaining foreign currency accounts.

**Actionable Insight:** For commitments below $500,000, the fixed cost drag of a GIFT City AIF can negate the tax arbitrage. TresoWealth recommends the GIFT City route exclusively for allocations exceeding $1 million, where the 400+ bps tax saving materially outweighs the incremental $30,000-$40,000 annual operational overhead.

Benchmarking the Unconventional: How to Measure GIFT City Performance

One of the most overlooked risks in GIFT City investments is benchmarking. Evaluating a USD-denominated, tax-exempt GIFT City fund against a standard INR-denominated, fully taxed Mainland benchmark is a mathematical fallacy that inflates perceived alpha.

What Benchmarks Should NRIs Use for GIFT City Investments?

When analyzing GIFT City AIFs, foreign HNIs must strip out currency risk and tax drag to compare like-for-like performance.

1. **For GIFT City Equity Funds:** The benchmark must be a USD Total Return index to account for the INR-USD depreciation curve (historically 2-3% annually). The correct proxy is the **MSCI India USD Net Total Return Index**.

  • *Why not Nifty 50?* The Nifty 50 TRI is an INR index. A GIFT City fund manager delivering 15% INR returns in a year where the Rupee depreciated 3% against the Dollar has only delivered a 12% USD return. Furthermore, the MSCI India USD Net Total Return Index accounts for withholding taxes that a Mainland FPI would pay, allowing the investor to isolate the GIFT City tax alpha accurately.

2. **For Private Credit / Debt AIFs:** Benchmark against the **MCLR-linked USD SOFR + Spread**. Since GIFT City funds deploy capital onshore, the benchmark must reflect the yield on Indian corporate paper (typically 9-12% INR), adjusted for the USD-INR forward premium.

**The Skeptical Takeaway:** If a GIFT City fund manager presents performance against the Nifty 50 TRI without adjusting for currency depreciation and the tax-exempt status of the IFSC, they are engineering a benchmark mismatch. Demand USD-adjusted, net-of-fee IRR modeling against the MSCI India USD Net TRI.

Case Studies: When Structure Dictates Returns

Case Study 1: The Long/Short Equity Allocation (GIFT City Advantage)

A Singapore-based HNI allocates $2 million to an Indian equity long-short strategy.

  • **Mainland Route:** The fund generates a 14% INR IRR. After STCG/LTCG taxes (approx. 15% blended) and a 3% INR depreciation against the USD, the net USD IRR collapses to roughly 8.9%.
  • **GIFT City Route:** The identical strategy generates a 14% INR IRR. Zero tax on capital gains. After the 3% currency depreciation, the net USD IRR is 10.78%.
  • **Verdict:** The GIFT City structure yields a 188 bps premium. Over a 5-year compounding period, this equates to an incremental $230,000+ in terminal value on a $2M allocation.

Case Study 2: The Onshore Real Estate Credit Play (Mainland Advantage)

A Dubai-based family office seeks 12% fixed returns by investing in Indian residential real estate construction finance.

  • **Mainland Route:** The fund deploys capital directly into Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs) via non-convertible debentures (NCDs). The structure is straightforward, with established legal precedents for enforcement.
  • **GIFT City Route:** To deploy foreign capital into onshore real estate, the GIFT City AIF must route funds through a Mainland FPI or an Alternative Investment Vehicle (AIV), adding a layer of structural complexity. Furthermore, enforcement of security interests on Indian real estate by a foreign jurisdiction entity introduces legal friction. The incremental setup cost of the AIV erodes the 1.5% tax saving on the fixed 12% yield.
  • **Verdict:** For lower-yield, asset-heavy strategies with complex enforcement mechanics, the Mainland AIF’s legal clarity outweighs GIFT City’s tax benefits.

Actionable Insights for Foreign HNIs

1. **Match Duration to the Tax Holiday:** Do not invest in a GIFT City AIF with a fund life extending beyond 15 years unless the manager contractually commits to liquidating the core portfolio within the 10-year SEZ tax holiday window. Tail-end extensions can trigger full Indian tax liabilities on deferred exits.

2. **Demand USD Waterfalls:** Ensure the Limited Partnership Agreement (LPA) calculates the carried interest waterfall in USD. An INR-denominated waterfall exposes the HNI to currency risk on the manager's performance fee, effectively forcing you to pay a 20% carry on depreciated Rupee returns.

3. **Audit the Physical Presence:** IFSCA mandates that fund managers must have a physical presence and conduct "core activities" from GIFT City. Request proof of the manager's lease and compliance staffing. A manager running the fund via a token presence risks regulatory censure, which could freeze the fund and trap capital.

4. **Scrutinize the Benchmarking:** Reject prospectuses that benchmark USD-denominated GIFT City funds against INR indices. The only acceptable benchmark for GIFT City equity funds is the MSCI India USD Net Total Return Index.

Conclusion

The choice between a Mainland and a GIFT City AIF is not a simple referendum on taxes; it is a calculation of net IRR factoring in operational drag, currency depreciation, and legal enforceability. While GIFT City provides an unparalleled, frictionless repatriation and tax-exempt environment that is highly advantageous for liquid and high-alpha strategies, its structural costs and regulatory nascency make it suboptimal for complex, asset-heavy, or lower-yield credit plays.

At TresoWealth, we advise foreign HNIs to look past the marketing of "zero tax" and rigorously interrogate the structural economics. Protect your capital by ensuring the fund's structure is tailored to the asset class, the duration, and the USD-denominated reality of your wealth.
