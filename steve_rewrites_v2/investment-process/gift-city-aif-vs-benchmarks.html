GIFT CITY AIFs vs Benchmarks

GIFT City AIFs vs. Benchmarks: Dissecting the $26 Billion Offshore India Play

For decades, foreign capital seeking Indian exposure has navigated a labyrinth of structural inefficiencies: complex regulatory approvals, restrictive repatriation timelines, and the persistent drag of taxation on intermediaries. The International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) at GIFT City was engineered to be the architectural solution to this friction. Today, it is no longer an experimental sandbox; it is a formidable conduit for offshore capital.

As of December 2024, GIFT City Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) have garnered a staggering $26 billion in commitments, according to industry data decoded by LinkedIn analysts. The trajectory is steep: projections indicate the GIFT City fund management industry is poised to scale from its current base to over $124 billion between 2024 and 2030.

Yet, for the discerning Foreign High Net Worth Individual (HNI), capital deployment is not a mere exercise in riding macroeconomic momentum. The critical imperative is alpha generation relative to a relevant, rigorous benchmark. As the ecosystem matures, the fundamental question shifts from "How do I access India?" to "Is my GIFT City AIF manager actually outperforming, or merely dressing up beta in offshore garb?"

At TresoWealth, we view the proliferation of GIFT City vehicles through a lens of structured skepticism. Below, we dissect the benchmarking conundrum of GIFT City AIFs, scrutinize the regulatory architecture, and isolate the metrics that genuinely matter for foreign capital.

The Scale of the Opportunity: By the Numbers

The velocity of capital aggregation in GIFT City is undeniable. Regulatory data from the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) paints a clear picture of institutional and HNI convergence. As of June 30, 2025, the GIFT City IFSC hosts **162 Fund Management Entities (FMEs)** overseeing **177 Funds**.

This represents a near-exponential year-over-year compression in the time it takes for the ecosystem to reach critical mass. However, aggregate statistics often obscure structural realities. Of the $26 billion in commitments, a significant portion remains in Category I and II AIFs—private equity, venture capital, and infrastructure—where J-curve dynamics and illiquidity premiums make immediate benchmarking a mathematical fallacy. The true test of benchmarking efficacy lies in Category III AIFs (hedge funds, long-short strategies) and private credit, where performance is measured against liquid, comparable indices.

The Regulatory Architecture: A Double-Edged Sword

To benchmark a GIFT City AIF accurately, one must first understand the regulatory paradigm under which it operates. IFSCA regulations were deliberately designed to offer regulatory arbitrage—without compromising systemic integrity—against onshore SEBI-governed AIFs.

Key regulatory differentiators impacting performance and benchmarking include:

1. **Sponsor and Manager Commitment:** Unlike onshore AIFs where sponsor commitment is rigidly tied to fund size, IFSCA provides a more flexible, negotiated framework, allowing offshore managers to scale without capital constraints.

2. **Co-Investment Flexibility:** IFSCA permits co-investment vehicles under the same FME umbrella with far fewer friction points than onshore regulations, allowing HNIs to negotiate lower fee structures (often 1.5/15 instead of 2/20) for direct bps savings.

3. **Tax Neutrality:** The 10-year tax holiday under Section 80-LA for FMEs, combined with the absence of distribution tax and capital gains surcharges for non-residents, means the gross-to-net conversion is vastly superior.

However, tax alpha is not operational alpha. A manager who generates a 12% gross return but retains 10% net due to tax efficiency should not be confused with a manager who generates 18% gross and yields 14% net. Benchmarks must be applied to gross returns before the tax shield is factored in, to isolate the manager’s actual security-selection skill.

The Benchmarking Conundrum: Apples, Oranges, and Offshore Indices

The most pervasive error in evaluating GIFT City AIFs is the reliance on inappropriate, easily beaten benchmarks. As highlighted in the recent *GetBelong* analysis on "Benchmarks to Compare GIFT City Mutual Funds," the offshore nature of the capital fundamentally alters the risk-return matrix.

If a GIFT City AIF deploys capital into Indian equities, the default reflex is to benchmark it against the Nifty 50 or BSE Sensex. This is structurally flawed for three reasons:

1. **Currency Dynamics:** GIFT City funds are predominantly USD-denominated. An INR depreciation of 2% annually against the USD immediately creates a deficit against an INR-denominated Nifty 50 benchmark. A true benchmark for a USD-denominated India fund must be a USD-hedged index or a total return index adjusted for the MSCI INR/USD cross rate.

2. **Liquidity Premiums:** AIFs often employ lock-in periods that mutual funds cannot. Comparing a 5-year locked Category II PE AIF to a daily-valued Nifty 50 index ignores the illiquidity premium the investor is owed.

3. **Benchmark Hacking:** Managers may select a broad, diluted index (e.g., Nifty 500 Total Return) that includes micro-caps their portfolio will never hold, artificially inflating relative outperformance.

For foreign HNIs, the correct baseline benchmarks should be:

  • **Long-Only Equity:** MSCI India Total Return Index (USD).
  • **Long-Short / Hedge:** MSCI India Net Total Return (USD) + a risk-free rate (e.g., SOFR + 200 bps) to justify the illiquidity and leverage.
  • **Private Credit:** MSCI India Credit Index or a custom hurdle based on SOFR + 400-600 bps, reflecting the true cost of offshore leverage.

Case Study: Deconstructing the 22.29% Alpha

The danger of isolated data points is best illustrated by the performance of the **Whitespace Alpha Fund I (Growth Equity / EDGE Fund)**, which reported a remarkable **22.29%** return.

At first glance, a 22.29% USD return vastly outperforms the MSCI India USD index over comparable timeframes. But as a foreign HNI, the immediate question must be: *How was this alpha generated?*

There are three probable drivers of such outsized performance in a GIFT City context:

1. **Genuine Operational Alpha:** Superior bottom-up stock picking in the mid-and-small cap space, where information asymmetry still exists.

2. **Factor Exposure:** The manager may have taken concentrated bets on specific factors (e.g., momentum or small-cap illiquidity) that experienced a temporary tailwind. If the benchmark is the Nifty 50, the outperformance is merely a reflection of beta mismatch, not skill.

3. **Valuation Methodologies:** In private equity or unlisted growth equity (as the "EDGE" nomenclature implies), NAV calculations are often based on the last funding round or discounted cash flow models rather than market marks. A 22.29% return in an illiquid book may be an accounting artifact rather than a realizable, liquid exit value.

For the Whitespace Alpha Fund I, the 22.29% is a data point, not a conclusion. Foreign HNIs must demand attribution analysis: Did the 22.29% come from 5 high-conviction winners, or 50 diversified positions? What was the maximum drawdown during the period? Without risk-adjusted metrics (Sharpe, Sortino, Maximum Drawdown), absolute return figures are marketing collateral, not investment theses.

The $124 Billion Horizon: Protecting Capital in a Crowding Market

As the GIFT City ecosystem scales toward the projected $124 billion mark by 2030, the influx of managers will inevitably dilute the average quality of fund performance. The early adopter advantage—where a handful of marquee managers had monopolistic access to the best offshore deal flow—will erode.

In a market approaching $124 billion, capital will chase fewer quality deals, driving up entry valuations and compressing future returns. For the foreign HNI, this makes protective due diligence paramount.

Actionable Insights for Foreign HNIs Evaluating GIFT City AIFs

1. **Demand Apples-to-Apples Benchmarking:** Refuse any offering document that benchmarks a USD-denominated, locked-in AIF against a liquid, INR-denominated public market index. Insist on MSCI India (USD) or a SOFR-linked hurdle rate as the minimum acceptable baseline.

2. **Separate Tax Alpha from Manager Skill:** GIFT City’s tax arbitrage is a feature of the jurisdiction, not the manager. Calculate the net return you would achieve onshore, subtract the tax savings, and evaluate the manager's gross performance against the benchmark. If the manager is relying solely on the IFSCA tax shield to deliver returns, you are paying 2/20 for regulatory arbitrage that you could access via a simpler passive vehicle.

3. **Scrutinize the "Committed" vs. "Deployed" Capital Ratio:** The $26 billion headline figure represents *commitments*, not dry powder in play. In a rising interest rate environment, delayed deployment (capital sitting in US Treasuries waiting for Indian deals) drags down the blended portfolio return. Demand transparency on the deployment pipeline.

4. **Interrogate Valuation Policies:** For Category I and II AIFs, understand how the FME values unlisted assets. IFSCA provides guidelines, but the application of "fair value" accounting can vary drastically. A 22% return based on third-party market trades is fundamentally different from a 22% return derived from an internal DCF model.

5. **Assess Liquidity Mismatch Risk:** GIFT City allows fund structures with longer lock-ins to match the asset lifecycle. Ensure your personal liquidity needs align with the fund's maturity. Do not pay an illiquidity premium for a strategy that could be replicated in a daily-liquid offshore ETF.

Conclusion

GIFT City has unequivocally resolved the structural access problem for foreign capital targeting India. The migration of $26 billion in commitments, scaling toward a $124 billion future, is a testament to the IFSCA’s robust regulatory framework and the undeniable pull of the Indian growth narrative.

However, access is not alpha. As the 177 funds currently operating in GIFT City compete for allocation, the burden falls on the foreign HNI to differentiate between market beta, tax alpha, and genuine operational outperformance. Returns like the 22.29% delivered by the Whitespace Alpha Fund I demonstrate the potential of the ecosystem, but they also demand rigorous, skeptical deconstruction.

At TresoWealth, we advise our clients to look past the headline commitments and absolute return figures. Benchmark your GIFT City exposures against USD-adjusted, risk-adjusted, and liquidity-adjusted indices. In the fastest-growing offshore financial center in the world, the investor who protects their capital with precise benchmarking will be the one who truly captures the India premium.
