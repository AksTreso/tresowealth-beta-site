India vs China Demographics: Why India Now

For decades, global capital treated the India-China comparison as a purely academic exercise. China was the undisputed engine of global manufacturing and economic growth, while India remained a peripheral promise. Today, that paradigm is fracturing. The demographic divergence between the two Asian giants is no longer a forecast; it is a present-day statistical reality that is fundamentally restructuring global capital flows.

For Foreign High Net Worth Individuals (HNIs) allocating capital across emerging markets, the question is no longer whether India will surpass China demographically—it already has. The operative question is whether India’s demographic dividend can be efficiently monetized through structural, regulatory, and economic frameworks, and whether the current $3.7 trillion GDP represents the floor of a multi-decade runway or a peak interrupted by structural bottlenecks.

At TresoWealth, we approach the "India Now" narrative with precise skepticism. Demographics alone do not guarantee returns; they merely provide the raw material. Capital preservation and compounding require an understanding of how those demographics interact with regulatory frameworks and capital allocation mechanisms. Here is a structured analysis of why the demographic pivot makes India a strategic imperative today, and how foreign capital can navigate it safely.

The Demographic Divergence: A Quantitative Reality

The most critical shift in the global economic landscape is the inversion of the population pyramid between India and China. In 2023, India officially surpassed China as the world's most populous nation, reaching 1.428 billion compared to China’s 1.425 billion (United Nations Population Fund, 2023). However, the sheer size of the population is a secondary metric; the primary driver of economic outperformance is the composition of that population.

**The Median Age Gap**

China’s median age is currently 39.5 years, placing it on the demographic trajectory of an aging developed economy like Japan or Western Europe. By 2050, China’s median age is projected to exceed 50 years. Conversely, India’s median age stands at 28.2 years (World Bank, 2023). This 11.3-year gap represents a multi-decade asymmetry in labor force dynamism, domestic consumption, and dependency ratios.

**Dependency Ratio Dynamics**

The working-age population (15-64 years) as a percentage of the total population dictates a nation's capacity to generate savings, fund infrastructure, and drive consumption.

  • **China:** Having peaked in 2010 at 72% of the total population, China's working-age cohort is shrinking. The old-age dependency ratio is rising precipitously, projected to jump from 20% in 2023 to over 45% by 2050 (UN Population Division).
  • **India:** India’s working-age population peaked recently and is expected to remain near its apex through 2055. India’s old-age dependency ratio is a mere 10%, affording the economy a 30-year window of demographic tailwinds.

The strategic implication is clear: China is transitioning from a labor-surplus economy to a labor-scarce economy, driving up manufacturing wages and compressing corporate margins. India is entering its peak labor-surplus phase, offering a fundamentally different cost structure for global supply chains.

The Economic Baseline: The $3.7 Trillion Gap

To benchmark India against China accurately, one must acknowledge the vast economic distance that still separates them. China remains the world’s second-largest economy, with a nominal GDP of approximately $17.7 trillion. India, ranking 5th, stands at $3.7 trillion (IMF, 2023).

Historically, a decade ago, China’s GDP was roughly $10 trillion compared to India’s $2 trillion. The absolute gap has actually widened. Why, then, is the consensus shifting toward "India Now"?

The answer lies in the marginal rate of change and the compounding effect of demographics on GDP growth. China’s GDP growth has decelerated from the double-digit era to an estimated 4.5-5% in the medium term, burdened by a real estate crisis, local government debt, and demographic stagnation. India, conversely, is sustaining a 6.5-7.5% real GDP growth rate.

At a 7% compound annual growth rate, India’s GDP doubles every 10.2 years. The demographic dividend ensures that the terminal velocity of India's growth remains higher than China's for the foreseeable future. For foreign HNIs, investing in India today is not about matching China's current scale; it is about capturing the compounding trajectory from a $3.7 trillion base to a projected $10 trillion economy over the next 12 to 15 years.

A Skeptical Lens: Demographics Are Not Destiny

While the demographic data is unambiguous, TresoWealth maintains a strictly skeptical approach to the assumption that population automatically translates into economic dominance. A large, young population without adequate job creation is not a dividend; it is a demographic disaster.

To benchmark India with China is useful, but only if it highlights the divergent structural challenges. China’s demographic boom was monetized through authoritarian efficiency, massive state-directed infrastructure spending, and aggressive export subsidization. India, as a democratic federal republic, requires a different, more fragmented execution model.

**The Employment Imperative**

India must generate approximately 10 to 12 million new jobs annually to absorb its entering workforce. The Periodic Labour Force Survey (PLFS 2022-23) indicates an improving, yet still precarious, labor force participation rate of approximately 42%. Furthermore, the female labor force participation rate, while rising to roughly 29%, remains critically low compared to China’s 61%.

Capital must be directed toward sectors that formalize the economy and absorb labor. If India cannot upscale its manufacturing and formal services sectors, the demographic dividend will plateau, and domestic consumption will remain constrained. Foreign investors must rigorously evaluate sectoral exposure, prioritizing industries backed by government Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes that are explicitly designed to bridge this employment gap.

Regulatory Framework: Navigating the Foreign Capital Entry Point

Understanding the demographic thesis is insufficient without a precise understanding of the regulatory architecture governing foreign capital. India’s regulatory framework has evolved from being notoriously restrictive to selectively open, prioritizing strategic stability over volatile speculative inflows.

**The FDI and FPI Architecture**

Foreign capital primarily enters India through two conduits governed by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) and the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI):

1. **Foreign Direct Investment (FDI):** Governed by the Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) of 1999, FDI allows for strategic control. The current regulatory framework permits up to 100% FDI under the automatic route in most sectors, including manufacturing, renewable energy, and financial services. However, sectors like multi-brand retail, insurance (capped at 74%), and defense (capped at 74% with government approval) remain restricted to protect strategic national interests.

2. **Foreign Portfolio Investment (FPI):** Regulated by SEBI, FPI is the preferred route for HNIs seeking market exposure without management control. Recent SEBI amendments have tightened the definition of "beneficial ownership" to prevent round-tripping and enhance transparency, aligning India with FATF (Financial Action Task Force) standards.

**Protective Compliance: The GIFT City Advantage**

For foreign HNIs, regulatory friction and tax inefficiencies are primary threats to compounding. The International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) at GIFT City offers a structurally protective solution. Operating as a special economic zone, GIFT City provides a 10-year tax holiday on capital gains, exemption from GST, and simplified compliance via a unified regulator (the IFSCA). Routing investments through GIFT City allows HNIs to access Indian equities and Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) while mitigating the regulatory friction inherent in cross-border capital flows.

Case Studies: Monetizing the Demographic Shift

To move from macro-theory to actionable strategy, we examine two distinct case studies where the demographic divergence is already creating asymmetric returns for informed capital.

Case Study 1: The Supply Chain Pivot (Manufacturing)

**Apple and the Foxconn Diversification**

For a decade, Apple relied on China for over 95% of its manufacturing. The demographic stagnation and geopolitical risk of the "China Plus One" era forced a recalibration. Foxconn’s manufacturing facility in Tamil Nadu, India, now produces the iPhone 14, 15, and 16 models.

Between 2021 and 2024, Apple’s share of India-manufactured iPhones rose from near zero to approximately 14%. This shift was not merely corporate strategy; it was an execution of the demographic reality. India offers a labor force whose wages are a fraction of China’s coastal manufacturing hubs, supported by state-level PLI subsidies. For HNIs, the actionable insight is to allocate capital not to the end manufacturer, but to the domestic supply chain—auto-ancillaries, specialty chemicals, and electronic manufacturing services (EMS)—that are structurally positioned to absorb global supply chain realignments.

Case Study 2: The Consumption Multiplier (Digital Fintech)

**UPI and the Digitization of the Mass Market**

China’s consumption boom was historically driven by an expanding middle class utilizing platforms like Alipay and WeChat. India is replicating this, but at a different scale and through a public infrastructure model. The Unified Payments Interface (UPI) processed over 10 billion transactions in a single month in 2023, valued at approximately $180 billion.

This is the demographic dividend digitized. A 28-year-old median age implies a massive cohort of first-time earners who are instantly onboarded into the formal financial system via smartphones. Unlike China, where private tech oligopolies captured the financial upside, India’s digital public infrastructure (DPI) lowers the customer acquisition cost for private players while keeping the rails public. HNIs should look toward Indian fintech, asset management, and consumer durable sectors that are leveraging this DPI to monetize the bottom of the pyramid with unprecedented margins.

Actionable Insights for Foreign HNIs

Allocating capital to India based on the demographic thesis requires a structured, protective approach. Generic broad-market exposure is suboptimal. We recommend the following strategic postures:

1. **Allocate to Labor-Intensive Manufacturing over Capital-Intensive:** Capital-intensive sectors in India often face bureaucratic delays and land acquisition hurdles. Labor-intensive sectors (textiles, EMS, pharmaceuticals) directly leverage the demographic dividend and are heavily supported by PLI incentives, offering a higher probability of near-term execution.

2. **Utilize the IFSC Route for Tax Alpha:** Do not subject your Indian portfolio to double taxation and complex bilateral treaty interpretations. Establish a FPI or AIF presence in GIFT City to benefit from the 0% capital gains tax regime and seamless repatriation of funds under FEMA regulations.

3. **Hedge Against the "Jobless Growth" Risk:** Monitor the PLFS data rigorously. If India’s labor force participation rate stagnates, pivot allocations away from discretionary consumption and toward export-oriented sectors that earn in USD, thereby hedging the domestic consumption risk.

4. **Avoid the China Playbook of Infrastructure Overbuild:** China’s GDP was artificially inflated by trillions in unproductive infrastructure debt. India’s infrastructure build-out is largely government-funded and strategically targeted (railways, highways, power transmission). Avoid overexposure to real estate developers; focus instead on operational infrastructure assets with regulated yields.

Conclusion

The India versus China comparison is no longer a theoretical debate about two similarly positioned economies. It is the story of a structural divergence. China’s $17.7 trillion economy is grappling with an irreversible demographic decline, a shrinking workforce, and mounting debt. India’s $3.7 trillion economy is at the inception of its peak demographic window, armed with digitized infrastructure and a reform-oriented regulatory framework.

However, at TresoWealth, we recognize that demographics are a necessary, but not sufficient, condition for wealth creation. The window of opportunity is narrow. India must execute on job creation, and foreign capital must navigate the regulatory landscape with precision and skepticism. By utilizing protective structures like GIFT City and focusing on sectors that directly monetize the working-age population, foreign HNIs can position themselves to capture one of the most compelling macro-economic shifts of the 21st century. The time for viewing India as a future prospect has passed; the structural data dictates that the time to act is now.
