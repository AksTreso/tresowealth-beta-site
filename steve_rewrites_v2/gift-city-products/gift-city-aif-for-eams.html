GIFT City AIFs for EAMs: The Ideal Route

GIFT City AIFs for EAMs: The Ideal Route for Foreign Capital into India?

For External Asset Managers (EAMs) advising foreign High Net Worth Individuals (HNIs), allocating capital to India has historically been an exercise in navigating friction. The structural appeal of India’s growth is undeniable, but the mechanics of entry, repatriation, and taxation have often diluted net returns. Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) was designed to eliminate this friction. Today, the International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) at GIFT City presents itself as the optimal jurisdiction for foreign capital. But is it truly the ideal route, or does the structural convenience mask underlying execution risks?

At TresoWealth, we approach jurisdictional arbitrage with strict skepticism. An efficient tax structure is only valuable if the underlying investment architecture, regulatory durability, and benchmarking methodologies hold up to rigorous scrutiny. This analysis dissects the GIFT City Alternative Investment Fund (AIF) ecosystem to determine where it genuinely serves foreign HNIs, and where EAMs must exercise extreme caution.

The Scale of the GIFT City AIF Ecosystem

The proliferation of fund managers setting up operations in GIFT City is no longer theoretical; it is an established trend backed by hard capital commitments. However, EAMs must differentiate between committed capital and deployed capital, a distinction that is often blurred in industry reporting.

As of June 30, 2025, the GIFT City IFSC hosts **162 Fund Management Entities (FMEs)** operating **177 Funds**. Broader industry data indicates that these 177 fund managers oversee over **270 funds** with a cumulative AUM that is rapidly scaling. The most widely cited headline figure is that GIFT City AIFs have garnered **$26 billion in commitments** (Source: LinkedIn/Industry Reports).

While $26 billion is a significant milestone, the protective stance required for foreign HNI capital demands a closer look. Commitments represent pledged capital, not capital subject to market risk. The velocity of capital drawdowns and the actual net asset value (NAV) deployed into onshore Indian equities or debt remain the true barometers of the ecosystem's maturity. EAMs must interrogate fund documentation to understand the drawdown schedules, as undrawn commitments do not generate alpha.

Performance Realities: Parsing Alpha from Noise

The ultimate justification for routing capital through an offshore AIF rather than accessing onshore mutual funds directly is the generation of idiosyncratic alpha. In a high-growth market like India, broad beta often disguises mediocre stock picking. GIFT City AIFs—particularly Category-III funds, which employ diverse strategies including long-short, absolute returns, and leveraged plays—are beginning to post track records that warrant attention.

Recent performance data reveals that certain managers are successfully extracting alpha:

  • **Whitespace Alpha Fund I (EDGE):** 22.29%
  • **Alta Alpha Fund I - Equity Plus:** 19.65%

These figures are compelling, but they demand contextualization. A 22.29% return in a bull market may simply be leveraged beta. EAMs must evaluate the Sharpe and Sortino ratios of these Category-III AIFs to determine if the volatility endured by the HNI was adequately compensated. Furthermore, the longevity of these track records is limited, given the nascent nature of the IFSC. A two-year track record in a unilateral bull market is not a sufficient statistical basis for a seven-figure allocation; it is a hypothesis that requires ongoing stress-testing.

The Benchmarking Conundrum

One of the most structurally flawed aspects of the GIFT City ecosystem is benchmarking. As highlighted by analytical platforms like GetBelong, establishing accurate benchmarks to compare GIFT City Mutual Funds—and by extension, AIFs—remains a complex challenge.

Why does benchmarking fail here? Traditional onshore Indian benchmarks (e.g., Nifty 50, BSE 200) do not account for the offshore currency hedging costs, withholding tax differentials, or the specific mandate of a Category-III AIF utilizing leverage. When an EAM reviews a GIFT City AIF pitching alpha over a standard onshore index, the comparison is fundamentally asymmetrical.

To protect HNI capital, EAMs must insist on custom benchmark constructions that factor in:

1. **The cost of USD-INR hedging:** Typically 4-6% annually, which can obliterate the "alpha" generated by a conservative long-only strategy masquerading as an AIF.

2. **Opportunity cost:** The risk-free rate available in the HNI’s home jurisdiction.

3. **Net-of-fee returns:** Management and performance fees in GIFT City AIFs often mirror onshore rates (typically 2% and 20%), which must be subtracted before any comparison to passive indices.

Without robust, apples-to-apples benchmarking, the reported outperformance of GIFT City funds is an illusion.

The Regulatory Architecture: Section 197A and Tax Neutrality

The primary structural advantage of GIFT City is its tax neutrality, which is critical for foreign HNIs who are otherwise subject to punitive withholding taxes on onshore Indian investments.

Under the standard Indian Income Tax Act, capital gains and interest income earned by non-residents are subject to withholding tax, which can range from 10% to 20% depending on the instrument and the applicable Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA). This creates severe tax leakage and administrative friction.

GIFT City circumvents this through specific dispensations. Most notably, **Section 197A** of the Income Tax Act provides that the central government may notify classes of income or cases where withholding tax shall not be deducted. As noted by leading tax counsel such as Desai Associates, the notification issued under Section 197A for IFSC units effectively exempts specified payments made to non-resident investors from withholding tax, provided the conditions stipulated for IFSC operations are met.

Furthermore, the IFSC framework offers a 100% tax holiday on specified income for the first five years and a 50% holiday for the subsequent five years under Section 80LA. For EAMs, this means:

  • **Zero withholding tax on distributions** from the AIF to the foreign HNI (under Section 197A notification).
  • **Tax-efficient compounding** within the fund structure before distribution.

However, a skeptical approach is mandatory. The Section 197A exemption is contingent upon strict compliance with IFSC regulations. Any structural deviation—such as the AIF investing in non-permissible assets or failing the "substance" requirements of the IFSC—can retroactively disallow the exemption, exposing the HNI to sudden tax liability. EAMs must conduct independent legal audits of the AIF's compliance architecture before allocating capital.

Category-III AIFs: The Double-Edged Sword

The GIFT City landscape is increasingly dominated by Category-III AIFs. These funds employ complex strategies, including short-selling, derivatives, and leverage. For foreign HNIs seeking portfolio diversification beyond long-only Indian equities, Category-III AIFs offer the only viable mechanism to access absolute-return strategies in India.

However, Category-III AIFs carry an inherently elevated risk profile:

  • **Leverage Risk:** Leverage amplifies losses. In a market correction, a leveraged Category-III fund can suffer drawdowns that significantly exceed the underlying index.
  • **Liquidity Mismatch:** While GIFT City facilitates easier repatriation, the underlying Indian securities (especially mid-and-small-cap stocks favored by alpha-seeking managers) may suffer from severe liquidity droughts during market panics. An AIF may gate redemptions not because of GIFT City regulations, but because the underlying onshore assets cannot be liquidated without market impact.
  • **Counterparty Risk:** The use of derivatives introduces counterparty risks that do not exist in cash-market Category-I or II AIFs.

EAMs must not conflate the regulatory safety of the GIFT City jurisdiction with the investment safety of the Category-III strategy. The IFSC protects against tax and repatriation friction; it does not protect against poor risk management by the FME.

Actionable Insights for EAMs

Routing foreign HNI capital through GIFT City AIFs is structurally advantageous, but only when executed with rigorous due diligence. To align with TresoWealth’s protective mandate, EAMs should adhere to the following framework:

1. Deconstruct the $26 Billion Commitment Figure

Do not allocate based on FOMO. Demand transparency on the *deployed AUM* versus *committed capital*. A fund with $500 million in commitments but only $50 million deployed is still an operational startup, regardless of its headline size.

2. Mandate Net-of-Hedging Benchmarking

Reject any AIF that benchmarks its performance against a standard onshore Nifty or BSE index without deducting the cost of USD-INR hedging. Require managers to present performance against a custom benchmark that reflects the true cost of offshore capital. If the alpha does not exceed the hedging cost and management fees, the HNI is better served by a passive onshore ETF accessed via a reputable custodian.

3. Audit the Section 197A Compliance

Before subscribing, require a legal opinion from the AIF’s counsel (or an independent firm like Desai Associates) confirming that the fund’s current operations strictly adhere to the IFSC conditions necessary to maintain the Section 197A withholding tax exemption. A minor regulatory breach can result in catastrophic tax leakage for the HNI.

4. Stress-Test Category-III Liquidity

For Category-III AIFs, review the offering documents for redemption gates, lock-in periods, and side-pocket provisions. Demand data on the average daily volume of the AIF’s top 10 holdings. If the fund cannot liquidate its top positions within 3-5 days without moving the market, the HNI’s capital is structurally illiquid.

5. Evaluate Manager Longevity

The 177 FMEs currently operating in GIFT City range from established global players to first-time managers. Prioritize managers who have navigated at least one full Indian market cycle (including a bear market) in their personal track records, even if the specific GIFT City vehicle is new. The 22.29% return of the Whitespace Alpha Fund I or the 19.65% of the Alta Alpha Fund I must be contextualized against downside protection during market reversals.

Conclusion

GIFT City has fundamentally altered the architecture of foreign investment into India. The combination of Section 197A tax exemptions, streamlined repatriation, and the proliferation of Category-III AIFs provides a compelling, and arguably unmatched, route for foreign HNIs.

However, the "ideal route" is not a frictionless one. The ecosystem is still maturing. Benchmarking remains inadequate, the true deployment of the $26 billion in commitments remains opaque, and the risks embedded in leveraged Category-III strategies are magnified by the liquidity constraints of the underlying onshore markets.

For EAMs, GIFT City is not a shortcut; it is a specialized tool. When used with precision, structural skepticism, and an unwavering focus on net-of-cost alpha, it can securely unlock India’s growth for foreign HNIs. When used as a blind leap of faith based on headline tax benefits, it is a liability waiting to materialize. At TresoWealth, we advise our partners to respect the jurisdiction, but to trust only the data.
