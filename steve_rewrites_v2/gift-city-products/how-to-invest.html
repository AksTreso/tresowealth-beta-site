How to Invest in GIFT CITY AIFs: Step-by-Step Guide

How to Invest in GIFT City AIFs: A Step-by-Step Guide for Foreign HNIs

For decades, foreign High Net Worth Individuals (HNIs) looking to capture India’s growth trajectory have navigated a labyrinthine regulatory framework. Between the Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) guidelines, the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) registration requirements, and the Reserve Bank of India’s (RBI) repatriation locks, the operational friction often eroded the alpha generated by the underlying assets.

Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) was engineered to resolve this friction. Operating as India’s first International Financial Services Centre (IFSC), it provides a offshore jurisdiction with onshore access to the Indian economy. However, at TresoWealth, our approach remains fundamentally skeptical of regulatory panaceas. Tax exemptions and streamlined mechanics are advantageous, but they do not immunize capital against poor fund construction, liquidity mismatches, or macroeconomic depreciation.

This guide provides a precise, structured, and protective framework for foreign HNIs evaluating Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) within GIFT City.

---

The Regulatory Pivot: Assessing the $75,000 Threshold

Historically, the barrier to entry for GIFT City AIFs was disproportionately high, locking out all but institutional capital. However, a critical regulatory shift has reshaped the landscape.

In February 2025, the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) reduced the minimum investment threshold for AIFs from $150,000 to $75,000. This 50% reduction was not merely an administrative adjustment; it was a calculated move to democratize access to the IFSC framework, allowing foreign HNIs to diversify into Indian alternatives without committing institutional-scale checks to a single manager.

**The Skeptical Takeaway:** While the lowered threshold allows for more granular portfolio construction, a $75,000 commitment still represents a significant concentration risk if deployed into a single Category II or III AIF. At TresoWealth, we advise that the reduced minimum should be utilized to access multiple uncorrelated strategies within GIFT City, rather than serving as a discount to bypass rigorous due diligence.

---

Structural Advantages and Hidden Frictions

Before deploying capital, foreign investors must separate the structural benefits of GIFT City from the operational realities.

The Advantages

1. **Tax Efficiency:** IFSC-based funds enjoy a 100% tax holiday on specified income for the first 10 consecutive years out of the first 15 years of operations, under Section 80LA of the Income Tax Act.

2. **Withholding Tax Exemptions:** Interest and dividend payments from GIFT City entities to non-residents often benefit from concessional or zero withholding tax rates.

3. **Repatriation Freedom:** Operating outside the standard FEMA rigidity, GIFT City allows for the free repatriation of capital and returns without the prior approval of the RBI, provided the source funds were originally remitted from outside India.

The Frictions

1. **Currency Risk:** While GIFT City operates in USD, the underlying assets are predominantly INR-denominated. An unhedged position exposes the HNI to INR depreciation against the USD, which has historically averaged a 3-4% annual drift.

2. **Regulatory Sunset Clauses:** The 10-year tax holiday is subject to statutory sunset dates. Investors must scrutinize the fund's lifespan against the expiry of Section 80LA benefits to ensure projected Net IRRs are not artificially inflated by temporary tax shields.

Step-by-Step Deployment Framework

Investing in a GIFT City AIF requires meticulous operational execution. A single misstep in remittance or documentation can trigger adverse tax consequences.

Step 1: Establish an IFSC Banking Unit (IBU) Account

Foreign HNIs cannot remit funds directly from an overseas bank to a GIFT City AIF and expect seamless regulatory cover. You must first open an IBU account.

This requires standard KYC/AML documentation, alongside proof of overseas address and source of wealth. Once established, this account acts as your offshore gateway into the Indian alternative market, holding USD or other permitted currencies.

Step 2: Execute a Systematic Transfer Strategy

Lump-sum deployments into illiquid alternatives expose capital to timing risk. A more protective structure involves systematic deployment.

For instance, an HNI can transfer $2,000 to their GIFT City IBU account every month. Over a 36-month period, this systematically builds a $72,000 capital base, strategically positioning the investor to meet the $75,000 minimum commitment threshold with minimal market-timing risk. Once the capital base approaches the threshold, the final $3,000 can be deployed to trigger the AIF subscription, allowing the investor to dollar-cost average into the INR exposure prior to the fund's capital calls.

Step 3: Manager Selection and Benchmarking

This is where capital is won or lost. The GIFT City label does not guarantee alpha. You must evaluate the fund manager against rigorous PE/VC industry benchmarks like the **Cambridge Associates indices**.

When reviewing a GIFT City AIF's pitch deck, demand the following:

  • **Net IRR vs. Cambridge Associates Global EM PE Index:** If the manager is projecting a 14% Net IRR, but the Cambridge Associates index for Indian PE returns 15.2%, the manager is not generating alpha; they are merely capturing beta and charging premium fees for a tax wrapper.
  • **DPI (Distributions to Paid-In):** Given the illiquidity of Indian alternatives, Net IRR is often a paper metric. Insist on seeing the track record of realized returns (DPI) of the management team, not just mark-to-market valuations (TVPI).

Step 4: Subscription and Compliance Execution

Once the manager passes due diligence, you will execute the Subscription Agreement, Contribution Agreement, and comply with FATCA/CRS declarations. Capital is typically called in tranches. Ensure your IBU account is sufficiently liquid to meet capital calls within the stipulated 10-15 day windows, as defaulting on a capital call can result in severe penalties and forfeiture of prior capital.

Step 5: Repatriation and Exit Monitoring

Upon the fund’s liquidity event (IPO, strategic sale, or secondary exit), the proceeds are credited back to your IBU account in USD. Verify that the fund administrator has correctly computed the Net IRR post the applicable tax shields, and execute the repatriation to your primary overseas banking institution.

Case Studies in GIFT City Allocation

To illustrate the practical application of this framework, consider two distinct profiles of foreign HNIs navigating the GIFT City ecosystem.

Case Study 1: The Strategic Yield Seeker

**Profile:** A Singapore-based HNI with a $5M liquid portfolio, heavily concentrated in US equities and seeking uncorrelated yield.

**Action:** The investor utilized the reduced $75,000 minimum to deploy into a GIFT City Category III AIF focused on Indian structured credit and arbitrage strategies.

**Outcome:** By utilizing the IBU account, the investor transferred $2,000 monthly over 30 months to accumulate the base capital, mitigating INR/USD volatility during the accumulation phase. The fund generated a 12% annualized yield. Because the AIF was domiciled in GIFT City, the interest income fell under the Section 80LA tax holiday, resulting in a zero withholding tax on the yield—a significant upgrade over the standard 20% withholding tax on Indian debt instruments for foreign entities. The investor repatriated the yield annually without RBI friction.

Case Study 2: The Venture Capital Allocation

**Profile:** A UAE-based family office looking for aggressive growth through Indian technology startups, but constrained by the operational complexity of directly applying for FDI approval in unlisted Indian companies.

**Action:** Deployed $500,000 into a GIFT City Category II AIF (Venture Capital fund).

**Skepticism Applied:** During due diligence, the family office noted the manager was projecting a 22% Net IRR. Benchmarking this against the **Cambridge Associates Venture Capital Index** for Emerging Markets, which showed a 5-year pooled net IRR of 18.4%, the family office identified that the premium was largely attributable to the GIFT City tax shield, not superior deal flow. They renegotiated the management fee from 2% to 1.5% on committed capital, arguing that the regulatory arbitrage was subsidizing the manager’s risk profile. The fund accepted the revised terms, protecting the family office’s downside.

The TresoWealth Protective Checklist

Before committing a single dollar to a GIFT City AIF, run your prospective investment through this structural filter:

1. **Is the tax shield the only alpha?** If removing the Section 80LA exemption causes the fund's projected returns to fall below the Cambridge Associates indices, the strategy is fundamentally flawed. Walk away.

2. **Have you stress-tested the INR?** Model an INR depreciation of 4% annually against the USD. If the fund’s projected 15% INR IRR drops to an 11% USD IRR, does the risk-reward ratio still justify the illiquidity?

3. **What is the exit timeline reality?** Indian legal systems can delay exits by 12-18 months beyond fund maturity. Ensure your liquidity planning accounts for a 10-year lock-in, even if the fund promises a 7-year exit.

4. **Is the $2,000 monthly transfer optimized?** Do not let cash sit idle in the IBU account. Utilize systematic transfers to time your FX conversion, ensuring you are not accumulating USD in an account yielding 0% while waiting to meet the $75,000 threshold.

Conclusion

GIFT City represents a structural evolution in how foreign capital accesses India’s alternative investment landscape. The reduction of the minimum investment threshold to $75,000 and the ability to systematically route capital via $2,000 monthly transfers have removed the historical barriers to entry.

However, operational ease must never be confused with investment merit. The tax efficiencies of the IFSC framework are a powerful tailwind, but they cannot compensate for poor manager selection, inadequate benchmarking, or unhedged currency exposure. At TresoWealth, we maintain that capital preservation in foreign alternatives requires a skeptical, hyper-structured approach. Demand that your GIFT City allocations outperform global benchmarks on their own operational merit, not merely on the crutch of a regulatory tax holiday.
