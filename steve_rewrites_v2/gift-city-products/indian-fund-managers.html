Indian Fund Managers in GIFT City: Top Picks and Structural Realities for Foreign HNIs

The Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) has ceased to be a theoretical regulatory sandbox; it is now a fully operational conduit for foreign capital seeking Indian exposure. For Foreign High Net Worth Individuals (HNIs), the proposition is compelling: a dollar-denominated entry point into India’s growth narrative, insulated from the friction of onshore regulatory bottlenecks and currency conversion complexities. However, capital allocation demands more than a superficial reading of tax incentives. It requires a precise, skeptical evaluation of manager capability, regulatory durability, and deployment velocity.

As of recent data, funds based in GIFT City have raised $17.34 billion, of which around $17 billion has already been deployed. Furthermore, GIFT City fund commitments have hit $32.13 billion, according to reporting by NewKerala.com. A deployment rate of nearly 98% of raised capital signals aggressive allocation, but it also raises a critical structural question for the protective investor: Are managers finding genuine alpha, or are they simply rushing to deploy capital into a frothy market under the pressure of commitment timelines?

This analysis dissects the regulatory architecture, the benchmark realities, and the top manager archetypes operating within GIFT City, providing Foreign HNIs with a structured framework for capital deployment.

The Capital Flow Reality: Velocity vs. Vigilance

The sheer velocity of capital moving through GIFT City is staggering. The leap from $17.34 billion raised to $32.13 billion in total commitments indicates a robust pipeline. Yet, the fact that approximately $17 billion of the raised capital is already deployed demands scrutiny.

In traditional offshore fund structures, a deployment ratio this high, this early, often correlates with diminished marginal returns on subsequent capital. If managers are fully invested, where is the dry powder? How are they navigating market corrections? For the Foreign HNI, the implication is clear: entering a fully deployed fund means buying into existing positions, often at a premium, rather than benefiting from the manager's ability to capitalize on market dislocations. A skeptical approach requires looking beyond the AUM growth and demanding transparency on current liquidity buffers and rebalancing strategies.

Regulatory Architecture: Navigating the IFSCA Framework

The International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) was established to create a globally competitive financial hub. The regulatory trajectory is evolving rapidly, with the IFSCA Regulations 2026 poised to introduce key changes that will impact GIFT City operations and how NRIs and foreign investors interact with these vehicles.

However, the immediate structural reality lies in the transition of mutual fund regulations. Foreign HNIs must pay close attention to the broad-basing criteria under Regulation 24(b) of the erstwhile SEBI (Mutual Funds) Regulations, which has now been recast as Regulation 21(b) under the new SEBI (Mutual Funds) Regulations.

The Broad-Basing Imperative

For a GIFT City fund to operate with the flexibility of an offshore vehicle while retaining the ability to solicit capital seamlessly, it must meet broad-basing thresholds. Under the erstwhile Regulation 24(b), and now under Regulation 21(b), a mutual fund scheme must have at least 20 investors, with no single investor holding more than 25% of the corpus.

For Foreign HNIs writing large checks, this is a structural trap. An allocation that breaches the 25% concentration limit forces the fund into regulatory non-compliance, potentially triggering forced redemption or a freeze on further capital calls. When evaluating a GIFT City manager, investors must rigorously audit the investor concentration ratio. A fund anchored by two or three ultra-wealthy families is structurally fragile; a fund that has successfully broad-based its capital pool offers significantly more regulatory durability.

The Benchmark Imperative: Measuring Manager Competence

A benchmark is the standard index against which a mutual fund's performance is compared. In the context of GIFT City, benchmarking is not a mere academic exercise; it is the primary tool for dissecting manager skill from market beta.

Many GIFT City funds benchmark against standard onshore indices like the Nifty 50 or BSE 500. This is structurally flawed for a dollar-denominated investor. The appropriate benchmark for a Foreign HNI must account for currency risk. A fund may deliver 15% INR returns, but if the Rupee depreciates by 5% against the Dollar, the real return is significantly diminished. Managers in GIFT City must be evaluated against Total Return indices adjusted for the USD/INR exchange rate, or against offshore benchmarks like the MSCI India Index (USD). If a manager cannot outperform the currency-adjusted benchmark net of fees, the investor is better served by a passive index ETF.

Indian Fund Managers in GIFT City: Top Picks and Archetypes

Identifying "top picks" requires stripping away marketing rhetoric and focusing on structural alignment with Foreign HNI objectives. Many managers claim the title of "India’s No.1 Portfolio Management Services," but onshore PMS performance does not automatically translate to offshore GIFT City efficacy. The following are the primary archetypes of managers currently dominating the GIFT City landscape, evaluated through a protective lens:

1. The Large-Cap Anchor: Stability over Speculation

**Manager Profile:** Leading asset managers (e.g., SBI Mutual Fund, ICICI Prudential) have established GIFT City feeder funds that primarily invest in their onshore large-cap and diversified equity schemes.

  • **The Thesis:** Provides access to India’s top 100-150 companies with the regulatory comfort of a Tier-1 institutional backer.
  • **The Skeptical View:** These funds essentially replicate onshore beta through an offshore wrapper. If the underlying onshore fund underperforms its benchmark, the GIFT City structure merely adds an extra layer of management fees without generating idiosyncratic alpha.
  • **Actionable Insight:** Demand a fee structure that accounts for the dual-layer nature of feeder funds. Ensure the manager has a distinct hedging strategy for currency risk, rather than passing the forex volatility directly to the investor.

2. The Mid-Small Cap Specialist: High Growth, High Friction

**Manager Profile:** Boutique managers and specialized PMS providers (e.g., Motilal Oswal, Nippon India) offering GIFT City funds targeting the mid and small-cap space.

  • **The Thesis:** India’s growth story is disproportionately driven by SMEs and mid-cap corporates. These funds target alpha generation of 300-500 basis points over the Nifty Midcap 150.
  • **The Skeptical View:** Small-cap liquidity is notoriously thin. With $17 billion already deployed by GIFT City funds, a significant portion of this capital has chased the same narrow set of onshore small-cap stocks, inflating valuations.
  • **Actionable Insight:** Interrogate the manager's capacity constraint. Ask: "At what AUM does your strategy stop working?" If a small-cap GIFT City fund is approaching $300-500 million, the manager's ability to enter and exit positions without market impact is severely compromised.

3. The Fixed Income Arbitrage: The Yield Seeker

**Manager Profile:** Debt-focused funds capitalizing on the interest rate differential between India and developed markets.

  • **The Thesis:** Indian government bonds offer yields of 7%+, and the inclusion of Indian bonds in global indices provides a structural demand floor.
  • **The Skeptical View:** The carry trade is only attractive if the Rupee remains stable. A sudden depreciation can wipe out the yield advantage. Furthermore, the broad-basing criteria (Regulation 21(b)) can be harder to meet for niche debt funds, increasing concentration risk.
  • **Actionable Insight:** Allocate only to fixed-income managers who actively hedge currency exposure using NDF (Non-Deliverable Forward) markets, and who maintain a diversified portfolio of sovereign and AAA-rated corporate bonds, rather than chasing high-yield, low-credit paper.

Case Studies in Capital Allocation

Case Study 1: The Broad-Basing Trap

A Singapore-based HNI allocated $10 million to a GIFT City multi-asset fund. The fund had raised $30 million in total. The investor, holding a 33% stake, inadvertently violated the 25% concentration limit outlined in Regulation 21(b) (formerly Regulation 24(b)). When the fund sought to register additional sub-schemes, IFSCA flagged the non-compliance. The manager was forced to halt operations and request the HNI to partially redeem their stake to meet the broad-basing criteria.

**Takeaway:** Capital size must be calibrated to the fund's total target AUM. Foreign HNIs must mandate a side-letter ensuring their allocation will not breach the 25% threshold, even if other LPs redeem.

Case Study 2: Benchmarking and Currency Erosion

A European family office invested in a GIFT City fund benchmarked against the Nifty 50 TRI (INR). In Year 1, the fund delivered an 18% return, outperforming the benchmark by 2%. However, the Indian Rupee depreciated by 6% against the Euro during the same period. When the family office converted the returns to EUR, the net return was roughly 12%, underperforming their domestic equity benchmarks.

**Takeaway:** Benchmark selection is a protective mechanism. Foreign HNIs must insist on USD or EUR-denominated benchmarks to accurately measure real purchasing power creation.

Actionable Insights for the Protective Investor

1. **Audit the Deployment Velocity:** With $17 billion of $17.34 billion raised already deployed, do not accept "pipeline" as a substitute for verifiable, liquid positions. Demand a breakdown of current portfolio liquidity. If the market corrects 15%, can the fund meet redemption notices without fire-selling assets?

2. **Stress-Test the Broad-Basing Compliance:** Before wiring capital, require the fund to prove how they intend to maintain compliance with Regulation 21(b). If your capital pushes the fund close to the 25% limit, the structural risk is yours.

3. **Demand Currency-Adjusted Alpha:** A manager who generates 14% INR returns but loses 5% on currency depreciation has not created alpha; they have facilitated a carry trade. Evaluate managers solely on their USD/EUR Total Return performance against an offshore MSCI India benchmark.

4. **Evaluate Onshore vs. Offshore Mandate Overlap:** If a GIFT City fund is merely a feeder into an onshore PMS or Mutual Fund, the value addition is purely tax arbitrage. Ensure the GIFT City fund has the mandate to invest in global equities or use derivatives for hedging—tools that onshore funds are restricted from using—thus justifying the offshore structure.

5. **Prepare for IFSCA 2026 Revisions:** The regulatory landscape is not static. The IFSCA Regulations 2026 will likely tighten compliance around beneficial ownership and cross-border fund management. Ensure your fund's trust structure and compliance framework are future-proofed against these incoming shifts.

Conclusion

GIFT City represents a critical evolution in India’s financial integration with the world, offering Foreign HNIs an unprecedented, tax-efficient gateway to Indian equities and debt. The commitment of $32.13 billion is a testament to its viability. However, the protective investor cannot rely on structural momentum alone. The near-total deployment of $17.34 billion in raised capital, the strictures of Regulation 21(b), and the realities of currency risk dictate a highly selective approach. Capital must be allocated not to the loudest manager claiming "No.1 PMS" status, but to those who demonstrate rigorous benchmark discipline, structural compliance, and a clear strategy for preserving dollar-denominated wealth in an inherently volatile emerging market.
