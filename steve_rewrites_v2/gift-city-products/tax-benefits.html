GIFT CITY Tax Benefits: 0% Capital Gains Explained

GIFT City Tax Benefits: Decoding the 0% Capital Gains Paradigm and the 9% Alternative for Foreign HNIs

For foreign High Net-Worth Individuals (HNIs) allocating capital to Indian equities and debt, the historical friction has not been market volatility, but tax inefficiency. Traditional Foreign Portfolio Investor (FPI) routes subject capital gains to a complex, often punitive, withholding tax regime ranging from 10% on long-term equity gains to upwards of 30% on debt and unlisted assets.

Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) was engineered to neutralize this friction. Promising 0% capital gains on qualifying investments, GIFT City presents a compelling jurisdictional arbitrage. However, at TresoWealth, our mandate is to separate structural alpha from regulatory mirage. The "0% tax" headline, while legally accurate under specific parameters, requires forensic scrutiny. Furthermore, the Finance Bill 2025 has introduced an alternative concessional rate of 9%, expanding the architecture but complicating the structural decision.

Here is a precise, skeptical, and structured analysis of GIFT City’s tax benefits, regulatory mechanics, and actionable strategies for foreign HNIs.

---

The Regulatory Architecture: Decoding the 0% Capital Gains Myth

The 0% capital gains tax in GIFT City is not a blanket exemption; it is a highly conditional concession governed by two distinct sections of the Income Tax Act, 1961. To access it, foreign investors must understand the interplay between the fund vehicle and the investor's residential status.

Section 10(4D): The Investor’s Shield

Section 10(4D) is the cornerstone of the GIFT City tax advantage for foreign investors. It stipulates that any income earned by a non-resident from a **Specified Fund** located in the International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) is exempt from Indian income tax.

For an HNI, this means:

  • **Eligibility:** You must be a non-resident Indian (NRI) or a foreign national.
  • **The Vehicle:** You must invest through a "Specified Fund"—predominantly Category III Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) registered in GIFT City.
  • **The Exemption:** Both long-term and short-term capital gains, as well as dividend income generated by this fund from its underlying Indian and global securities, are taxed at 0% in the hands of the non-resident investor.

Section 80LA: The Fund Manager’s Incentive

While Section 10(4D) benefits the investor, Section 80LA benefits the IFSC unit (the fund house or fund manager). Under Section 80LA, IFSC units are entitled to a 100% deduction on profits for the first five consecutive assessment years, and a 50% deduction for the subsequent five years.

**The Skeptical Take:** Why does an HNI care about the fund manager's tax break? Because 80LA subsidizes the operational costs of setting up in GIFT City, allowing fund managers to offer lower management fees and carry percentages compared to traditional Mauritius or Singapore domiciled funds. Without 80LA, the economics of GIFT City fund management would be unsustainable, and the 10(4D) ecosystem would not exist.

---

The Finance Bill 2025 Expansion: The 9% Concessional Rate

Until recently, the architecture was binary: qualify for 0% under 10(4D) or fall back to standard domestic tax rates. The Finance Bill 2025 introduced a critical pivot, expanding the IFSC tax code to include a concessional capital gains rate as low as 9%.

Why Introduce 9% When 0% Exists?

The 9% rate addresses a structural gap. Section 10(4D) strictly applies to non-residents investing in Specified Funds. However, what if an HNI wants to set up a direct holding company, a family investment vehicle (FIV), or invest in an asset class that does not qualify under the rigid "Specified Fund" definition of Category III AIFs?

Under the expanded Finance Bill 2025 provisions, certain capital gains derived by IFSC units that do not qualify for the 10(4D) exemption are now subject to a flat, concessional rate of 9%.

**Actionable Insight:** For foreign HNIs establishing direct corporate vehicles in GIFT City to hold global or Indian unlisted assets, the 9% flat rate provides a definitive ceiling. Compared to the standard Indian corporate tax rate of 25.17% (plus surcharge and cess, pushing effective rates to 30%+), a 9% capital gains rate offers a 70% tax efficiency improvement. It eliminates the uncertainty of treaty shopping and provides a statutory, domestic low-tax alternative.

Comparative Analysis: GIFT City vs. US Brokerage vs. Traditional FPI

To truly quantify the GIFT City advantage, we must benchmark it against the two primary alternatives utilized by foreign HNIs.

GIFT City vs. US Brokerage Investing

Many foreign NRIs based in the US or Middle East utilize US-based brokerages (like Interactive Brokers or Schwab) to access Indian ETFs or direct equities. This is structurally hazardous for three reasons:

1. **Withholding Tax Drag:** US brokerages will withhold 25% to 30% on dividends from US-domiciled India-focused ETFs.

2. **Estate Tax Exposure:** The US levies a 40% estate tax on US-situated assets (including US brokerage accounts and US-domiciled ETFs) exceeding $60,000 for non-resident aliens.

3. **Double Taxation:** Capital gains remitted to India from US vehicles are subject to Indian capital gains tax, with limited treaty relief depending on the investor's domicile.

GIFT City neutralizes this. By investing through a GIFT City AIF, the HNI holds a non-US asset, entirely bypassing the US estate tax trap, while paying 0% Indian capital gains tax under Section 10(4D).

GIFT City vs. Traditional FPI (Mauritius/Singapore Route)

Historically, foreign capital entered India via Mauritius to exploit the Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA). Post-2018, the India-Mauritius treaty was amended, introducing Limitation of Benefit (LoB) clauses and subjecting capital gains to Indian domestic tax rates (10% on long-term equity, 15% on short-term equity, plus surcharges).

  • **Equity LTCG:** Traditional FPI pays 10% (plus surcharge/cess). GIFT City pays 0%.
  • **Debt/Unlisted:** Traditional FPI pays up to 30%+ (plus surcharge/cess). GIFT City pays 0% (via 10(4D)) or 9% (via Finance Bill 2025 direct vehicle).

For a $10 million allocation yielding a 15% return, the GIFT City structure preserves $150,000 to $450,000 that would otherwise be eroded by Indian withholding taxes and surcharges.

---

Section 54 Integration: Reinvesting Indian Real Estate Capital

A frequent liquidity event for foreign HNIs is the sale of inherited or long-held Indian residential real estate. Such sales typically trigger Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) tax at 20% (with indexation) under the domestic regime.

The government has progressively simplified NRI capital gain reinvestment rules. Under **Section 54**, an NRI can claim a capital gains exemption by reinvesting the proceeds into another residential property in India. However, real estate is illiquid, and management from abroad is operationally burdensome.

**The Strategic Pivot:** While Section 54 mandates reinvestment into physical real estate for the exemption, the broader simplification of NRI capital gains rules now allows for smoother repatriation of the *un-taxable* corpus. An HNI can sell Indian real estate, utilize Section 54 to shield the gain by purchasing a single qualifying residential property, and then deploy their *other offshore liquid capital* into GIFT City.

By isolating the real estate gain via Section 54, and deploying fresh offshore capital into GIFT City, the HNI achieves dual tax optimization: 0% tax on the GIFT City market returns, and deferred/neutralized tax on the real estate disposition.

The Skeptical Lens: Caveats, Compliance, and Friction Costs

At TresoWealth, we do not endorse tax structures without rigorously auditing the friction costs. GIFT City is not a panacea. It demands strict compliance, and failure to adhere to the letter of the law will retroactively trigger the very taxes you sought to avoid.

1. The Substance Requirement

GIFT City is not a "brass plate" jurisdiction like certain Caribbean islands. IFSC units must demonstrate economic substance. Fund managers must maintain a minimum of 13 full-time employees, incur minimum operational expenditures, and conduct core income-generating activities locally. For HNIs, this means conducting deep due diligence on the AIF sponsor to ensure their GIFT City operations are genuinely compliant. A failed substance audit collapses the 10(4D) exemption.

2. Sunset Clauses

Tax exemptions in GIFT City are not perpetual. Section 10(4D) and Section 80LA currently carry sunset dates. As of the latest regulatory updates, the 80LA exemption applies to units commencing operations before March 31, 2026. While the Finance Bill 2025 has expanded the tax code (introducing the 9% rate), the 0% 10(4D) exemption must be monitored for future extension. An HNI committing $50 million today must model the scenario where the 0% rate expires and defaults to the 9% concessional rate or standard rates a decade from now.

3. Liquidity and Market Depth

GIFT City operates on the NSE-IFSC and BSE-IFSC exchanges. While they provide access to Indian equities via Nifty derivatives and specific debt securities, the market depth is a fraction of the onshore NSE/BSE. For HNIs deploying large blocks of capital, slippage on entry and exit is a tangible risk that can negate the tax alpha.

4. Repatriation Mechanics

While the RBI has streamlined repatriation, operational bottlenecks remain. Converting INR to USD within the IFSC banking framework can occasionally face friction due to domestic banking liquidity constraints. Your capital may be tax-free, but it must be physically mobile.

The TresoWealth Actionable Playbook for Foreign HNIs

To systematically capture the GIFT City tax alpha while mitigating structural risk, foreign HNIs should adopt the following framework:

**Step 1: Route Selection (AIF vs. Direct Vehicle)**

  • If your objective is passive exposure to Indian equities and debt, utilize a GIFT City Category III AIF. This unlocks Section 10(4D) for the 0% capital gains rate, and the AIF sponsor absorbs the substance compliance burden.
  • If your objective is direct control over unlisted investments, global allocation, or bespoke family office structures, establish a direct IFSC unit. This falls under the Finance Bill 2025 provisions, subjecting capital gains to the 9% concessional rate—a superior outcome to the 30%+ domestic alternative.

**Step 2: US Situs Asset Purge**

For NRIs residing in the US or utilizing US brokerages, systematically liquidate US-domiciled India ETFs and re-allocate to GIFT City vehicles. This immediately removes your portfolio from the purview of the 40% US estate tax and eliminates dividend withholding drag.

**Step 3: Real Estate Liquidity Optimization**

If holding legacy Indian real estate, execute sales utilizing Section 54 exemptions to shield the LTCG. Rather than reinvesting the entire corpus into illiquid physical property, deploy the required minimum into a qualifying residential asset, and route your offshore liquidity into GIFT City to generate 0% tax-optimized market returns.

**Step 4: Counterparty Due Diligence**

Before committing capital to a GIFT City AIF, mandate proof of their IFSCA registration, their physical office lease, and their employee roster. If the fund manager fails a substance test, the Income Tax Department will disallow the 10(4D) exemption, and the HNI will inherit a 30%+ tax liability plus penalties.

Conclusion

GIFT City represents a paradigm shift for foreign capital entering India. The availability of a statutory 0% capital gains rate under Section 10(4D) and the expanded 9% concessional rate under the Finance Bill 2025 provide unprecedented tax efficiency. Compared to the US brokerage estate tax trap or the eroded Mauritius treaty benefits, GIFT City is structurally superior.

However, tax alpha is only valid if it survives regulatory scrutiny. The 0% rate is a conditional privilege, bound by strict substance rules, specific vehicle requirements, and sunset clauses. Foreign HNIs must approach GIFT City not as a tax haven, but as a highly regulated, concessional financial zone. By aligning vehicle selection with investment objectives and rigorously auditing counterparty compliance, HNIs can securely protect and compound their Indian allocations.
