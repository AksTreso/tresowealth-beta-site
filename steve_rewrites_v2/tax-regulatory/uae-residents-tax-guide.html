GIFT CITY AIF Tax Guide for UAE Residents

GIFT City AIFs for UAE Residents: A Structural Tax Arbitrage or Regulatory Mirage?

For UAE-based High Net Worth Individuals (HNIs) looking to capture India’s growth premium, the friction has never been about market returns—it has been about jurisdictional tax drag. India’s robust regulatory framework, while protective, has historically subjected foreign capital to complex withholding taxes, surcharges, and treaty shopping limitations.

Enter GIFT City (Gujarat International Finance Tec-City). Positioned as a financial gateway, GIFT City operates as a distinct jurisdiction within India, offering a regulatory arbitrage that demands precise evaluation. At TresoWealth, we view GIFT City not as a panacea, but as a structural tool. When deployed correctly, it materially alters the after-tax return profile for UAE residents. When misunderstood, it creates compliance bottlenecks and liquidity mismatches.

This guide dismantles the GIFT City Alternative Investment Fund (AIF) framework, specifically tailored for UAE-domiciled investors, analyzing the recent regulatory shifts, the exact tax architecture, and the benchmarking realities that private bankers often gloss over.

The $75,000 Paradigm Shift: Redefining Access

Historically, the GIFT City AIF structure was restricted to institutional capital and ultra-high-ticket retail investors due to a prohibitive minimum investment threshold of $150,000.

However, in February 2025, the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) mandated a structural reduction, dropping the minimum investment requirement from $150,000 to $75,000.

**The Strategic Implication:**

This is not merely a quantitative reduction; it is a qualitative shift in portfolio construction. For a UAE-based HNI allocating $1 million to Indian equities, the previous threshold limited diversification to a maximum of six distinct GIFT City AIFs. At $75,000, the same capital can be stratified across 13 to 14 specialized strategies—allowing precise alpha generation across private equity, venture capital, and long-short hedge fund strategies without over-concentrating manager risk.

Decoding the Tax Arbitrage: Sections 10(4D), 80LA, and 206AA

The core value proposition of GIFT City lies in its tax architecture. For UAE residents, who benefit from a zero-personal-income-tax environment, the objective is to ensure capital repatriation is not eroded by Indian tax liabilities. GIFT City facilitates this through three specific regulatory pillars.

1. The Capital Gains Shield: Section 10(4D)

Under the Income Tax Act, Section 10(4D) provides a specific exemption for income earned by non-residents from investments in GIFT City AIFs. If your GIFT City gains are exempt in India under Section 10(4D), and you are a UAE resident, the income is not subject to Indian capital gains tax, nor is it subject to tax in the UAE.

This creates a bilateral zero-tax corridor. However, a skeptical approach is required: this exemption applies strictly to specified categories of AIFs in GIFT City. Investing in a domestic AIF outside GIFT City subjects UAE residents to a 10% to 20% capital gains tax (plus surcharges and cess), immediately eroding alpha.

2. The Business Income Holiday: Section 80LA

GIFT AIFs have a 100% tax holiday under Section 80LA for 10 of the first 15 years on business income, allowing for full exemption.

*The TresoWealth Caveat:* It is critical to distinguish between "business income" and "capital gains." For Category I and II AIFs (Private Equity, Venture Capital), income is typically treated as capital gains in the hands of the investor, making 80LA less relevant for the investor's personal tax liability. However, for Category III AIFs (Hedge Funds), where the fund's trading income could be construed as business income, the 80LA holiday ensures the fund vehicle itself is not taxed, preserving the pass-through efficiency. Do not conflate the fund's 80LA holiday with a blanket exemption from capital gains; always verify the AIF category.

3. The Operational Bypass: Section 206AA

One of the most persistent operational hurdles for NRIs investing in India is the mandatory requirement of a Permanent Account Number (PAN) under Section 206AA. Without a PAN, withholding tax rates spike to 20%, regardless of treaty benefits.

For GIFT City investors, the rules shift. Under the Income Tax Act, Section 206AA exemption for IFSC specifically states that investors in GIFT City AIFs **do not need a PAN card**.

**The UAE Advantage:** For a UAE resident, obtaining an Indian PAN is an administrative friction point that often delays capital deployment. The Section 206AA exemption means a UAE investor can subscribe to a GIFT City AIF using only their Emirates ID and passport, bypassing Indian tax registration entirely while still benefiting from the zero-withholding tax structure under Section 10(4D).

Benchmarking Realities: Private Equity vs. Hedge Funds

A fundamental flaw in evaluating GIFT City AIFs is the misuse of benchmarks. Private equity and hedge fund AIFs require entirely different performance metrics, and using generic Indian indices (like the Nifty 50) is intellectually dishonest.

**Private Equity (Cat I & II) AIFs:**

These are illiquid, long-duration vehicles (typically 7-10 years). Benchmarking them against the Nifty 50 ignores the illiquidity premium. UAE investors should demand that GIFT City PE AIFs benchmark against the Cambridge Associates India PE/VC Index or a customized Public Market Equivalent (PME). If a PE AIF is merely matching Nifty 50 returns, the illiquidity is unjustified. The GIFT City advantage here is the tax-efficient compounding of unlisted gains over a decade.

**Hedge Fund (Cat III) AIFs:**

These are absolute-return vehicles. Benchmarking a long-short equity or multi-strategy hedge fund against a long-only equity index is structurally flawed. The correct benchmarks are MSCI India or risk-free rates (like the RBI's repo rate) plus a 400-600 basis point alpha premium. For UAE investors, GIFT City Cat III AIFs offer a distinct advantage: the ability to execute complex strategies (derivatives, shorting) that mutual funds cannot, compounded tax-free.

GIFT City vs. Offshore Mutual Funds: The Structural Cleanliness

For UAE NRIs, the three primary vehicles for Indian equity exposure are Domestic Mutual Funds, Offshore Mutual Funds (typically domiciled in Mauritius or Singapore), and GIFT City AIFs. While all three are operationally accessible, GIFT City is structurally "cleaner" for high-ticket capital.

1. **Treaty Shopping Scrutiny:** Offshore funds in Mauritius have faced intense scrutiny from Indian tax authorities regarding the General Anti-Avoidance Rule (GAAR) and Limitation of Benefits (LoB) clauses. GIFT City, being a recognized IFSC under Indian law, operates outside the ambiguity of treaty shopping.

2. **Withholding Efficiency:** Offshore funds are subject to withholding tax on dividends and capital gains at the source before repatriation. GIFT City AIFs, leveraging Section 10(4D), bypass this withholding entirely.

3. **Regulatory Oversight:** GIFT City falls under the dual oversight of SEBI and IFSCA, providing a higher degree of investor protection and transparency compared to certain offshore jurisdictions.

Quantifying the Alpha: A Capital Gains Case Study

To understand the material impact of the GIFT City structure, consider the following case study comparing the tax liability of a UAE resident investing in a Domestic Mutual Fund versus a GIFT City Mutual Fund/AIF.

**Scenario:** A UAE resident realizes long-term capital gains (LTCG) on an Indian equity portfolio.

**Domestic Mutual Fund Taxation (Non-GIFT City):**

Under the current domestic regime, LTCG above ₹1.25 lakh is taxed at 12.5%, plus applicable surcharges and cess.

  • **LTCG Tax (12.5% above ₹1.25 lakh):** ₹2,46,875
  • **Surcharge (10%):** ₹24,688
  • **Cess (4%):** ₹10,863
  • **STT (Securities Transaction Tax):** Approximately ₹4,200
  • **Total Tax Liability:** **₹2,86,626**

**GIFT City AIF/Mutual Fund Taxation:**

Under Section 10(4D), the capital gains are exempt. Furthermore, GIFT City transactions do not attract STT.

  • **LTCG Tax:** ₹0
  • **Surcharge:** ₹0
  • **Cess:** ₹0
  • **STT:** ₹0
  • **Total Tax Liability:** **₹0**

**The Alpha Generation:**

By eliminating a tax outflow of ₹2,86,626, the GIFT City structure allows the full capital to be reinvested. Over a standard 5-year compounding period, avoiding this tax drag results in **a 13.6% improvement in terminal returns**, just from structural optimization. This is not alpha generated by manager skill; this is structural alpha preserved through jurisdictional arbitrage.

Actionable Insights for UAE HNIs

Before allocating capital to a GIFT City AIF, UAE investors must execute the following due diligence:

1. **Verify the AIF Category, Not Just the Domicile:** Section 10(4D) applies to specified AIFs. Ensure the fund's offering document explicitly confirms IFSCA registration and eligibility for the Section 10(4D) exemption. A Category III AIF utilizing the 80LA business income holiday operates differently from a Category II PE fund relying on capital gains exemptions.

2. **Demand Appropriate Benchmarks:** Reject pitchbooks that compare illiquid PE or absolute-return hedge funds to the Nifty 50. Insist on PME for PE and risk-premium benchmarks for hedge funds.

3. **Leverage the $75,000 Threshold for Manager Diversification:** With the February 2025 threshold reduction, avoid concentrating $500,000 into a single manager. Allocate $75,000 to $100,000 across 5 to 7 distinct GIFT City strategies to mitigate idiosyncratic manager risk.

4. **Streamline Compliance via Section 206AA:** Do not let intermediaries force you into obtaining an Indian PAN. Cite the Section 206AA IFSC exemption to expedite onboarding. Ensure your Emirates ID and UAE bank statements are apostilled to satisfy the AIF's KYC requirements seamlessly.

5. **Evaluate Repatriation Mechanics:** Ensure your GIFT City AIF utilizes an IFSC Banking Unit (IBU) for capital calls and distributions. This guarantees that repatriation of capital and tax-exempt gains to your UAE bank account is not delayed by domestic Indian banking regulations.

Conclusion

GIFT City is not a marketing gimmick; it is a structurally superior jurisdiction for UAE residents seeking Indian exposure. The convergence of the reduced $75,000 minimum investment, the Section 10(4D) capital gains exemption, the Section 80LA business income holiday, and the PAN-less onboarding under Section 206AA creates an unparalleled investment corridor.

However, alpha is never free. The 13.6% improvement in returns demonstrated in our case study is only realized if the underlying AIF strategy is sound and the benchmarks are honest. At TresoWealth, we advise UAE HNIs to view GIFT City as a protective tax shield—one that preserves the alpha generated by rigorous, appropriately benchmarked fund managers, rather than a substitute for fundamental investment due diligence.
