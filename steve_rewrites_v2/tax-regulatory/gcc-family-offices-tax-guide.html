GIFT City AIF Tax Guide for GCC Family Offices: Navigating the 2026 Regulatory Reality

For Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) family offices, the narrative surrounding India’s Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) has been overwhelmingly triumphant—marketed as a seamless, tax-advantaged gateway into the world’s fifth-largest economy. However, the reality of deploying capital through GIFT City Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) demands a far more rigorous examination.

At TresoWealth, we operate on a singular premise: capital preservation requires structural precision, not blind adherence to market euphoria. For GCC family offices seeking exposure to Indian equities, private credit, or real estate, GIFT City AIFs offer undeniable advantages. Yet, the regulatory architecture—particularly the impending GIFT City AIF Regulations 2026 and the nuanced mechanics of Section 115UB of the Income Tax Act of 1961—introduces complexities that can erode returns if not navigated with extreme skepticism and expert foresight.

This guide deconstructs the GIFT City AIF framework, stripping away the marketing rhetoric to provide GCC family offices with a clear, actionable, and legally sound roadmap for Indian deployment.

The Regulatory Architecture: GIFT City AIF Regulations 2026

The International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) has been progressively tightening the regulatory framework to ensure GIFT City aligns with global standards of institutional integrity. The upcoming GIFT City AIF Regulations 2026 serve as a powerful reality check for Indian family offices and foreign investors alike, shifting the paradigm from lax offshore experimentation to stringent compliance.

Under the 2026 framework, the baseline entry ticket for foreign investors is firmly established. The minimum commitment per investor stands at USD 150,000 (approximately ₹1.25 crore). While this threshold is significantly lower than the ₹1 crore minimum mandated for domestic AIFs under the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI), it remains a substantial commitment that necessitates precise portfolio sizing.

Crucially, the 2026 regulations delineate clear boundaries on leverage and structure. Leverage is permitted, but strictly circumscribed for Category I and Category II AIFs. For Category II AIFs—which remain the preferred vehicle for GCC family offices targeting private equity and structured debt—leverage is allowed only to the extent of meeting temporary liquidity shortfalls, capped at a strict 20% of the fund’s net asset value. This restriction prevents the kind of excessive leverage that historically plagued offshore structures, protecting GCC capital from margin-call-induced liquidations in volatile Indian markets.

Furthermore, the 2026 regulations mandate how these vehicles must be domiciled. Structured as trusts, companies, or LLPs, these funds operate within the IFSC, but the choice of structure has profound implications for GCC investors:

  • **Trusts:** Offer the highest degree of tax pass-through efficiency but complicate direct corporate governance for GCC families accustomed to equity-shareholding structures.
  • **Companies:** Provide familiar governance and liability shields, but risk double taxation at both the fund and investor level if not meticulously managed under IFSC exemptions.
  • **LLPs:** Offer operational flexibility and pass-through taxation, but face stringent regulatory scrutiny regarding partner liability and cross-border profit repatriation.

Deconstructing the Tax Pass-Through: Section 115UB

The primary allure of the GIFT City AIF for GCC investors is the promise of tax efficiency. However, "tax-efficient" is not synonymous with "tax-free." The critical provision governing this is Section 115UB of the Income Tax Act of 1961.

Under Section 115UB, the tax pass-through status applies specifically to Category I and Category II AIFs. This means the AIF itself is not subject to Indian income tax; instead, the income is passed through to the unitholders—i.e., the GCC family office—who are then taxed at their applicable rates.

**The Skeptical Viewpoint:** While pass-through status prevents double taxation at the fund level, it transfers the compliance burden entirely to the foreign investor. GCC family offices must recognize that income passed through under Section 115UB retains its character. Therefore:

1. **Dividend Income:** Passed through as dividend, subject to a 20% withholding tax (subject to Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements - DTAAs).

2. **Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG):** Passed through as STCG, taxed at applicable rates depending on the underlying asset class.

3. **Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG):** Passed through as LTCG, often benefiting from concessional rates (e.g., 10% on listed equities exceeding ₹1 lakh).

For GCC entities, the application of the India-UAE or India-Saudi Arabia DTAA is paramount. If the GCC family office lacks a valid Tax Residency Certificate (TRC), the AIF administrator is legally obligated to withhold tax at the higher of the treaty rate or the domestic rate. Failure to structure the GCC entity correctly prior to capital deployment will result in irreversible tax leakage.

Operational Rigor: The Mandatory Fund Administrator

Transparency is no longer optional; it is a regulatory mandate. Under current and forthcoming IFSCA benchmarks, the mandatory appointment of a fund administrator for transparency is a non-negotiable prerequisite for GIFT City AIFs.

This requirement serves as a critical safeguard for GCC family offices. Historitionally, offshore Indian structures suffered from opaque valuation practices and irregular reporting. The mandatory fund administrator—an independent entity responsible for NAV calculations, compliance monitoring, and financial reporting—creates an institutional bulwark against mismanagement.

For GCC investors, this translates into standardized, audited reporting aligned with International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS). However, the cost of this transparency must be factored into the Total Expense Ratio (TER). A GIFT City AIF with a TER below 1.5% is likely cutting corners on administrative rigor, posing a latent risk to capital preservation.

Case Study: The Al-Rashid Family Office Structural Pitfall

*The following case study is a composite representation of common structural failures observed in GCC-to-India capital deployments, based on real-world advisory scenarios.*

**The Setup:** The Al-Rashid Family Office, a multi-generational wealth vehicle based in Dubai, sought to deploy USD 50 million into Indian infrastructure and mid-market private equity. Attracted by the GIFT City narrative, they committed USD 10 million as a limited partner in a GIFT City Category II AIF structured as a Company.

**The Pitfall:** The Al-Rashid family assumed that because the AIF was domiciled in GIFT City, all returns would be tax-exempt under the IFSC 10-year tax holiday. They failed to scrutinize the interplay between the fund's structure and Section 115UB.

Because the fund was structured as a Company rather than a Trust, the AIF earned dividend income from Indian portfolio companies. Under Section 115UB, this income was passed through to the Al-Rashid Family Office. However, the family office had not secured a UAE Tax Residency Certificate for the year of income distribution. Consequently, the Indian AIF administrator was forced to withhold tax at the statutory domestic rate of 20% on the dividend income, rather than the 0% rate available under the India-UAE DTAA.

Furthermore, the Al-Rashid office discovered that the 10-year IFSC tax holiday applied only to the *fund's* business income, not to the capital gains passed through to the investors under Section 115UB. Their assumption of blanket tax exemption resulted in an unplanned tax liability of approximately USD 400,000, severely impacting the net IRR of their Indian deployment.

**The Resolution:** TresoWealth intervened to restructure the family's subsequent commitments. We mandated that all future deployments be channeled through a GIFT City AIF structured as a Trust, ensuring clean pass-through mechanics. We also instituted a strict compliance calendar requiring the Al-Rashid office to obtain and remit their UAE TRC to the fund administrator by Q1 of every fiscal year, ensuring DTAA benefits were securely captured.

Actionable Insights for GCC Family Offices

To navigate the GIFT City AIF landscape effectively, GCC family offices must adopt a protective, structurally rigorous approach.

**1. Prioritize Trust Structures for Pass-Through Efficiency**

Unless there is a compelling legal reason for corporate governance, GCC investors should insist on AIFs structured as Trusts. This ensures that Section 115UB operates cleanly, avoiding the risk of residual taxation at the fund level and simplifying the repatriation of capital to the UAE or Saudi Arabia.

**2. Secure Tax Residency Certificates (TRCs) Proactively**

Do not wait for income distribution to secure your TRC. The Indian tax authorities are highly stringent about DTAA claims. Ensure your GCC entity has a valid TRC, a certificate of incorporation, and a tax identification number ready before the first capital call is made.

**3. Audit the Fund Administrator's Independence**

Demand transparency regarding the fund administrator. Verify that the administrator is a reputable, IFSCA-registered independent third party—not an affiliate of the fund manager. The administrator's independence is your primary defense against inflated NAVs and opaque fee structures.

**4. Scrutinize Leverage Covenants**

While leverage is allowed for Category II AIFs in GIFT City, excessive reliance on leverage amplifies downside risk in India’s often-illiquid mid-market space. Cap your exposure to funds utilizing less than 10% leverage for liquidity management, strictly avoiding funds that use debt to artificially boost equity returns.

**5. Map the 2026 Regulatory Horizon**

The GIFT City AIF Regulations 2026 will likely introduce stricter reporting requirements and potential modifications to permissible investment universes. Ensure your subscription agreements include clauses that allow for orderly redemption or renegotiation of terms if the 2026 regulations materially alter the fund's risk profile or tax structure.
