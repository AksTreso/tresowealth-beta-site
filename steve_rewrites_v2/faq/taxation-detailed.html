Taxation FAQ: Decoding 0% LTCG, DTAA, and Withholding Tax for Foreign HNIs in India

India’s capital markets have delivered a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of approximately 12.5% (INR terms) over the last decade, consistently outperforming emerging market peers. However, for the Foreign High Net Worth Individual (HNI), gross returns are a mirage. The actual determinant of portfolio alpha is the post-tax yield—a metric frequently eroded by structural friction in the Indian tax code.

At TresoWealth, we operate on a foundational premise: do not let the complexity of the Indian Income Tax Act, 1961, subsidize your returns. Below, we dissect the most critical, and frequently misunderstood, taxation mechanics for foreign investors—stripping away the theoretical to focus strictly on the operational and the actionable.

1. The 0% LTCG Reality: Exemptions vs. Structural Arbitrage

The promise of "0% Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG)" in India is not a myth, but it is heavily conditional. For foreign investors, achieving a zero-tax outcome on capital gains requires precise alignment with specific regulatory frameworks, rather than relying on broad-based market exemptions.

The SEBI-Registered Fund Exemption: Section 10(23FBA)

For institutional and ultra-high-net-worth structures, the most robust pathway to tax-exempt income in India lies in Section 10(23FBA) of the Income Tax Act. This provision explicitly states that the income of a specified fund registered with the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) is exempt from Indian income tax.

To leverage this, foreign capital must be routed into Category I or Category II Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) that specifically qualify under this section.

  • **The Precision Requirement:** Not all AIFs qualify. Category III AIFs—the preferred vehicle for many hedge-fund-style strategies—do *not* enjoy this exemption.
  • **The Skeptical Take:** If your advisor suggests a direct equity portfolio or a Category III AIF for tax efficiency, they are structurally misaligned with your net objectives. The 0% rate is an artifact of vehicle selection, not asset selection.

The IFSC Arbitrage: Section 10(4D)

The Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) operates as a financial Special Economic Zone. Under Section 10(4D), any income accruing or arising to or received by a non-resident from a specified transaction undertaken on the International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) exchanges is exempt from tax, provided the transaction is carried out in a currency other than the Indian Rupee.

  • **Actionable Insight:** For Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs) looking for 0% LTCG on listed Indian equities, trading through a GIFT City broker on the IFSC segment is the only legally unassailable method to achieve a zero-tax rate on direct equities, circumventing the standard 10% LTCG (plus surcharge and cess) under Section 112.

The Mauritius/Singapore DTAA Grandfathering Trap

Historically, foreign investors utilized the Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements (DTAAs) with Mauritius or Singapore to claim 0% LTCG on Indian shares, as capital gains were taxed solely in the resident country (which levied 0%). Following the 2016 protocol amendments, India introduced a Source-Based Taxation rule.

  • **The Grandfathering Clause:** Investments made prior to April 1, 2017, still qualify for the 0% LTCG rate under the old treaty. Investments made after this date are subject to Indian domestic tax rates (10% on LTCG for listed equities, 15% on STCG).
  • **The Risk:** The Indian Revenue Department aggressively scrutinizes Mauritius/Singapore structures for "treaty shopping." Under the General Anti-Avoidance Rule (GAAR), if the structure lacks commercial substance (e.g., no local employees, no real office in Mauritius), the 0% LTCG claim will be rejected, resulting in a 10% tax liability plus potential penalties and interest.

2. DTAA Mechanics: Section 90 vs. Section 91

When income is taxed in both the investor's home jurisdiction and India, Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements become the primary shield. However, the method of relief depends entirely on whether a bilateral treaty exists.

Bilateral Treaties: Section 90

Section 90 of the Income Tax Act empowers the Central Government to enter into agreements with foreign countries to avoid double taxation. For foreign HNIs, the DTAA provides relief in two ways:

1. **Exemption Method:** Income is taxed in only one country (e.g., the 0% LTCG on pre-2017 Mauritius investments).

2. **Credit Method:** Income is taxed in the source country (India), and the resident country allows a Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) against its domestic tax liability.

**The Operational Friction:** Claiming FTC is not automatic. An HNI based in the UK, for instance, paying 10% LTCG in India, must obtain a Form 10F and a Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) from India to claim the credit in the UK. Failure to secure these exact documents results in double taxation.

Unilateral Relief: Section 91

What happens if the HNI resides in a country that does *not* have a DTAA with India (e.g., certain jurisdictions in the Middle East or emerging markets)? This is where Section 91 becomes critical.

Section 91 allows unilateral relief to a resident of a foreign country with which India has no DTAA, provided that country allows a similar relief to Indian residents.

  • **The Calculation:** If an HNI from a non-treaty country earns ₹10 million in capital gains in India, and the Indian tax rate is 10% (₹1 million), but the home country tax rate is 5% (₹500,000), the HNI can claim a deduction of the lower of the two taxes. The effective tax paid in India remains ₹1 million, but the home country must exempt that portion.
  • **Skeptical Warning:** Section 91 relief is an administrative nightmare. Indian tax assessors frequently challenge the "reciprocity" clause, demanding proof that the HNI's home country extends the exact same relief to Indian residents. Do not rely on Section 91 without advance ruling clarity.

3. The Withholding Tax (TDS) Trap: Section 195

The most immediate threat to a foreign HNI’s cash flow in India is not the final tax liability, but the Withholding Tax (Tax Deducted at Source - TDS) under Section 195.

Any person responsible for paying to a non-resident any interest or any other sum (including capital gains) is required to deduct tax at the rates in force.

The Cash Flow Erosion

The Indian domestic tax rate on LTCG for listed equities is 10% (plus applicable surcharge and cess). However, Indian brokers and paying agents, terrified of penalties for under-withholding, routinely deduct TDS at the maximum marginal rate—often 20% plus surcharge and cess (effective rates exceeding 24%)—especially if the investor's DTAA status is not formally registered with the broker.

**Case Study: The UK Family Office**

A UK-based Family Office executed a ₹500 million sale of Indian equities. Because they had not filed the requisite DTAA declaration with their Indian custodian prior to the trade, the custodian withheld 20% (₹100 million) plus a 15% surcharge and 4% cess, totaling roughly ₹119.2 million. The actual final tax liability under the India-UK DTAA was only ₹11.2 million (10% plus surcharge/cess). The Family Office had ₹108 million of capital locked in the Indian tax system for 18 months awaiting a refund.

Actionable Strategy: Form 13

Under Section 195(2) and 195(3), an HNI can apply to the Assessing Officer (AO) for a certificate authorizing the payer to deduct tax at a *lower rate* or *nil rate*. This is done via **Form 13**.

1. **Pre-Trade Filing:** File Form 13 at least 30-45 days before the anticipated transaction.

2. **Documentation:** You must provide the AO with your TRC, PAN (Permanent Account Number), DTAA article references, and a detailed computation of expected gains.

3. **The TresoWealth Mandate:** Never execute a large exit without a lower deduction certificate in hand. The opportunity cost of locked capital invariably exceeds the administrative cost of Form 13.

4. Surcharge and Cess: The Hidden Multipliers

Quoting the Indian LTCG rate as "10%" is factually incomplete. The Indian tax code applies a surcharge (an additional tax on the tax itself) and a Health and Education Cess (HEC) based on total taxable income in India.

For Foreign HNIs, the income thresholds for surcharges are structurally punitive:

  • **Income > ₹50 Lakhs (≈ $60,000):** 10% Surcharge
  • **Income > ₹1 Crore (≈ $120,000):** 15% Surcharge
  • **Income > ₹2 Crore (≈ $240,000):** 25% Surcharge
  • **Income > ₹5 Crore (≈ $600,000):** 37% Surcharge

**The Cap on Capital Gains:** For long-term capital gains under Section 112, the maximum surcharge is capped at 15%, even if income exceeds ₹5 Crore. However, for short-term capital gains (STCG) under Section 111A, the surcharge can go up to 37% if income exceeds ₹5 Crore.

**Effective Tax Rate Calculation (LTCG on Listed Equities for >₹5 Crore income):**

  • Base Tax: 10%
  • Surcharge (Capped at 15%): 1.5%
  • Health and Education Cess (4% on Base + Surcharge): 0.46%
  • **Total Effective Rate:** 11.96%

**Effective Tax Rate Calculation (STCG on Listed Equities for >₹5 Crore income):**

  • Base Tax: 15%
  • Surcharge (Up to 37%): 5.55%
  • Health and Education Cess (4% on Base + Surcharge): 0.822%
  • **Total Effective Rate:** 21.372%

*Insight:* A 10% LTCG rate is a regulatory fiction. The material rate is 11.96%. When evaluating deal IRR, always apply the surcharge-adjusted rate.

5. Compliance Architecture: 15CA/15CB and PAN Mandates

Repatriating funds out of India is a controlled process. No bank will remit funds without compliance with the 15CA/15CB regime.

  • **Form 15CA:** A declaration made by the remitter (the HNI or their Indian entity) furnishing details of the remittance and the tax deducted.
  • **Form 15CB:** A certificate from a Chartered Accountant (CA) in India verifying that the provisions of the Income Tax Act and applicable DTAA have been complied with regarding the TDS on the remittance.

**The PAN Requirement:** To file Form 15CA/15CB, a PAN is mandatory. While a non-resident without a PAN was historically able to claim DTAA benefits, Section 206AA now mandates that if the PAN is not provided, TDS must be deducted at the higher of:

1. 20%

2. The rate specified in the Finance Act

3. The rate specified in the DTAA

Therefore, obtaining a PAN is not optional; it is the prerequisite to accessing the DTAA rates you are entitled to.

The TresoWealth Protective Framework

To navigate the Indian tax labyrinth without sacrificing returns, Foreign HNIs must implement a strict, chronological operational protocol:

1. **Vehicle Optimization First:** Before capital hits Indian shores, determine if your strategy qualifies for the Section 10(23FBA) AIF exemption or the GIFT City IFSC exemption. Direct equity exposure should be a conscious, post-tax decision, not a default.

2. **PAN and TRC Procurement:** Secure an Indian PAN and your local Tax Residency Certificate before the first rupee is invested. Without these, DTAA benefits are inaccessible.

3. **Custodian DTAA Registration:** Ensure your Indian custodian/broker has your DTAA declaration on file to prevent default 20% TDS on gains.

4. **Pre-Exit Form 13 Filing:** 45 days prior to any major portfolio liquidation, file for a lower deduction certificate. Do not allow the Indian revenue service to hold your capital hostage via over-withholding.

5. **15CA/15CB Pre-Staging:** Engage a domestic CA to prepare the 15CA/15CB framework before the settlement date to ensure immediate repatriation capability.

India offers exceptional growth, but it does not offer leniency on procedural compliance. Structure precisely, document skeptically, and protect your alpha from the friction of the code.
