Investment Products FAQ: AIFs, PMS, Fund of Funds

Navigating Indian Alpha: A Structural FAQ on AIFs, PMS, and Fund of Funds for Foreign HNIs

India remains a critical arena for capital allocation, offering demographic dividends and GDP growth trajectories rarely found in developed markets. However, for the Foreign High Net Worth Individual (HNI), accessing this alpha requires penetrating a complex, multi-layered regulatory and structural ecosystem. The Indian investment landscape is not a monolith; it is bifurcated by regulation, ticket sizes, and operational mechanics.

At TresoWealth, we maintain a strictly structured and skeptical approach to product selection. Generic exposure is no longer sufficient. To protect and grow capital, foreign investors must understand the precise mechanics of the vehicles available to them. This FAQ dissects the three primary avenues for sophisticated capital deployment in India: Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs), Portfolio Management Services (PMS), and Fund of Funds (FoFs).

1. The Core Vehicles: AIFs, PMS, and FoFs

What is an Alternative Investment Fund (AIF)?

An AIF is a privately pooled investment vehicle domiciled in India or the GIFT City IFSC, collecting funds from sophisticated investors for investing in accordance with a defined investment policy. Under the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) framework, AIFs are categorized by strategy:

  • **Category I:** Venture Capital, Infrastructure, and Angel funds (positive spillover effects).
  • **Category II:** Private Equity, Private Credit, and Real Estate funds (no leverage, no priority distribution).
  • **Category III:** Hedge funds and complex strategies (employing leverage, listed or unlisted derivatives).

**The Skeptical View:** AIFs are blind pools. Once capital is committed, the Limited Partner (LP) has virtually no say in day-to-day deployment. The minimum investment is ₹1 Crore (approx. $120,000), making it a high-stakes commitment where alignment of interest is paramount.

What is Portfolio Management Services (PMS)?

PMS is a bespoke, discretionary or non-discretionary service where a portfolio manager manages the funds of an individual client. The minimum ticket size is ₹50 Lakhs (approx. $60,000). Unlike an AIF, the investor holds direct ownership of the underlying securities (stocks, bonds).

**The Skeptical View:** PMS offers the illusion of control. While you own the stocks, the manager still dictates the trades. Furthermore, the direct ownership structure can create severe tax inefficiencies and administrative burdens for foreign investors, as every corporate action, dividend, and capital gain is directly attributable to the individual's PAN (Permanent Account Number).

What is a Fund of Funds (FoF)?

An FoF is a mutual fund or AIF that invests in other funds rather than direct securities. For foreign investors, FoFs—particularly those domiciled in GIFT City—serve as critical feeder vehicles, routing foreign capital into domestic Indian AIFs or mutual funds while mitigating direct regulatory friction.

**The Skeptical View:** FoFs introduce a double layer of fees. If an FoF invests in a Category III AIF, the investor pays the FoF management fee plus the underlying AIF's management and performance fees. This fee drag can suffocate net returns if the structural arbitrage is not explicitly quantified.

2. AIFs vs. PMS vs. Mutual Funds: The Structural Distinction

To understand the key differences between PMS funds, mutual funds, and AIFs, one must look beyond marketing brochures and examine the legal and operational architecture.

  • **Mutual Funds (MFs):** Highly regulated, retail-oriented, strictly mandated diversification (e.g., 25% cap on single-sector exposure), and low minimums (₹500). They are designed for the masses, meaning they cannot take concentrated bets.
  • **PMS:** Tailored for HNIs. Allows concentrated portfolios (up to 25% in a single stock). However, PMS is restricted primarily to listed securities and debt. It lacks the structural flexibility to invest in unlisted assets or employ complex hedging strategies.
  • **AIFs:** The domain of institutional capital. AIFs can invest across the capital structure—unlisted equity, distressed debt, venture debt, and complex derivatives. They allow for priority distributions (waterfalls) and bespoke fee structures (management + performance fees).

**The Distinction in Action:** The distinction between AIFs and PMS provides clear and concise information to investors for their investment growth. If you seek pure listed-equity alpha with high transparency, PMS may suffice. If you seek structural advantages—such as participating in a pre-IPO unicorn or accessing a structured debt yield—only an AIF provides the legal mandate to execute.

3. The Regulatory Architecture: SEBI and IFSCA

Foreign investors cannot navigate India using a single regulatory lens. The jurisdiction is split between SEBI (domestic) and the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) in GIFT City.

SEBI AIF Regulations

Under SEBI AIF Regulations, Regulation 23(4) mandates strict governance: *"The Manager shall ensure that the AIF appoints a compliance officer... and adheres to the provisions of the regulations regarding conflict of interest."* This is a critical protective measure. It ensures that the fund manager cannot unilaterally alter the investment mandate or engage in self-dealing without independent oversight.

IFSCA Framework: The 2022 and 2025 Amendments

For foreign capital, GIFT City is the most efficient jurisdiction. The IFSCA Fund Management Regulations 2022 (as amended) laid the groundwork for foreign investors to access Indian markets with a favorable tax regime (including a 10-year tax holiday on specified income and zero capital gains tax on certain transfers).

Crucially, the regulations stipulate strict skin-in-the-game requirements. *Provided that the contribution by the sponsor or manager* meets the minimum threshold—typically 2.5% of the fund size or ₹5 Crore, whichever is lower—ensuring alignment of interest.

However, compliance is not static. The IFSCA (Fund Management) Regulations, 2025 (“FM Regulations 2025”), notified on **February 19, 2025**, introduced tighter compliance frameworks for risk management and valuation of unlisted assets within IFSCA-domiciled funds. For foreign HNIs, this is a net positive: the 2025 regulations mandate independent valuation at least once a quarter for Category I and II AIFs, severely limiting a manager's ability to mark up illiquid portfolios artificially to trigger premature performance fees.

4. Performance Metrics: The Alpha Reality

Skepticism regarding manager skill is warranted. The Indian market is deep, but alpha is highly concentrated.

Recent industry data analyzing Category III AIF performance reveals a stark reality. Out of 75 funds analyzed, the average expense ratio stood at 0.93% at the portfolio level. However, the dispersion of returns is alarming: **55 out of 75 funds successfully beat the BSE 500 TRI benchmark**, while **6 funds delivered negative absolute returns**, destroying capital despite a broadly bullish macroeconomic environment.

**The TresoWealth Takeaway:** A 73.3% success rate (55 out of 75) against the BSE 500 TRI looks compelling, but the existence of 6 catastrophic underperformers proves that manager selection is not an afterthought—it is the entire game. Paying a 0.93% expense ratio plus a 20% performance fee for a manager who underperforms a passive index is an unacceptable wealth transfer.

When you compare PMS funds with mutual funds and AIFs, you must demand a minimum Information Ratio (IR) of 0.5. If the manager cannot generate 0.5 units of excess return per unit of tracking error, the alpha is statistically indistinguishable from noise, and the premium fee structure is unjustified.

5. Case Studies in Capital Deployment

Case Study 1: The $5M Allocation – PMS vs. AIF

*Profile: Singapore-based HNI seeking Indian mid-cap equity exposure.*

**Option A (PMS):** The investor deploys $5M into a PMS. The manager buys 25 mid-cap stocks. The investor holds the stocks in their own demat account.

*Result:* High transparency, but extreme tax drag. Every time the PMS manager churns the portfolio, the investor realizes a short-term capital gain (taxed at 20% in India). Furthermore, any dividends are taxed at the investor's slab rate.

**Option B (AIF - Category III):** The investor deploys $5M into a Cat III AIF. The manager buys the same 25 stocks but also employs Nifty Put options to hedge downside risk (a strategy illegal under PMS rules).

*Result:* The AIF is a pass-through vehicle for tax purposes. Portfolio churn does not trigger immediate tax events for the investor; taxes are only realized upon the investor redeeming from the AIF. The ability to hedge provides structural downside protection that PMS cannot match.

Case Study 2: The FoF Arbitrage via GIFT City

*Profile: UK-based Family Office allocating $10M across Indian Real Estate and Private Credit.*

Directly investing in a domestic Indian AIF exposes the family office to complex KYC norms, potential angel tax issues on unlisted shares, and a tangled web of repatriation rules.

Instead, the family office invests $10M into an IFSCA-domiciled FoF. This FoF acts as a feeder, routing capital into two domestic Category II AIFs (one real estate, one private credit).

*Result:* The investor deals with a single GIFT City entity (simplifying KYC and compliance), benefits from the IFSCA tax holidays, and enjoys streamlined repatriation. The cost is the double-layer fee, but the operational efficiency and tax alpha far outweigh the 50-100 bps of additional FoF fees.

6. Actionable FAQs for the Foreign HNI

**Q1: How involved do I want to be in the investment process?**

The choice between these vehicles ultimately dictates how involved you want to be. Investors in India can access different levels of market exposure, but delegation requires rigorous due diligence. If you require daily liquidity and direct stock ownership, PMS is your vehicle, but prepare for administrative overhead. If you want to write a check and let institutional managers execute complex, illiquid strategies over a 5-7 year lock-in, AIFs are the only viable option.

**Q2: What is the most critical risk in AIFs today?**

Liquidity mismatch. Many Category II AIFs hold unlisted assets that cannot be sold quickly. If a fund offers quarterly redemptions but holds 60% unlisted equity, the redemption terms are a fiction. Always cross-reference the liquidity terms with the underlying asset liquidity.

**Q3: How do I verify if a manager's performance is legitimate?**

Demand the audited track record, not the marketing fact sheet. Under the IFSCA FM Regulations 2025 (effective February 19, 2025), independent valuation is mandated. Ensure the returns are calculated using the Modified Dietz method or TWRR, and verify that performance fees are calculated High-Water Mark (HWM), not on a hurdle rate that resets annually.

**Q4: Is the 0.93% average expense ratio justified?**

It depends on the net return. If the gross return is 18% and the net return is 14% after the 0.93% expense ratio and a 20% performance fee, you have lost 400 bps to fees. The expense ratio is merely the floor of your costs; the performance fee is the ceiling. Negotiate hard on the HWM and ensure the manager pays for organizational expenses, not the LPs.

**Q5: Can I use leverage in these structures?**

Only Category III AIFs can employ leverage. PMS and Category I/II AIFs are strictly prohibited from borrowing. If your mandate requires capital efficiency through leverage, you must use a Cat III AIF.
