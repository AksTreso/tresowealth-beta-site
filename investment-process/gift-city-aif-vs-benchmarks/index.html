# GIFT City AIFs vs. Domestic Benchmarks: The Structural Reality Behind the Offshore Hype **As of April 2026**, the Indian alternative investment landscape operates in a state of structural bifurcation. On one side lies the deeply entrenched domestic onshore market, governed by SEBI’s established frameworks and denominated in INR. On the other stands the Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City)—India’s audacious attempt to capture offshore capital, offering USD-denominated structures and regulatory arbitrage. For investment professionals, the narrative surrounding GIFT City Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) has long been dominated by promotional rhetoric. However, a precise examination of the data, benchmarked against the domestic onshore market, reveals a more sobering reality. This analysis dissects the empirical disparities between GIFT City AIFs and their domestic benchmarks, evaluates the regulatory architecture driving offshore capital, and provides actionable intelligence for allocators navigating this dichotomy. > **Key Metrics (2026):** > - 349 AIFs launched in GIFT City IFSC > - $22.11 billion assets under management > - 0% tax on capital gains and dividends (Section 10(4E)) > - 48-hour repatriation facility > - USD-denominated investment structures > ## The Scale Discrepancy: Empirical Reality vs. Aspirational Narratives The most glaring distinction between GIFT City and the domestic onshore market is the sheer magnitude of capital deployment. While GIFT City is frequently championed as the future of Indian offshore finance, the current numbers expose a severe velocity deficit when benchmarked against the domestic ecosystem. ### Market Size vs. Total Market Share As of Q1 2026, the total Assets Under Management (AUM) for GIFT City AIFs stands at approximately **$15 billion**. This figure is routinely celebrated in policy circles as evidence of the IFSC’s maturation. However, precision requires context. The domestic onshore AIF market currently commands a staggering **$164 billion**. When benchmarked directly, GIFT City represents a mere **9.1%** of the total Indian alternative investment market share. This is not a parallel market; it is a peripheral one. The $149 billion chasm between the offshore and onshore markets underscores a critical reality: despite tax incentives and USD-denominated fund structures, global institutional capital still overwhelmingly prefers the liquidity, legal precedence, and operational maturity of the domestic INR route. ### Market Size & Dry Powder: The Deployment Friction The discrepancy becomes even more pronounced when evaluating committed capital and dry powder. When factoring in total commitments, GIFT City scales to **$22 billion**, while the domestic onshore benchmark balloons to **$260 billion**. Here, the critical metric for investment professionals is not the headline commitment number, but the ratio of deployed capital to uncalled capital. In GIFT City, the uncalled capital (dry powder) sits at roughly 30% of committed offshore capital, indicating that approximately **$6.6 billion** of committed offshore capital is yet to be deployed. A 30% dry powder ratio in a nascent jurisdiction is not inherently anomalous, but it warrants a skeptical interpretation. In the domestic market, a 30% dry powder ratio typically signals a healthy pipeline of upcoming deployments. In GIFT City, it often signals deployment friction. Allocators must question whether this $6.6 billion is strategic optionality waiting for the right entry point, or capital trapped by operational bottlenecks, limited high-quality USD-denominated deal flow, and the structural complexities of deploying offshore capital into onshore assets. ## The Regulatory Architecture: Section 10(4D) and the Benchmark Asymmetry To understand why GIFT City exists in its current form, one must analyze the regulatory incentives—specifically the tax architecture that makes the jurisdiction viable. The cornerstone of the GIFT City AIF value proposition is tax neutrality, formalized by the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT). ### CBDT Notification No. 89/2020 The pivotal regulatory instrument is **Notification No. 89/2020 / S.O. 2853(E)**, issued under **Section 10(4D) of the Income-tax Act, 1961**. This notification grants a blanket tax exemption to specified funds established in GIFT City. Specifically, it exempts the income of these funds from taxation, effectively providing a pass-through status that neutralizes the double-taxation risks that historically deterred offshore LPs from Indian alternatives. However, the true sophistication of Section 10(4D) lies in an often-overlooked structural nuance: it allows offshore funds to benchmark against international risk-free rates **without mandating a domestic risk-free rate component**. ### Implications for Hurdle Rates and Benchmarking In the domestic onshore market, AIFs are inherently tied to the Indian macroeconomic environment. Hurdle rates and benchmarks are typically pegged to the Indian 10-year G-Sec yield plus a risk premium, reflecting INR volatility and domestic inflation. As of April 2026, with domestic risk-free rates hovering at elevated levels, onshore AIFs must generate substantial alpha just to clear the hurdle rate. Conversely, GIFT City AIFs leveraging Section 10(4D) can structure their hurdle rates against US Treasuries or SOFR. This creates a profound benchmark asymmetry. A GIFT City fund can target a 10% IRR benchmarked against a 4.5% US Treasury yield, while a domestic fund might require a 14% IRR to clear a 7.5% Indian G-Sec hurdle. For the offshore LP, this is an unambiguous advantage: the required alpha is lower, the currency risk is mitigated (as the fund is USD-denominated), and the tax drag is eliminated. But for the allocator, this asymmetry demands skepticism. Does the lower hurdle rate reflect genuine structural efficiency, or does it obscure the operational costs and illiquidity premiums inherent in routing capital through a developing jurisdiction? ## Structural Constraints: Apples, Oranges, and Operational Friction Benchmarking GIFT City AIFs against domestic onshore AIFs is an exercise in comparing fundamentally different structures. The operational reality of the IFSC introduces variables that domestic benchmarks do not account for. ### 1. The Repatriation and Routing Paradox GIFT City AIFs are designed to capture foreign capital. Yet, the underlying assets these funds invest in are predominantly Indian companies. This creates a structural paradox: USD-denominated capital must be converted to INR to fund onshore operations, and the subsequent exits must be converted back to USD for repatriation. While GIFT City offers liberalized remittance schedules, the FX conversion friction is a hidden cost that domestic AIFs do not bear. If a GIFT City fund generates a 15% IRR in INR terms, but the INR depreciates against the USD by 3% during the fund's life, the net USD IRR drops to roughly 12%. The Section 10(4D) tax exemption protects against tax drag, but it does not protect against currency drag. Allocators must independently model FX scenarios rather than relying on the fund manager’s pro-forma IRR. ### 2. Legal Precedent and Enforcement Risk Domestic AIFs operate within the well-trodden legal framework of SEBI and the Indian judicial system. While the Indian legal system is notoriously slow, it is highly predictable. GIFT City operates under the IFSC Authority, with disputes routed through the Gujarat International Financial Services Centre Court. While this court is designed for expediency, it lacks the decades of legal precedent that underpin domestic AIF enforcement. In the event of a sponsor default, a portfolio company breach, or an LP-GP dispute, the predictability of outcomes in the IFSC Court remains an untested variable. This jurisdictional risk is rarely priced into the management fees of GIFT City AIFs, yet it represents a tangible threat to capital protection. ### 3. The Liquidity Discount Domestic onshore AIFs, despite their illiquid nature, benefit from a secondary market ecosystem that is slowly maturing. GIFT City AIFs, denominated in USD and structured for offshore LPs, suffer from a severe secondary liquidity drought. The $15 billion AUM is spread across a limited number of funds, and the LP base is concentrated among a small cohort of global institutional investors. Finding a buyer for a GIFT City AIF stake on the secondary market in 2026 is significantly more difficult than for a domestic SEBI-registered AIF, necessitating a higher illiquidity premium that benchmarks often fail to capture. ## Actionable Insights for Investment Professionals For allocators evaluating the GIFT City opportunity set, the data dictates a disciplined, protective approach. **1. Demand Deployment Velocity Metrics on the $6.6B Dry Powder** Do not accept committed capital as a proxy for success. With $6.6 billion in uncalled offshore capital, allocators must pressure GIFT City GPs on deployment velocity. Require granular data on the pipeline-to-deployment conversion rate. If a GP has raised commitments but is sitting on uncalled capital for over 12–18 months, it indicates a lack of bankable USD-denominated deal flow. Capital sitting idle in a tax-advantaged structure is not capital working for you; it is capital eroding your net returns due to FX timing risks and management fees on undeployed capital. **2. Exploit the Benchmark Asymmetry in Negotiations** Because Section 10(4D) does not mandate a domestic risk-free rate component, GIFT City funds have the flexibility to benchmark against USD rates (e.g., SOFR + 400 bps). This should theoretically result in a lower hurdle rate compared to domestic funds benchmarked against high-yield INR G-Secs. If a GIFT City GP attempts to set a hurdle rate equivalent to a domestic INR benchmark, they are double-dipping—capturing the tax advantage of Section 10(4D) while retaining the high domestic hurdle rate. Allocators must negotiate hurdle rates strictly pegged to USD risk-free rates, ensuring the tax arbitrage benefits the LP, not just the GP’s carry calculation. **3. Stress-Test the FX Hedging Strategy** GIFT City AIFs inherently carry INR/USD mismatch. Allocators must demand transparency on how GPs manage this. Does the GP utilize NDF (Non-Deliverable Forward) contracts to hedge the FX risk? If so, what is the cost of carry, and how does it impact the net distributed yield? If the GP does not hedge, the fund is implicitly making a directional FX bet, which is rarely the mandate of an alternative investment fund. **4. Isolate Regulatory Arbitrage from Manager Alpha** When evaluating GIFT City AIF performance against domestic benchmarks, strip out the tax alpha. A GIFT City fund may outperform a domestic equivalent simply due to the Section 10(4D) exemption, not because of superior deal sourcing or operational value-add. Allocate only to GIFT City GPs who demonstrate genuine alpha generation—such as access to Indian startups that specifically require USD capital, or cross-border structuring expertise—rather than those merely repackaging onshore deals through an offshore tax wrapper. ## Conclusion As of April 2026, the GIFT City AIF ecosystem is a structurally significant but empirically small fraction of the Indian alternative investment landscape. The $15 billion AUM versus the $164 billion domestic benchmark illustrates that offshore capital has not yet dethroned the onshore market. The $22 billion versus $260 billion total commitment comparison, highlighted by the $6.6 billion in uncalled dry powder, suggests that capital commitment is outpacing the operational capacity to deploy it efficiently. The regulatory architecture—specifically CBDT Notification No. 89/2020 under Section 10(4D)—provides a legitimate and powerful tax-neutral vehicle. The exemption from domestic risk-free rate benchmarking offers a distinct structural advantage for USD-denominated capital. However, tax exemption is not a substitute for operational maturity, FX risk management, or legal precedent. For the discerning allocator, GIFT City is not a replacement for the domestic onshore market; it is a specialized tool. It is highly effective for USD-denominated investors seeking tax-efficient exposure to Indian assets, provided the allocator rigorously discounts for the FX friction, demands appropriate USD-based hurdle rates, and remains highly skeptical of undeployed dry powder. In the pursuit of offshore yield, capital protection requires looking past the regulatory hype and demanding the same, if not higher, operational rigor that the domestic onshore market has spent decades refining.
