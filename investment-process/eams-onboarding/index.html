# Navigating GIFT City AIF Onboarding for External Asset Managers: A Structured Audit of Risk and Regulation As of April 2026, the Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) has firmly established itself as the primary conduit for global capital seeking Indian exposure, and increasingly, for Indian capital seeking global diversification. For External Asset Managers (EAMs), the International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) in GIFT City presents a compelling, yet highly nuanced, jurisdiction for Alternative Investment Fund (AIF) deployment. However, the narrative surrounding GIFT City often suffers from institutional hyperbole. While the regulatory arbitrage and tax incentives are real, the onboarding process for EAMs is fraught with structural friction, compliance tripwires, and operational latency. At TresoWealth, our mandate is to look past the macroeconomic sales pitch and audit the microeconomic reality. This article provides a precise, structured, and skeptical examination of the GIFT City AIF onboarding landscape for EAMs, dissecting the macro statistics, decoding the regulatory architecture, and delivering actionable insights to protect client capital and operational integrity. --- > **Key Metrics (2026):** > - 349 AIFs launched in GIFT City IFSC > - $22.11 billion assets under management > - 0% tax on capital gains and dividends (Section 10(4E)) > - 48-hour repatriation facility > - USD-denominated investment structures > ## The Macro Context: Separating Projections from Reality To understand the current onboarding environment, EAMs must first ground themselves in the empirical data defining GIFT City’s AIF ecosystem. The trajectory of assets under management (AUM) and proposed capital commitments reveals a maturing market, but one that has not kept pace with the most aggressive institutional forecasts. Consider the following data points covering the 2024-2026 period: * **$12.8 Billion:** The total assets under management (AUM) within GIFT City AIFs across the 2024-2026 period. This figure represents actual deployed and live capital, demonstrating solid organic growth and successful capital calls by established managers. * **$2.5 Billion:** The total proposed corpus pending registration and deployment as of October 2024. This pipeline indicates sustained interest, but also highlights the latency between regulatory application and live operations—a critical friction point EAMs must anticipate. * **$25 Billion:** The projected total AUM benchmark established by industry projections as of June 2024. **The Skeptical Audit:** Viewed from April 2026, the delta between the $25 billion projection and the $12.8 billion reality is stark. The market has delivered a 48.8% shortfall against bullish forecasts. For EAMs, this is not necessarily a negative indicator; rather, it is a vital recalibration. It suggests that regulatory friction and operational bottlenecks have naturally capped the velocity of capital inflows. EAMs should approach GIFT City with the understanding that onboarding is a marathon, not a sprint, and unrealistic timelines will result in capital commitment failures. --- ## The Regulatory Architecture: The $10M Threshold and Section 10(4D) The strategic value of GIFT City hinges on its dual-layered regulatory arbitrage: operational flexibility via the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) and tax efficiency via the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT). EAMs must structure their onboarding to satisfy both simultaneously. ### The $10 Million Minimum Corpus Advantage Under IFSCA regulations, a foreign investor or EAM can establish an AIF in GIFT City with a minimum corpus of **$10 million**. This is a deliberate regulatory design to lower the barrier to entry compared to onshore Indian AIFs, which face higher capital thresholds and more stringent investment restrictions. Crucially, this $10 million threshold allows for onboarding with fewer regulatory restrictions. EAMs leveraging this structure benefit from: 1. **Relaxed Sponsor/Manager Qualifications:** IFSCA allows greater flexibility in the track record requirements for the investment manager, enabling first-time fund managers or offshore EAMs without an extensive Indian track record to register. 2. **Broader Investment Mandates:** Subject to the fund's private placement memorandum, these funds can deploy capital across a wider spectrum of global and Indian securities without the granular concentration limits that constrain onshore Category I and II AIFs. *Protective Insight:* The "fewer restrictions" clause is often misinterpreted as "light-touch regulation." It is not. IFSCA maintains rigorous reporting and substance requirements. The $10 million threshold is an entry point, not a shield against regulatory scrutiny. EAMs must maintain strict compliance to prevent enforcement actions that can freeze redemptions. ### The Tax Exemption Trap: Section 10(4D) and Notification No. 86/2020 The crown jewel of the GIFT City AIF structure is the tax exemption on specified income. However, this is where EAMs most frequently stumble. Under the **Income Tax Act, 1961 - Section 10(4D)**, read with **CBDT Notification No. 86/2020/Income Tax**, specified income earned by a Category III AIF (the most common structure for EAMs executing global or multi-asset strategies) in the IFSC is exempt from income tax, provided certain conditions are met. **The Critical Caveat:** This tax exemption on specified income is **breached if the AIF invests back into onshore India.** This creates a profound structural paradox that EAMs must internalize: * If the GIFT City AIF invests in offshore securities (global equities, US treasuries, etc.), the income is tax-exempt. * If the GIFT City AIF routes capital *back* into onshore India (e.g., buying Indian equities on the NSE/BSE via the FPI route), the tax exemption under Section 10(4D) is voided, and the AIF becomes liable to pay tax on that income as per standard domestic rates. *Protective Insight:* Many EAMs erroneously assume that GIFT City is simply a tax-free wrapper for Indian equity exposure. It is not. If your mandate involves onshore Indian equities, the Section 10(4D) exemption offers no protection. EAMs must firewall their GIFT City AIF mandates to purely offshore or IFSC-listed securities to preserve the tax integrity of the vehicle. Mixing onshore and offshore mandates within a single GIFT City AIF is a compliance death trap. --- ## The EAM Onboarding Framework: A Structured Approach Transitioning from regulatory theory to operational reality, EAMs must navigate a multi-phased onboarding process. The timeline from initial application to first capital call typically spans 4 to 6 months, assuming flawless documentation. ### Phase 1: Entity Structuring and IFSCA Registration The EAM must first establish a presence in the IFSC. This typically involves incorporating a company or setting up a branch office, followed by applying to IFSCA for registration as a Fund Management Entity (FME). Key friction points include: * **Substance Requirements:** IFSCA mandates physical substance. The EAM must have a physical office in GIFT City and at least one key investment professional residing in India. "Brass plate" structures are systematically rejected. * **Fit and Proper Test:** IFSCA conducts rigorous background checks on directors, partners, and beneficial owners. Any historical regulatory censure, even minor, will delay the process. ### Phase 2: Fund Setup and Compliance Architecture Once the FME license is secured, the EAM files the Fund Offering Document (Private Placement Memorandum) with IFSCA. * **The $10 Million Commitment:** The fund must demonstrate a firm commitment of $10 million before or at the time of the final closing. EAMs cannot launch a GIFT City AIF on a "rolling close" basis without clear visibility on this threshold. * **KYC/AML Protocols:** While IFSCA follows global FATF standards, the implementation by Indian banking partners in GIFT City often mirrors the extreme conservatism of onshore Indian banks. EAMs must prepare for invasive source-of-wealth documentation requirements from Limited Partners (LPs), particularly those domiciled in jurisdictions not on the FATF white-list. ### Phase 3: Banking and Custody Integration This is arguably the most operationally frustrating phase of GIFT City onboarding. Despite being an IFSC, the banking infrastructure is still governed by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) and executed through Indian banks with limited experience in complex offshore fund structures. * **Banking Latency:** Opening an IFSC Banking Unit (IBU) account can take 8 to 12 weeks. EAMs must initiate banking relationships concurrently with the IFSCA application to avoid post-approval capital deployment delays. * **Custodial Limitations:** The choice of custodians in GIFT City is expanding but remains limited compared to Cayman or Luxembourg. EAMs must ensure their custodian can seamlessly settle both global securities and IFSC-listed products without requiring manual workarounds. --- ## The Skeptical Audit: Friction Points and Hidden Risks TresoWealth’s mandate is to protect capital by identifying operational and regulatory risks that standard due diligence often overlooks. In the context of GIFT City AIF onboarding, EAMs must be vigilant regarding the following: #### 1. The Illusion of "Fewer Restrictions" While the $10 million threshold grants operational leeway, IFSCA is increasingly tightening the screws on compliance. In late 2025, IFSCA introduced enhanced periodic reporting requirements for FMEs, demanding granular data on portfolio concentration, liquidity risk, and counterparty exposures. EAMs used to the relatively opaque regulatory environments of the BVI or Cayman Islands will find IFSCA’s reporting requirements uncomfortably prescriptive. Failure to file these reports on time results in immediate financial penalties and potential license suspension. #### 2. The Repatriation Bottleneck While GIFT City theoretically allows free repatriation of capital, the practical reality involves navigating foreign exchange management rules. If an LP requests a redemption, the EAM must ensure that the banking partner processes the outward remittance without imposing ad-hoc documentary requirements. We have observed instances where Indian banking partners have delayed redemptions by weeks due to internal compliance paranoia regarding capital flight. EAMs must secure pre-approved remittance protocols with their IBU before accepting LP capital. #### 3. The Section 10(4D) Clawback Risk Because the Section 10(4D) exemption is breached by onshore investment, EAMs must implement hard compliance firewalls within their order management systems (OMS). An accidental execution of an onshore Indian derivative or equity, even for a brief hedging purpose, can trigger a tax liability for the entire fund. This is not merely a tax issue; it is a fiduciary breach. EAMs must mandate that their compliance software automatically blocks any trade executed on an onshore Indian exchange. #### 4. Talent Arbitrage and Key-Person Risk The requirement for physical substance means EAMs must hire investment professionals in GIFT City. However, the talent pool in Gujarat, while growing, does not yet match the depth of Mumbai, Singapore, or Hong Kong for specialized alternative asset strategies. EAMs face the risk of overpaying for imported talent or underperforming with local hires. Furthermore, the loss of the single resident key investment professional can jeopardize the FME license, creating severe key-person risk. --- ## Actionable Insights for EAMs To navigate the GIFT City onboarding landscape successfully, EAMs must adopt a proactive, structurally defensive posture. Based on the current April 2026 regulatory and operational environment, TresoWealth advises the following: 1. **Treat the $10 Million as a Hard Floor, Not a Target:** Do not attempt to launch a GIFT City AIF with only $10 million in soft circled commitments. The fixed costs of compliance, substance, and administration require a minimum viable AUM of $15–$20 million to achieve operational breakeven without eroding LP returns through excessive management fees. Launching with the bare minimum creates structural fragility. 2. **Firewall the Investment Mandate:** If your strategy requires even a 5% allocation to onshore Indian equities, do not use a GIFT City AIF. The Section 10(4D) breach risk under CBDT Notification No. 86/2020/Income Tax is absolute. Establish a separate onshore AIF for Indian exposure and use the GIFT City vehicle exclusively for offshore and global mandates. The administrative cost of two entities is vastly cheaper than a tax clawback. 3. **Pre-Clear Banking Operations Before IFSCA Application:** Do not wait for FME approval to approach an IBU. Engage legal counsel to draft standardized repatriation and subscription documentation with your chosen banking partner in advance. Secure written confirmation from the bank’s compliance team regarding their specific documentation requirements for outward remittances. 4. **Over-Engineer the Substance Requirement:** IFSCA is actively scrutinizing "shell" FMEs. Ensure your GIFT City office is not merely a conference room. The key investment professional must have demonstrable decision-making authority and a clear audit trail of portfolio management activities conducted from the IFSC. Implement detailed investment committee minutes that explicitly record the GIFT City location of the decision-makers. 5. **Audit Your Custodian's Global Settlement Capabilities:** Before signing a custodian, stress-test their systems. Can they settle a corporate action on a European small-cap stock seamlessly? Can they manage multi-currency cash sweeps? The custodial infrastructure in GIFT City is still maturing, and operational failures in settlement can trigger breach of covenant clauses in your LP agreements. --- ## Conclusion The GIFT City AIF ecosystem, with its $12.8 billion in realized AUM and a $2.5 billion pending pipeline, represents a legitimate and valuable jurisdictional tool for External Asset Managers. The $10 million minimum corpus and the tax exemptions under Section 10(4D) offer undeniable advantages over onshore structures. However, the gap between the $25 billion projections and the $12.8 billion reality is a testament to the friction inherent in the system. GIFT City is not a regulatory void; it is a highly engineered financial zone where the rules are different, but strictly enforced. For EAMs, successful onboarding requires abandoning the assumption that GIFT City is merely "India with a tax break." It is a distinct jurisdiction that demands structural precision, a skeptical approach to operational timelines, and an uncompromising firewall against onshore investment breaches. By respecting the friction and auditing the risks, EAMs can protect their LPs' capital and effectively harness the architecture of GIFT City.
