# Mainland vs. GIFT City AIFs: A Structural and Regulatory Comparison for Capital Allocators **Date: April 2026** As of April 2026, the Indian alternative investment landscape is fundamentally bifurcated. The dichotomy between the traditional onshore Mainland framework and the Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) presents investment professionals with a strategic choice that extends far beyond mere geography. It is a decision weighted by regulatory friction, tax arbitrage, capital mobility, and operational scalability. The scale of this divergence is quantifiable. Mainland Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) command an estimated Assets Under Management (AUM) of ₹11.15 lakh crore (approximately $133 billion), dwarfing the $10.5 billion currently domiciled in GIFT City. However, looking at the projected market size for 2026, the trajectories are both expanding but structurally distinct: Mainland AIFs are projected to reach a total AUM of ~$215 billion, while GIFT City AIFs are projected to scale to ~$25 billion. For General Partners (GPs) and Limited Partners (LPs), the decision of where to domicile a fund is no longer default-driven. This analysis deconstructs the regulatory architecture, tax regimes, and operational realities of both jurisdictions to provide a rigorous framework for capital allocation. --- > **Key Metrics (2026):** > - 349 AIFs launched in GIFT City IFSC > - $22.11 billion assets under management > - 0% tax on capital gains and dividends (Section 10(4E)) > - 48-hour repatriation facility > - USD-denominated investment structures > ## 1. The Scale of Capital: A Tale of Two Markets To understand the structural dynamics, one must first acknowledge the sheer disparity in capital depth. The Mainland AIF ecosystem, regulated by the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI), is a mature, deep-volume market. At $133 billion in current AUM, it represents the bedrock of Indian alternative capital. The projected 2026 market size of ~$215 billion indicates a robust, albeit decelerating, compound annual growth rate driven by domestic institutional capital, family offices, and high-net-worth individuals (HNIs). Conversely, the GIFT City AIF framework, regulated by the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA), remains an emergent architecture. With a current AUM of $10.5 billion, it constitutes less than 8% of the Mainland’s asset base. However, the projected 2026 AUM of ~$25 billion represents a near 2.5x scaling over a short horizon. This growth is not driven by domestic retail or HNI capital, but by targeted foreign direct investment (FDI), diaspora capital, and outbound strategies requiring USD-denominated structures. **The TresoWealth Insight:** The $215 billion Mainland projection is built on compounding domestic inertia. The $25 billion GIFT City projection is built on regulatory arbitrage and policy mandates. Capital allocators must recognize that GIFT City's growth is highly dependent on the continuation of preferential tax treatments and foreign exchange liberties; it is a policy-derivative market. --- ## 2. Regulatory Architecture and Capital Mobility The most acute divergence between Mainland and GIFT City AIFs lies in the regulatory architecture governing capital mobility, specifically regarding overseas investments and downstream deployments. ### The Mainland Constraint: RBI Circular No. 13 For Mainland AIFs, overseas investment is tightly constrained by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI). Under **RBI/2023-24/115 A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 13**, the framework for Overseas Investment by AIFs and downstream investments was significantly overhauled. While Circular No. 13 consolidated prior regulations, it maintained a skeptical stance on outward capital mobility. Mainland AIFs (specifically Category I and II) are permitted to make overseas investments subject to stringent conditions, including: * An aggregate ceiling of 25% of the corpus of the AIF for overseas investments. * Restrictions on the utilization of leverage or borrowed funds for overseas deployment. * Mandatory reporting and pre-transaction compliance friction that delays capital deployment. For GPs seeking global portfolio diversification or Indian tech companies with substantial offshore operations, the Mainland framework acts as a structural impediment. Circular No. 13 ensures that domestic capital remains largely domestic-bound, prioritizing onshore economic activity over optimal portfolio construction. ### The GIFT City Liberty: IFSCA Seamless Outbound In stark contrast, the IFSCA framework was designed as a conduit for two-way capital flow. A GIFT City AIF operates in a foreign currency (typically USD) and is treated as a foreign entity for exchange control purposes. Consequently, the 25% corpus ceiling imposed by Circular No. 13 does not apply. A GIFT City AIF can deploy 100% of its corpus into overseas assets, or seamlessly invest back into the Mainland through the FDI route. This "Outbound-First, Inbound-Optional" architecture makes GIFT City the only viable domicile for managers running global multi-asset strategies or India-centric strategies that require offshore holding company structures (common in tech and IP-driven ventures). **The TresoWealth Insight:** Do not underestimate the operational drag of Circular No. 13. If your thesis requires agile cross-border capital calls or the acquisition of offshore IP, the Mainland structure will introduce a minimum of 30-45 days of regulatory friction per transaction. GIFT City eliminates this drag, but introduces banking and administrative friction of a different nature, which we will explore below. --- ## 3. Taxation and Safe Harbor Regimes Tax arbitrage remains the primary catalyst for GIFT City fund domiciliation. The Indian tax code explicitly bifurcates the treatment of onshore and IFSC-based vehicles. ### The Mainland Reality: Section 9A and Notification No. 93/2019 To make India a viable hub for fund management, the government introduced Section 9A of the Income Tax Act, 1961, which provides a "safe harbor" to offshore funds managed from India. Read with **CBDT Notification No. 93/2019/F.No.370142/28/2019-TPL**, this framework stipulates that if an offshore fund meets specific conditions, the mere fact that the fund is managed by an Indian resident manager will not trigger a Place of Effective Management (POEM) issue, thereby preventing the offshore fund from being taxed as an Indian resident. However, Section 9A is a regime of exceptions. The conditions are draconian: * The fund must have a minimum corpus of $25 million. * No single investor can hold more than 10% of the fund (with limited exceptions). * The remuneration paid to the Indian manager cannot exceed 5% of the total remuneration paid to all managers globally. If an AIF fails even one of the 13 conditions under Section 9A, the entire fund risks being classified as a tax resident of India, subjecting its global income to Indian tax rates. Notification 93/2019 attempted to ease some of these constraints for certain sovereign and pension funds, but for private GPs, Section 9A remains a high-wire act. ### The GIFT City Advantage: Section 10(4D) and Notification No. 74/2020 GIFT City operates on a fundamentally different premise. Under Section 10(4D) of the Income Tax Act, 1961, read with **CBDT Notification No. 74/2020/F.No.370142/24/2020-TPL**, a specified income earned by an AIF located in the IFSC is granted a 100% tax exemption, provided the fund is compliant with IFSCA regulations. This exemption covers capital gains, dividend income, and interest income arising from specified investments. For a GIFT City Category I or II AIF, this translates to a 10-year tax holiday (expiring in March 2027, though industry consensus anticipates an extension given the 2026 budget cycle). Furthermore, the strict ownership and remuneration constraints of Section 9A are entirely absent. A GIFT City fund can have a single LP holding 100% of the corpus, and the Indian GP can charge standard management fees and carry without threatening the fund’s tax status. **The TresoWealth Insight:** The tax differential is stark but requires skeptical evaluation. The 10-year tax holiday under Section 10(4D) is a sunset clause. For a fund with a 7+2+1 year life, the exemption may expire before the final harvest. Allocators must model the terminal tax drag if the sunset clause is not extended by the government post-2027. --- ## 4. Operational Realities and Structural Nuances Beyond the macro-statistics and regulatory texts, the operational realities of setting up and running an AIF dictate the ultimate viability of the structure. ### Setup Costs and Timeframes * **Mainland:** Setting up a SEBI AIF is a commoditized process. Legal fees range from $20,000 to $40,000. The regulatory approval process via the SEBI Intermediary Portal is streamlined, with registration typically secured within 60-90 days. Fund administration, compliance, and auditing ecosystems are deep, competitive, and cost-efficient. * **GIFT City:** The IFSCA ecosystem is nascent and expensive. Legal and structuring fees frequently exceed $75,000 due to the necessity of dual-compliance (IFSCA plus global standards). Setup timelines can stretch to 4-6 months. Furthermore, the requirement for a physical presence in GIFT City—mandating office space in a high-rent commercial zone and the employment of local staff—adds a fixed overhead that makes micro-funds (sub-$30 million) economically unviable. ### Banking and Currency Friction A GIFT City AIF is a USD-denominated entity. For foreign LPs, this is a feature, eliminating the currency hedging costs associated with INR-denominated Mainland funds (which can erode 3-5% of IRR annually). However, for domestic LPs investing in a GIFT City fund, the process is operationally cumbersome. The remittance of INR to GIFT City must be done under the Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS), subject to a $250,000 per individual per year limit, and is converted to USD at the time of deployment. This introduces FX conversion costs and restricts the ability of domestic HNIs to make large, single-ticket commitments. ### Investor Appetite and Market Depth The Mainland ecosystem is supported by deep domestic capital—insurance companies, provident funds, and large family offices. These entities are accustomed to SEBI’s regulatory cadence and INR-denominated returns. GIFT City, conversely, is heavily reliant on Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs) and global institutional capital. While the IFSCA framework is explicitly designed to attract this capital, global LPs often remain skeptical of the Indian judicial system's reach into GIFT City and the lack of established precedent in IFSCA dispute resolution. **The TresoWealth Insight:** The fixed-cost infrastructure of GIFT City acts as a natural filter. If your fund is raising less than $50 million, the compliance and operational overhead will materially impact the net IRR. GIFT City is optimal for established managers with a proven track record raising $100M+ vehicles targeting global LPs. --- ## 5. Actionable Insights for Investment Professionals As the AUM scales toward the projected $215 billion (Mainland) and $25 billion (GIFT City) marks by the end of 2026, allocators must adopt a highly structured approach to fund domicile selection. 1. **Let the Capital Source Dictate the Structure:** If your LP base is >70% domestic (Indian HNIs, family offices, domestic institutions), the Mainland AIF is the default choice. The LRS constraints and FX friction make GIFT City unappealing to domestic capital. If your LP base is >70% global institutional capital, GIFT City is mandatory to provide USD-denominated returns and tax certainty. 2. **Outbound Mandates Require IFSCA:** For venture capital or private equity strategies that require the acquisition of offshore IP, setting up holding companies, or global portfolio hedges, do not attempt to navigate the RBI’s Circular No. 13 constraints. The 25% overseas limit will suffocate the thesis. Domicile in GIFT City. 3. **Scrutinize the Section 9A Compliance:** For offshore feeder structures managed from India, conduct an independent audit of the fund’s Section 9A compliance quarterly. A single breach in investor concentration or remuneration thresholds under Notification 93/2019 can trigger catastrophic tax liabilities for your LPs. 4. **Model the Tax Sunset Risk:** When evaluating GIFT City AIFs, do not underwrite a 0% tax rate in perpetuity. Model the exit IRR assuming the Section 10(4D) exemption lapses in 2027 and the fund is subject to the standard IFSC tax rate (which is still concessional at 9%, but not zero). This protects the downside if policy momentum shifts. ## Conclusion The Mainland and GIFT City AIF frameworks are not competitors; they are complementary infrastructures designed for entirely different capital flows. The Mainland, at $133 billion, is the undisputed engine for domestic capital formation, constrained by conservative capital controls but buoyed by operational maturity. GIFT City, scaling toward $25 billion, is an offshore conduit, leveraging the tax exemptions of Section 10(4D) and the freedom from Circular No. 13 to bridge global capital with Indian and outbound ambitions. For the investment professional, the choice of domicile is a risk-management decision. It requires a skeptical evaluation of regulatory permanence, a precise calculation of operational drag, and a structured alignment of the fund's investment thesis with the realities of capital mobility. In 2026, there is no universal right answer—only the right architecture for the specific capital at hand.
