# GIFT City AIF Fees & Costs Explained: A Critical Deconstruction for 2026 As of April 2026, the Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) remains India’s most ambitious regulatory arbitrage experiment. For Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs), the International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) offers a seemingly irresistible proposition: a tax-efficient, dollar-denominated sandbox with streamlined access to global capital. However, beneath the headline-grabbing tax exemptions lies a complex, often opaque fee structure that can rapidly erode the arbitrage advantage if not meticulously managed. At TresoWealth, our approach to jurisdictional analysis is rooted in skepticism and structural precision. The promise of GIFT City is only as strong as the math validating it. This article deconstructs the explicit, implicit, and friction costs of establishing and operating a GIFT City AIF in 2026, evaluating whether the regulatory subsidies justify the operational overhead. > **Key Metrics (2026):** > - 349 AIFs launched in GIFT City IFSC > - $22.11 billion assets under management > - 0% tax on capital gains and dividends (Section 10(4E)) > - 48-hour repatriation facility > - USD-denominated investment structures > ## The Macro Context: Capital Projections vs. Ground Reality To understand the fee ecosystem, one must first understand the scale of capital deployment. The trajectory of GIFT City AIFs has been aggressive, though the gap between projections and realized capital warrants close monitoring: * **$4.6 billion (INR 38,596 crore):** The actual baseline of AIF commitments established as of Q1 2024. This figure represents the hard capital that navigated the initial regulatory friction of the IFSC. * **$14.7 billion (INR 1.22 lakh crore):** The projected 2025-2026 standard for AIF commitments, reflecting the accelerated interest following regulatory clarifications and the maturation of the fund administration ecosystem. * **$25 billion – $30 billion:** The broader 2024-2025 estimates for the projected total AIF ecosystem capacity, encompassing both committed capital and the dry powder mandated by regulatory thresholds for fund viability. While the leap from $4.6 billion to a projected $25 billion–$30 billion ecosystem suggests a seamless scaling, the reality is that as AUM increases, the fixed costs of compliance scale non-linearly. The fees and costs outlined below dictate whether a fund operating within the $14.7 billion projection actually captures alpha, or merely subsidizes the jurisdiction’s infrastructure. --- ## 1. Setup and Registration Costs: The Price of Entry Establishing an AIF in GIFT City requires navigating the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) framework, which demands a higher capitalization and structural rigor than its onshore SEBI counterpart. ### IFSCA Registration Fees Unlike domestic AIFs, GIFT City funds must apply to the IFSCA. The application fee is typically **$1,000**, with a registration fee of **$5,000** upon approval. While these statutory fees are negligible, the legal and advisory fees required to draft the Scheme Offer Document, compliance manuals, and structural frameworks for an IFSCA-compliant fund generally range from **$50,000 to $120,000**, depending on the fund's complexity (CAT I, II, or III) and cross-border structuring requirements. ### Minimum Capital and Commitment Thresholds The IFSCA mandates minimum capital thresholds that inherently price out sub-scale managers: * **CAT I & II AIFs:** Minimum commitment of **$5 million** (approx. INR 41.5 crore). * **CAT III AIFs:** Minimum commitment of **$10 million** (approx. INR 83 crore). * **Sponsor/Manager Commitment:** Typically 2.5% of the fund size or $1 million, whichever is lower. **The TresoWealth Insight:** The structural cost of entry means GIFT City is economically unviable for fund sizes below $50 million. The fixed costs of legal setup, IFSCA registration, and mandatory infrastructure will consume an outsized percentage of the management fee, rendering the tax arbitrage mathematically irrelevant for emerging managers. --- ## 2. Operational and Infrastructure Costs: The Mandatory Overhead Operating within the GIFT IFSC mandates a physical and administrative presence that carries a premium. ### Physical Presence Requirements The IFSCA requires fund managers to maintain a physical presence in GIFT City. Office rentals in GIFT City, while competitive compared to Singapore or Dubai, carry a premium relative to domestic Indian commercial real estate. Expect lease costs of **INR 80–120 per square foot per month**. For a modest 1,000 sq. ft. office, managers are looking at an annual fixed cost of roughly **INR 10–15 lakh ($12,000–$18,000)** before adding fit-out costs. ### Fund Administration and Custody Because GIFT City AIFs operate in a dollar-denominated environment, managers must engage IFSCA-registered fund administrators and custodians. * **Custody Fees:** Typically 1–3 basis points (bps) on AUM. * **Fund Administration:** Ranges from **$30,000 to $80,000 annually**, heavily dependent on the frequency of NAV calculations and the number of investors. ### Banking and Transaction Costs Cross-border capital flows incur banking friction. Remittance fees, KYC compliance for global LPs, and transaction charges on SWIFT transfers add approximately **15–25 bps** in hidden annual friction costs. --- ## 3. The Regulatory Subsidy: Tax Exemptions vs. Compliance Costs The primary justification for GIFT City's operational premium is its tax regime. However, tax exemptions are not free; they are purchased with strict compliance overhead. ### Section 10(4D) and CBDT Notification No. 89/2020 **Section 10(4D) of the Income Tax Act, 1961**, operationalized via **CBDT Notification No. 89/2020**, provides a 100% tax exemption on specified income received by specified funds located in the IFSC. This covers dividends, interest, and long-term capital gains. **The Cost of Compliance:** To claim this exemption, the fund must file prescribed forms and maintain a rigorous audit trail proving that all income falls within the "specified" parameters. The cost of specialized IFSC-compliant tax auditing ranges from **$15,000 to $30,000 annually**. Failure to maintain this compliance strips the fund of its tax advantage, instantly rendering the GIFT City structure a high-cost liability. ### Section 9A Safe Harbor Rules and CBDT Notification No. 74/2020 For offshore funds managed from GIFT City, the threat of creating a taxable presence (Permanent Establishment) in India is a critical risk. **Section 9A of the Income Tax Act**, clarified via **CBDT Notification No. 74/2020**, establishes safe harbor rules. If the fund meets stringent conditions—such as the number of investors, participation limits, and remuneration of the Indian manager—it is exempt from the "business connection" nexus in India. **The Cost of Compliance:** Proving a negative—that you *do not* have a permanent establishment—requires ongoing legal certification. Legal retainers for Section 9A compliance monitoring typically add **$20,000–$40,000** in annual costs. Furthermore, the safe harbor rules dictate strict investor diversification; breaching these thresholds inadvertently triggers a massive tax liability. ### RBI A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 14 / RBI/2023-24/101 Capital movement into GIFT City from domestic investors is governed by the Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS) and foreign investment frameworks. **A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 14 / RBI/2023-24/101** outlines the remittance efficiencies and boundaries for foreign investment flowing through the IFSC. **The Friction Cost:** While Circular No. 14 streamlines the pipeline, domestic LPs investing in GIFT City AIFs are still subject to the LRS limit (currently $250,000 per individual per year). For managers raising domestic capital, this creates an administrative bottleneck. The compliance cost of tracking, verifying, and reporting LRS utilization across hundreds of retail or HNI LPs inflates fund administration fees by an estimated **20–30%** compared to a purely onshore domestic AIF. --- ## 4. Management and Performance Fees: The Dollar-Denominated Drag In GIFT City, management fees are typically denominated in USD. While this protects the manager from INR depreciation, it introduces a structural friction point with domestic LPs whose income is in INR. * **Management Fees:** Standard 1.5% – 2.0% of committed/called capital. * **Performance Fees:** Standard 15% – 20% over a preferred return (hurdle rate) of 8% – 10%. **The Hidden Cost – Currency Hedging:** If a GIFT City AIF deploys capital back into Indian onshore markets (a common strategy for CAT III funds), the fund faces massive FX exposure. Hedging INR against the USD costs roughly **3.5% – 4.5% annually** in the forward market. If a manager charges a 2% management fee and the fund incurs a 4% hedging cost, the gross return required just to break even for the LP is **6%** before the manager even begins to generate alpha. This is the most dangerous hidden cost in the GIFT City ecosystem, and it is frequently glossed over in pitchbooks. --- ## 5. The Break-Even Analysis: When Does GIFT City Make Sense? Given the explicit fees and implicit friction costs, the GIFT City arbitrage only yields a net positive outcome beyond a specific AUM threshold. Let us model the fixed and semi-variable costs for a standard CAT II AIF: | Cost Component | Annualized Cost (USD) | | :--- | :--- | | Legal & IFSCA Registration (Amortized over 5 yrs) | $15,000 | | Physical Office & Compliance Staff | $60,000 | | IFSC Tax & Section 9A Audit | $35,000 | | Fund Administration & Custody (Base) | $40,000 | | **Total Fixed/Quasi-Fixed Costs** | **$150,000** | Assuming a 1.5% management fee on a $50 million fund, the gross management fee yield is $750,000. After the $150,000 overhead, the net yield is $600,000. However, if the fund is $20 million, the gross yield is $300,000. The $150,000 overhead consumes **50%** of the management fee, leaving insufficient capital to compensate investment professionals. **The TresoWealth Verdict:** The mathematical floor for a viable GIFT City AIF is **$50 million** in AUM. Below this, the compliance and operational overhead cannibalizes the manager's economics. --- ## Actionable Insights for Investment Professionals (2026) As the GIFT City ecosystem matures toward its $14.7 billion 2025-2026 projection, the low-hanging fruit of mere jurisdictional arbitrage is disappearing. To survive and generate alpha in this environment, managers and LPs must adopt a protective, highly structured approach: 1. **Demand Total Cost Transparency:** Do not accept a pitchbook that highlights the Section 10(4D) tax exemption without explicitly modeling the 3.5%–4.5% annual FX hedging cost. The tax saving is often a direct transfer to the hedge counterparty. 2. **Audit the Section 9A Compliance Rigor:** The safe harbor provided by CBDT Notification No. 74/2020 is fragile. Ensure the fund’s legal counsel provides quarterly certifications that the fund has not inadvertently breached the investor concentration limits, which would trigger a permanent establishment status and retroactive taxation. 3. **Optimize the LRS Pipeline Early:** For funds targeting domestic Indian capital under the framework of RBI Circular No. 14, pre-qualify LPs for their LRS eligibility *before* the final close. Delayed remittances create cash drag, forcing the fund to sit on uninvested USD while still paying management fees and custody charges. 4. **Evaluate the AUM Threshold Rule:** If you are an LP evaluating a GIFT City AIF, demand to see the break-even analysis. If the manager has less than $50 million in hard commitments, the structural costs will either impair the manager's ability to operate or force them to take excessive risk to meet the hurdle rate. 5. **Scrutinize the "Onshore Rerouting" Strategy:** Many GIFT City funds simply raise offshore USD, convert it to INR, and invest in domestic Indian equities. The math of this strategy—tax savings minus hedging costs minus operational premiums—rarely beats a straightforward onshore Mauritius/Singapore feeder structure unless the AUM is massive (exceeding $200 million). ## Conclusion GIFT City is a powerful structural tool, but it is not a panacea. The $25 billion–$30 billion projected AIF ecosystem reflects the immense potential of a dollar-denominated gateway to India. However, the reality of the $4.6 billion actual capital base as of Q1 2024 suggests that the friction is still working itself out. The fees and costs associated with GIFT City AIFs—ranging from IFSCA registration to the hidden drags of currency hedging and Section 9A compliance—require a rigorous, skeptical evaluation.
