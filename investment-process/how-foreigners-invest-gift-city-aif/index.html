Navigating GIFT City: A Strategic Guide for Foreign Investment in AIFs

As of April 2026, India’s alternative investment landscape has matured into a compelling, yet structurally complex, arena for global capital. The Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City)—India’s sole operational International Financial Services Centre (IFSC)—has emerged as the primary conduit for foreign investors seeking exposure to the subcontinent’s high-growth private equity, venture capital, and hedge fund strategies.

However, the promise of outsized returns is inextricably linked to a dense regulatory architecture. For foreign capital allocators, GIFT City is not a regulatory blank check; it is a highly engineered jurisdiction that demands precise navigation. At TresoWealth, our analysis cuts through the institutional marketing to examine the structural realities. This is how foreigners can invest in GIFT City Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs)—and the critical pitfalls they must avoid.

Key Metrics (2026):
- 349 AIFs launched in GIFT City IFSC
- $22.11 billion assets under management
- 0% tax on capital gains and dividends (Section 10(4E))
- 48-hour repatriation facility
- USD-denominated investment structures

The Macro Context: Capital Flows and Concentration Risks

To understand the strategic imperative of GIFT City, one must first examine the velocity of capital moving through the IFSC. According to the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) and industry reports, AIFs in GIFT City mobilized $10.5 billion (INR 87,716 crore) in FY 2023-24. This represents a staggering concentration of foreign capital funneling through a relatively nascent regulatory sandbox.

Looking forward, the pipeline is expanding even further. Projections indicate that non-resident capital targeting Indian alternative assets via the IFSC is expected to reach $30 billion (INR 2.5 lakh crore) in the near term. These figures are not arbitrary; they reflect a structural shift where global limited partners (LPs)—sovereign wealth funds, endowments, and family offices—are utilizing GIFT City as the primary jurisdictional gateway to bypass the historical frictions of the domestic Indian AIF route.

But a skeptical allocator must ask: what structural mechanisms are driving this capital, and where are the friction points?

The Regulatory Architecture: IFSCA and the Offshore Paradigm

GIFT City operates as a deemed foreign jurisdiction. Physically located within India, it is financially offshore. This jurisdictional duality is governed by the IFSCA, which acts as a unified regulator, consolidating the oversight of the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI), and the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority (IRDAI) into a single authority.

For foreign investors, the IFSCA framework offers a distinct advantage: the ability to invest in India-domiciled funds using foreign currency (primarily USD), without being subject to the stringent capital controls that govern the domestic Indian markets.

Under the IFSCA (Alternative Investment Funds) Regulations, 2021, AIFs are categorized into Category I, II, and III. Foreign investors typically gravitate toward Category II (Private Equity, Venture Capital) and Category III (Hedge Funds, Managed Futures) due to their operational flexibility. Crucially, IFSCA regulations allow these funds to invest "onshore" into the domestic Indian market, subject to certain limits and routing mechanisms, effectively allowing foreign LPs to participate in the India growth story through a tax-efficient, offshore-style vehicle.

The Tax Shield: Dissecting the CBDT Framework

The primary magnet for the $30 billion non-resident capital influx is the tax neutrality offered by the IFSC. However, this neutrality is highly conditional. Foreign investors cannot assume blanket tax exemptions; they must engineer their compliance architecture around three specific regulatory pillars.

1. Section 10(4D) of the Income Tax Act, 1961

Introduced via the Finance Act, 2020, Section 10(4D) is the bedrock of GIFT City’s tax appeal. It stipulates that the income of an AIF located in the IFSC, earned from specified investments, is exempt from income tax in India—provided the fund meets the conditions specified by the Central Government.

For a foreign investor, this means that capital gains, dividends, and interest income generated by the AIF are not subject to Indian withholding taxes, preserving the gross IRR. However, the exemption is strictly contingent upon the AIF’s compliance with IFSCA regulations and the specific nature of its income. Any drift in the fund's investment mandate can jeopardize this status, triggering a tax liability that cascades down to the LP.

2. CBDT Notification No. 93/2019

While Section 10(4D) provides the exemption, CBDT Notification No. 93/2019 (F.No. 370142/14/2019-TPL) defines the exact contours of the fund's income that is taxable in India. This notification is critical because it delineates between "specified income" (which is exempt) and non-specified income.

For foreign investors, the protective stance requires rigorous due diligence on the fund's Private Placement Memorandum (PPM). If the AIF engages in investment activities that fall outside the purview of the exempted income defined in Notification 93/2019, the fund's income becomes taxable in India. This creates a severe jurisdictional risk: a foreign LP could find itself subject to Indian taxation without the benefit of a tax treaty, a scenario that destroys fund economics.

3. CBDT Notification No. 89/2020 and Form 10F

Even if the AIF’s income is tax-exempt, the administrative burden on the foreign investor remains stringent. CBDT Notification No. 89/2020 (F.No. 370142/18/2020-TPL), which introduced Rule 21AE, mandates that non-resident investors claiming treaty benefits or exemptions must establish their jurisdiction and submit Form 10F online.

Before a foreign LP can receive a tax-exempt distribution from a GIFT City AIF, they must possess a Permanent Account Number (PAN) in India and file Form 10F on the Income Tax portal. This form requires the investor to disclose their tax residency, jurisdiction of incorporation, and Tax Identification Number (TIN) in their home country. Failure to file Form 10F prior to the distribution date can result in the AIF manager withholding tax at the highest marginal rate (currently surcharges included, exceeding 40%), regardless of the Section 10(4D) exemption.

The Investment Mechanism: Step-by-Step Allocation

For a foreign investor looking to deploy capital into a GIFT City AIF, the operational workflow is structured but unforgiving of administrative errors.

Step 1: Fund Selection and KYC/AML Scrutiny
The investor must identify an IFSCA-registered AIF. Given the deemed offshore status, the Know Your Customer (KYC) and Anti-Money Laundering (AML) standards align with global FATF guidelines, but are enforced by the IFSCA. Investors must provide comprehensive beneficial ownership documentation. Treso Wealth advises allocators to independently verify the fund manager's net worth certifications and track record, as the IFSCA’s registration process focuses on capital adequacy rather than historical performance validation.

Step 2: Capital Commitment and Inward Remittance
Capital is committed in foreign currency (typically USD) and remitted directly to the AIF’s bank account held with an IFSC Banking Unit (IBU). The IBU operates under a different regulatory framework than domestic Indian banks, offering greater flexibility in interest rates and foreign exchange management. Investors must ensure their remittance instructions explicitly reference the IFSCA registration number to prevent the capital from being intercepted by domestic FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act) regulations.

Step 3: Tax Compliance and Form 10F Filing
Prior to the first capital call, the foreign investor must apply for an Indian PAN and file Form 10F. This is a non-negotiable prerequisite. Allocators should budget 15 to 20 business days for this process, as the Indian Income Tax portal frequently requires manual verification for non-resident entities.

Step 4: Drawdowns and Deployments
Once the capital is called, the AIF manager deploys the funds. For Category II and III AIFs, this often involves routing capital downstream into domestic Indian portfolio companies. This downstream investment is treated as Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) or Foreign Portfolio Investment (FPI), subject to RBI regulations. The AIF structure shields the foreign LP from direct FDI compliance, but the LP bears the indirect risk of regulatory bottlenecks at the portfolio company level.

Step 5: Repatriation
Upon exit, the proceeds (capital and gains) are credited to the investor’s IBU account. Because the income is tax-exempt under Section 10(4D) and the investor has filed Form 10F, the repatriation of funds out of GIFT City to the LP’s home jurisdiction is frictionless, free from Indian capital controls and exit taxes.

Structural Pitfalls: A Skeptical Analysis

While the regulatory architecture is designed to attract the projected $30 billion in non-resident capital, TresoWealth identifies several structural frictions that foreign investors must actively mitigate.

The Treaty Shopping Scrutiny

The Indian tax authorities have historically been aggressive in combating treaty shopping. While GIFT City is designed to be a conduit, foreign investors utilizing holding company structures in jurisdictions like Mauritius or Singapore solely for the purpose of accessing GIFT City must ensure they meet the Limitation of Benefits (LoB) clauses. If the Indian tax authority determines that the LP’s structure lacks "substance," the Section 10(4D) exemption could be challenged, and the LP may be assessed for indirect transfer taxes.

Downstream Investment Regulations

An IFSCA-registered AIF is not a free pass to bypass domestic sectoral caps. If the AIF invests in sectors with FDI caps (e.g., defense, insurance, or digital media), the AIF must comply with the downstream FDI guidelines. In 2026, we are seeing increased regulatory scrutiny on AIFs that act as conduits for restricted foreign capital in sectors prohibited from FDI. Foreign investors must demand transparency from AIF managers regarding the sectoral composition of the target portfolio.

The "Onshore vs. Offshore" Valuation Mismatch

GIFT City AIFs that invest onshore must mark their portfolio to market using domestic Indian valuations. However, the LPs' capital is denominated in USD. The volatility of the INR/USD exchange rate can create severe valuation mismatches. A 15% IRR in INR terms can easily compress to 9% in USD terms if the rupee depreciates significantly. While GIFT City allows access to hedging mechanisms, the cost of carry for INR hedging remains elevated, often consuming 400-500 basis points of the fund’s yield.

Actionable Insights for Global Allocators

To successfully navigate the GIFT City AIF landscape in 2026, foreign investors must adopt a disciplined, protective posture:

  1. Mandate Independent Tax Opinions: Do not rely solely on the AIF manager’s tax opinion regarding Section 10(4D). Engage independent Indian tax counsel to verify that the fund’s investment strategy strictly adheres to the definitions in CBDT Notification No. 93/2019.
  2. Pre-empt Form 10F Delays: Initiate the PAN application and Form 10F filing the moment the Letter of Intent (LOI) is signed. Delaying this until the first capital call exposes the LP to unnecessary withholding tax risks.
  3. Scrutinize Downstream FDI Compliance: Ensure the PPM explicitly caps the AIF’s exposure to sectors with restrictive FDI frameworks. The AIF’s compliance officer must provide quarterly certifications confirming downstream investments adhere to RBI master directions.
  4. Implement Currency Hedging Mandates: For debt-oriented Category II or Category III AIFs, where returns are capped, mandate that the fund implements a systematic INR hedging strategy to protect the USD-denominated IRR from currency depreciation.
  5. Evaluate IBU Counterparty Risk: The capital is held in an IBU. Assess the credit rating and liquidity profile of the specific IBU used by the AIF. While the RBI oversees IBUs, they do not carry the same deposit insurance guarantees as domestic retail banks.

Conclusion

GIFT City represents a paradigm shift in how foreign capital accesses Indian alternative investments. The mobilization of $10.5 billion in FY 2023-24 and the projected influx of $30 billion underscore its structural validity. The jurisdictional arbitrage—facilitated by Section 10(4D) of the Income Tax Act and the streamlined IFSCA framework—offers a compelling value proposition for global LPs.

However, the system is built on precise regulatory compliance. The protections offered by CBDT Notification No. 93/2019 and the administrative requirements of Notification No. 89/2020 are not self-executing; they require proactive, meticulous oversight. For the foreign investor, GIFT City is not a jurisdiction for passive allocation. It is a high-performance engine that yields superior returns only when operated with precise, skeptical, and structurally informed intent.
