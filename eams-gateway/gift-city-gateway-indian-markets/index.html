GIFT City: India’s Offshore Gateway and the Hard Numbers for Institutional Capital

As of April 2026, the narrative surrounding India’s Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) has shifted from speculative infrastructure project to operational financial nucleus. For global investment professionals, GIFT City’s International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) is no longer a theoretical gateway to Indian markets—it is a quantifiable one. However, penetrating this market requires stripping away the promotional rhetoric and examining the structural mechanics, regulatory realities, and raw liquidity metrics that dictate capital deployment.

India’s domestic financial markets are characterized by high growth, but also by capital controls, tax friction, and regulatory opacity for foreign investors. GIFT City was engineered as the jurisdictional workaround—a sandbox where offshore capital can interact with the Indian growth story under a streamlined, common-law-adjacent regulatory framework.

At TresoWealth, our thesis is clear: GIFT City is a viable gateway, but only for capital that respects its specific architectural boundaries and regulatory constraints. Jurisdictional arbitrage is never a substitute for fundamental risk management. Below, we dissect the hard data, the regulatory matrix, and the actionable pathways for institutional capital looking to access the Indian markets via GIFT City.

Key Metrics (2026):
- 349 AIFs launched in GIFT City IFSC
- $22.11 billion assets under management
- 0% tax on capital gains and dividends (Section 10(4E))
- 48-hour repatriation facility
- USD-denominated investment structures

1. The Macro Metrics: Separating Substance from Spin

To evaluate GIFT City’s efficacy as a gateway, we must first audit the capital currently deployed within it. The growth trajectory has been aggressive, but institutional capital must look past the growth rates and scrutinize the absolute values and asset composition.

Assets Under Management (AUM)
Over a five-year period ending in Q1 2026, total AUM in GIFT City surged from a baseline of $2.5 billion to $50 billion. While a 20x multiple is mathematically impressive, the context is critical. This $50 billion represents the cumulative tipping point where operational viability begins to outweigh setup friction. However, investment professionals must interrogate the composition of this AUM. Is it sticky, long-term institutional capital, or is it hot money chasing the IFSC’s tax sunset clauses? The reality is a hybrid: a base of permanent capital from global private equity and hedge funds, overlaid with tactical allocations leveraging the 10-year tax holiday.

IFSC Banking Unit (IBU) Balance Sheets
Total balance sheet assets across IFSC Banking Units currently stand at $58 billion. This metric is the bedrock of the gateway thesis. IBUs act as the offshore funding arteries for onshore Indian projects. They allow foreign banks to raise cheap offshore capital and deploy it into Indian infrastructure, corporate debt, and trade finance without the Reserve Bank of India’s (RBI) stringent domestic statutory reserve requirements. The $58 billion figure indicates that global credit is actively utilizing GIFT City to bridge India’s infrastructure financing gap.

Liquidity and Trading Volume
Average daily trading volume on GIFT City exchanges has reached $12.5 billion. This is the most critical metric for market makers and algorithmic traders. A gateway is useless if you cannot exit. The $12.5 billion daily liquidity—primarily driven by NSE IFSC and BSE IFSC derivatives tied to underlying Indian equities (SGX Nifty transition) and global commodities—proves that the order books have finally achieved the depth required to absorb large institutional block trades without prohibitive slippage.

Specialized Asset Deployment: Aircraft Leasing
Aviation financing deployment in GIFT City has reached $4 billion. While niche, this number is structurally significant. Prior to GIFT City, Indian airlines leased 100% of their aircraft from foreign jurisdictions like Ireland, bleeding foreign exchange. The $4 billion deployed demonstrates that the IFSC’s specific legal framework (which mirrors the Cape Town Convention) successfully competes with established global lessors, validating the jurisdiction's ability to host complex, asset-backed financial structures.

2. The Regulatory Matrix: The Price of Access

GIFT City operates under a unified regulator, the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA). The premise is "light-touch regulation," but TresoWealth views this as "streamlined oversight," not deregulation. The IFSCA has aggressively tightened its framework in 2025 and 2026, moving from a growth-at-all-costs phase to a compliance-and-substance phase. Ignorance of these specific statutory frameworks is an unacceptable operational risk.

Inspection and Intermediary Oversight: IFSCA/2023-24/GEN/P0042
This notification fundamentally altered the operational risk calculus for capital market intermediaries. Under the IFSCA (Capital Market Intermediaries) regulations, IFSCA/2023-24/GEN/P0042 grants the authority sweeping powers to inspect books of accounts, records, and the systems of trading members and beneficiaries.
* The TresoWealth Takeaway: The era of "nameplate" operations in GIFT City is over. If you are setting up a fund or a brokerage, you must maintain economic substance. Regulatory inspection under this framework is rigorous and unannounced. Compliance architecture must be built into the operational stack from day one, not retrofitted after a notice of inspection.

Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and KYC: IFSCA/2022-23/GEN/P0015
Aligning with global FATF standards, the IFSCA Anti-Money Laundering (Know Your Client) Registration Agency Regulations, 2011—subsequently updated and enforced rigorously under the directives of IFSCA/2022-23/GEN/P0015—mandate strict client onboarding protocols.
* The TresoWealth Takeaway: GIFT City is not a secrecy haven. This regulation requires intermediaries to verify the identity of beneficial owners down to the natural person level. For private equity and venture capital funds routing capital through the IFSC, the burden of KYC on Limited Partners (LPs) is substantial. Failure to comply results in immediate freezing of accounts and potential revocation of the IFSCA license.

Foreign Exchange Management: A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 10
The gateway between GIFT City (an offshore jurisdiction) and mainland India (a tightly controlled onshore jurisdiction) is governed by the RBI. A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 10, pertaining to the Foreign Exchange Management (Objectives for Foreign Investment), delineates the exact pathways and repatriation rules for capital moving from the IFSC to the domestic Indian market.
* The TresoWealth Takeaway: This circular is the operational hinge of the "Gateway" thesis. It permits IFSC entities to invest in Indian markets, but strictly defines the instruments and the repatriation of returns. Capital cannot move freely; it moves through designated routes. Misinterpreting this circular leads to trapped capital. Institutional investors must structure their IFSC vehicles precisely to ensure that dividend repatriation and capital gains can be freely converted back to hard currency without falling afoul of onshore FEMA restrictions.

3. Mechanics of the Gateway: How Capital Actually Moves

Understanding the regulations is step one; structuring the vehicle is step two. GIFT City provides three primary conduits for foreign capital seeking Indian exposure:

A. The Foreign Portfolio Investor (FPI) Route
Prior to GIFT City, foreign investors had to register as FPIs with SEBI, often navigating cumbersome KYC and operational delays. Today, an IFSC-established fund can register as an FPI through the IFSCA, benefiting from a single-window clearance mechanism. Once registered, the FPI can trade directly on the NSE and BSE (onshore). The advantage is operational speed and the ability to utilize the IBU banking infrastructure for faster settlement and margin financing.

B. Exchange Traded Derivatives (The Offshore Onramp)
For hedge funds and algorithmic traders who do not want the custody risk or settlement friction of onshore Indian equities, GIFT City offers NSE IFSC and BSE IFSC. Here, investors can trade single-stock futures and index derivatives (like the Nifty 50) denominated in USD. The $12.5 billion average daily trading volume proves this market has matured. It allows global macro funds to take directional bets on Indian interest rates, equities, and currency without ever touching an onshore Indian bank account.

C. Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) and Private Equity
For private equity, GIFT City allows the pooling of offshore capital into an IFSC Fund, which then deploys capital into mainland Indian unlisted companies as FDI. The structural advantage lies in the IFSC's tax regime—specifically, the 10-year tax holiday on specified incomes, and the exemption from capital gains tax on the transfer of specified offshore assets routed through the IFSC. Furthermore, the IBU can provide leverage to these funds at global interest rates, a facility heavily restricted under onshore RBI guidelines.

4. The TresoWealth Risk Assessment: Friction Points in the Gateway

No institutional allocation should be made based solely on yield and tax incentives. The protective lens requires a clear-eyed assessment of the structural friction points inherent in GIFT City as of 2026.

  • The Tax Sunset Cliff: The current 100% tax holiday on specified income for IFSC units expires in March 2027 (for units set up before this date). While there are industry expectations of an extension, institutional capital must model a scenario where the tax advantages normalize. If the tax benefit evaporates, GIFT City must compete purely on regulatory efficiency and infrastructure—a much harder thesis to justify against established hubs like Singapore or Dublin.
  • Repatriation Reality Checks: While A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 10 facilitates investment, the physical act of moving large sums of INR out of India, converting it to USD in the IFSC, and wiring it to New York or London still encounters onshore banking friction. The RBI’s primary mandate is currency stability; during periods of INR volatility, even IFSC entities may face implicit friction in large-scale repatriation.
  • Judicial Precedent and Insolvency: GIFT City operates under the Indian legal system, with cases adjudicated in the Gujarat High Court and ultimately the Supreme Court of India. While the IFSCA has established its own dispute resolution mechanism, the enforcement of collateral and bankruptcy proceedings still relies on the National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT) and the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC). Global investors accustomed to the predictability of English common law or Singapore's International Commercial Court must price in the timeline inefficiencies of the Indian judicial system.
  • Operational Ecosystem Maturity: Despite the gleaming towers, the ancillary ecosystem—specialized legal counsel, independent auditors, prime brokerage services, and deep secondary debt markets—remains shallower than London or New York. Execution risk in bespoke OTC derivatives is still higher than in legacy financial centers.

5. Actionable Architecture: Structuring for Capital Preservation

For investment professionals allocating to GIFT City in 2026, the strategy must prioritize structural integrity over speculative yield. We recommend the following framework:

1. Substantiate or Fail
Under IFSCA/2023-24/GEN/P0042, the cost of non-compliance is existential. Do not establish shell entities. Ensure your IFSC unit has physical office space, full-time employees executing core activities, and robust local compliance infrastructure. The regulatory arbitrage is in the tax code, not in the substance requirements.

2. Centralize AML/KYC Architecture
Leverage the IFSCA/2022-23/GEN/P0015 framework to build a centralized KYC repository within your IFSC entity. Because the IFSCA mandates strict adherence, outsourcing this to sub-par vendors is a liability. Invest in top-tier regtech solutions that cross-reference global sanctions lists and beneficial ownership registries automatically. Your LP onboarding must be airtight before capital touches the IBU.

3. Hedge the Regulatory Risk via Circular 10
When structuring onshore investments from your IFSC vehicle, strictly adhere to the permissible classes of investors and instruments defined in A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 10. Avoid complex, multi-layered structures that obscure the ultimate beneficial owner. The RBI monitors the "round-tripping" of capital aggressively. Transparency in routing is the ultimate form of capital protection.

4. Optimize the Tax Window, Plan for the Expiry
If deploying long-term private equity capital, establish the IFSC fund immediately to lock in the remaining months of the tax holiday. However, model your IRR targets assuming a standard 25-30% effective tax rate post-sunset. If the deal does not clear your hurdle rate without the tax subsidy, the underlying asset does not possess sufficient alpha to justify the jurisdictional complexity.

5. Utilize IBUs for Currency Matching
One of the most underappreciated advantages of GIFT City is the ability of IBUs to lend in foreign currency (USD/EUR) to Indian corporates. For fixed-income investors, this eliminates the INR depreciation risk that plagues onshore rupee-denominated debt. Structure your credit allocations to leverage the $58 billion IBU balance sheet for foreign-currency-denominated trade finance and supply chain lending, rather than taking on onshore currency risk.

Conclusion

GIFT City in 2026 is no longer an experiment; it is a functional, high-growth gateway to the Indian markets. The surge from $2.5 billion to $50 billion in AUM, the $58 billion in IBU assets, and the $12.5 billion in daily trading liquidity are undeniable proof of concept.

However, a gateway is only as good as the rules governing its use. The IFSCA has made it abundantly clear through notifications like IFSCA/2023-24/GEN/P0042 and IFSCA/2022-23/GEN/P0015 that access to the Indian market via this offshore node requires strict adherence to global compliance standards. Furthermore, the RBI’s firm hand, as delineated in A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 10, ensures that while capital can enter India efficiently, it cannot do so opaquely.

For the institutional investor, GIFT City offers a rare proposition: the growth velocity of an emerging market combined with the financial infrastructure of an offshore center. But the protective mandate remains—capital must be deployed with precision, structured with full regulatory substance, and constantly stress-tested against the realities of onshore friction. At TresoWealth, we believe GIFT City is a premier gateway, but only for capital that enters with its eyes wide open and its compliance infrastructure fully armored.
