I. The 0% LTCG Conundrum: Exemption vs. Evasion

The promise of a 0% Long-Term Capital Gains tax rate is the holy grail of cross-border structuring. However, in the post-2024 regulatory environment, 0% LTCG is rarely a loophole; it is a highly conditional statutory exemption that requires exact structural compliance.

FAQ 1: Is a 0% LTCG rate legally achievable for foreign investors, and what is the regulatory basis?

Yes, but only under strictly defined parameters, most notably under Section 10(4D) of the Income-tax Act, 1961. This section provides a specific exemption for income arising to a specified person from an offshore fund, provided stringent conditions are met.

The regulatory framework governing this was solidified by CBDT Notification No. 89/2021-Income Tax, which laid down the exhaustive conditions for claiming the Section 10(4D) exemption. The notification mandates that the offshore fund must be a widely held fund, complying with specific investor diversification norms, and must not engage in directly or indirectly carrying on any business in India.

The financial incentive for maintaining compliant structures is immense. The projected market size for such tax-efficient offshore fund structures reached $100 Billion for the period ending FY 2023-24. Furthermore, the projected additional tax optimization (or revenue protection, depending on which side of the ledger you sit) generated by compliant 0% LTCG structures was estimated at INR 5,000 Crore (~$600 Million) for FY 2023-24.

FAQ 2: What triggers the collapse of a 0% LTCG structure?

The TresoWealth view is categorical: regulatory friction destroys lazy structures. A 0% LTCG claim collapses the moment an offshore fund fails the "widely held" test or inadvertently triggers a "business connection" (Significant Economic Presence) in India.

If the fund is deemed to be operating a business in India rather than merely making investments, the income is recharacterized as business income. The 0% LTCG exemption evaporates, and the income becomes subject to the standard corporate tax rate for foreign companies (typically 40% plus surcharge and cess), potentially leading to retroactive taxation and penalties.

II. DTAA & Form 10F: The Treaty Shield and Its Cracks

Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements (DTAA) are the primary shield against jurisdictional overlap, allowing taxpayers to claim relief in their resident country for taxes deducted in the source country (India). However, a treaty right is not a self-executing guarantee; it is a conditional privilege contingent on rigorous compliance.

FAQ 3: How does a non-resident claim DTAA benefits, and what is the current status of Form 10F?

To claim DTAA benefits, a non-resident must provide a Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) from the tax authority of their home country. However, the Indian CBDT requires additional information not always present on a standard TRC (such as the specific article of the DTAA under which relief is claimed, and the status of the taxpayer). Historically, this supplementary data was furnished via Form 10F.

The compliance landscape shifted with CBDT Circular No. 3/2022-Income Tax. The Circular mandated that non-residents without a Permanent Account Number (PAN) in India must electronically file Form 10F on the Income Tax portal to claim DTAA benefits. This created immediate operational friction, as the portal's architecture was not initially designed to accommodate non-resident users without PANs, leading to a temporary relaxation.

However, as of 2026, the CBDT has strictly enforced the e-filing of Form 10F. Non-compliance means the Assessing Officer (AO) is legally bound to reject the DTAA claim, resulting in a flat withholding rate (often 20% or 40%) rather than the treaty rate (e.g., 10% or 15%).

FAQ 4: What is the financial exposure tied to DTAA structuring?

The capital protected by DTAAs is staggering. Total assets under treaty-protected and cross-border structures were recorded at INR 86,432 Crore (~$10.4 Billion) for the 2024-2026 timeframe. A failure to secure DTAA benefits on this volume of assets exposes investors to double taxation, effectively eroding yields by up to 20-30% on a gross basis.

III. Withholding Tax & Remittances: The 15CA/15CB Gauntlet

Withholding tax (TDS) is the government's primary mechanism for capturing tax at the source before capital leaves the Indian jurisdiction. For any payment chargeable to tax in India made to a non-resident, the remitter must navigate the Form 15CA/15CB ecosystem. This is the tollgate of cross-border capital flight.

FAQ 5: What are the current compliance requirements for Form 15CA and 15CB?

Form 15CA is a declaration made by the remitter, while Form 15CB is a certificate from a practicing Chartered Accountant (CA) verifying the nature of the remittance and confirming that the applicable withholding tax has been deducted.

The regulatory framework was significantly tightened by CBDT Notification No. 30/2022-Income Tax, which amended the Income-tax Rules, 1962. This notification restructured the 15CA/15CB matrix, introducing stricter purpose codes and mandating a granular breakdown of the remittance. It explicitly linked the withholding tax rate to the DTAA article claimed, meaning the CA must now explicitly verify the treaty eligibility before certifying Form 15CB.

FAQ 6: How does the $810 Billion foreign portfolio landscape affect remittance friction?

When total foreign portfolio liabilities sit at INR 67.5 Trillion (~$810 Billion), the volume of daily repatriation requests is immense. Banks in India act as the first line of defense for the RBI and CBDT. Post-Notification 30/2022, banks are instructed to reject remittances where the 15CA/15CB forms do not perfectly align with the stated purpose of the SWIFT transfer.

Furthermore, with outstanding non-resident deposits at $146.61 Billion (as of March 2024), the repatriation of interest and principal on NRI deposits is heavily monitored. Any mismatch between the withholding tax rate stated on Form 15CB and the rate prescribed under the relevant DTAA or the Income Tax Act will result in a frozen transaction.
