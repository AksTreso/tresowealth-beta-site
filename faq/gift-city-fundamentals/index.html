# GIFT City Fundamentals: 10 FAQs Answered As of April 2026, Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) has evolved from an ambitious regulatory experiment into a critical node in global capital flows. For investment professionals, however, the operational realities of India’s only International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) often lag behind the geopolitical rhetoric. While the regulatory arbitrage opportunities are undeniable, they are layered with structural frictions, compliance complexities, and liquidity constraints that demand rigorous due diligence. At TresoWealth, our mandate is to look past the institutional marketing brochures. Below, we dissect the fundamental mechanics of GIFT City, answering the 10 most critical FAQs with precise data, regulatory citations, and actionable insights for capital allocators. --- ### 1. What exactly is GIFT City, and does it warrant the "global hub" moniker? GIFT City is a demarcated Special Economic Zone (SEZ) in Gujarat, governed as an IFSC by the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA). It operates under a distinct legal and financial ecosystem, separate from the Domestic Tariff Area (DTA) of India. The premise is simple: provide a jurisdiction with world-class infrastructure, a unified regulator, and a favorable tax regime to allow India to capture offshore financial transactions that currently migrate to Singapore, Dubai, or London. Does it warrant the moniker? Partially. The growth metrics are mathematically impressive but require context. Between FY 2020 and FY 2024, the targeted market capitalization and aggregate business volume surpassed the **$100 Billion** milestone. However, professionals must remain skeptical of headline numbers; a significant portion of this volume reflects booking transactions and treasury operations rather than deep, sticky asset management capital. The ecosystem is functionally viable for specific structures, but it lacks the secondary market depth required to be a true global trading hub. ### 2. How fast is the ecosystem actually growing, and is the growth sustainable? The compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of funds established in GIFT City stands at **40%** as of August 2024. This is an aggressive trajectory, driven primarily by the migration of Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) and global treasury centers seeking tax optimization. However, a 40% CAGR on a relatively low base is easier to achieve than sustaining velocity at scale. The growth is currently propelled by regulatory arbitrage—specifically, tax exemptions that have a sunset clause (currently set for March 2025, though widely anticipated to be extended in 2026). The sustainability of this growth is entirely dependent on whether the IFSCA can transition the value proposition from *arbitrage* to *infrastructure*. If the tax incentives lapse without a corresponding improvement in operational liquidity and market depth, capital flight will be swift. ### 3. What is the actual capital deployed versus capital committed? This is where skepticism is required. As of August 2024, the net allocation and deposits in GIFT City reached **$22.5 Billion**. While this represents a robust influx, investment professionals must distinguish between "committed" capital by Limited Partners (LPs) and actual "deployed" capital by Fund Managers. A substantial portion of the $22.5 billion remains in banking deposits and low-yield liquid instruments within GIFT City banks. The friction lies in deployment: GIFT City funds investing back into the Indian DTA face regulatory caps and withholding tax mechanisms that can trap capital. Consequently, while the fundraising numbers look pristine in pitch decks, the capital velocity—the speed at which capital is translated into productive assets—remains suboptimal. ### 4. What are the specific tax advantages for funds operating in GIFT City? The tax framework is the primary catalyst for GIFT City's existence. For investment professionals, the mechanics of these exemptions dictate structuring decisions: * **Fund-Level Tax Exemption:** Under **CBDT Notification No. 86/2020 / Income Tax Section 10(23EC)**, any income received by an IFSC unit (including AIFs) from specific investments, or capital gains arising from the transfer of such investments, is exempt at the fund level. This effectively creates a pass-through vehicle that avoids the double taxation historically prevalent in Indian fund structures. * **Zero GST:** Services provided by IFSC units to offshore entities are zero-rated under GST, eliminating the indirect tax drag on management fees and carry. * **Carry Interest:** While global jurisdictions are tightening the noose on carried interest, GIFT City currently offers a benign environment, though structuring carry to ensure it falls under the Section 10(23EC) exemption requires meticulous documentation. ### 5. How does repatriation of funds work when returning capital to the Domestic Tariff Area (DTA)? This is a structural blind spot that catches many offshore allocators off guard. While moving money *into* GIFT City is frictionless, moving it *out*—especially into the Indian DTA—triggers tax events. Under **CBDT Notification No. 89/2020 / Income Tax Section 10(4D)**, the exemption applies to income and withdrawals *within* the IFSC. However, when a GIFT City fund repatriates capital or gains to a resident Indian entity or individual in the DTA, the protective shield collapses. The repatriated amount is subject to standard Indian income tax provisions as per the recipient's residential status. **Actionable Insight:** Do not structure a GIFT City fund assuming seamless tax-free repatriation to India. If the end-investor is a resident Indian entity expecting to utilize the funds domestically, the DTA tax liability will nullify the IFSC tax arbitrage. GIFT City is optimally structured for offshore investors or for Indian capital destined for offshore deployment. ### 6. Can resident Indians invest through GIFT City, and what are the constraints? Yes, but under highly specific parameters. The Reserve Bank of India allows resident individuals to invest in IFSC units, but it is not a carte blanche to bypass the Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS). Under **RBI A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 51**, resident individuals can remit funds to IFSCs for investing in securities. However, the circular explicitly mandates that these investments must be in entities operating in **FATF-compliant jurisdictions**. Furthermore, the LRS annual limit of $250,000 per individual applies. **Actionable Insight:** For wealth managers advising UHNWIs, Circular No. 51 provides a legal pathway for geographic diversification, but it cannot be used to route domestic black money or exceed LRS caps. Ensure strict compliance with FATF declarations; the RBI has tightened reporting requirements in 2026, and non-compliance triggers severe FEMA penalties. ### 7. What are the operational and banking frictions on the ground? The marketing material emphasizes a "single-window clearance" via IFSCA. The operational reality is more fragmented. Banking in GIFT City remains a bottleneck. Only a handful of banks (SBI, HDFC, Kotak, and a few foreign banks) operate IFSC Banking Units (IBUs). Opening an IBU account requires rigorous KYC, and transaction processing often suffers from a lack of integration with global custodians. Furthermore, the time-zone advantage (IST) is somewhat negated by the fact that settlement cycles and custodian services still rely on offshore infrastructures that operate on Western time. Capital allocators must budget an additional 15-20% in operational overhead and timeline delays compared to setting up a fund in a mature jurisdiction like the Cayman Islands or Luxembourg. ### 8. How does GIFT City compare to established IFSCs like DIFC or Singapore? GIFT City is structurally a "cost-center" IFSC, whereas DIFC and Singapore are "full-stack" ecosystems. * **Taxation:** GIFT City offers a 10-year tax holiday (which is competitive but temporary), whereas DIFC offers a 50-year zero-tax guarantee. * **Legal Framework:** DIFC uses English Common Law with an independent judicial system. GIFT City relies on Indian laws adjudicated by Indian courts, which introduces systemic jurisdiction risk and potential judicial delays. * **Talent Pool:** Singapore and Dubai have deep pools of global compliance and fund management talent. GIFT City relies heavily on talent commuting from Mumbai, which increases operational friction and cost. GIFT City wins strictly on cost (setup and operational expenses are roughly 30-40% cheaper) and for specific India-centric macro plays. It does not yet win on legal certainty or ecosystem depth. ### 9. What asset classes and fund structures make the most sense in GIFT City right now? Given the current regulatory and liquidity environment, TresoWealth advises a highly selective approach to GIFT City structuring. The optimal strategies include: * **Category III AIFs targeting offshore investors:** For global LPs seeking India exposure, a GIFT City CAT III fund offers the Section 10(23EC) tax exemption, avoiding the complex treaty-shopping requirements previously needed through Mauritius or Singapore. * **Aircraft and Ship Leasing:** The IFSCA has created highly specific, favorable depreciation and tax frameworks for leasing. This is currently the most successful and fundamentally sound vertical in GIFT City. * **Global Treasury Centers:** Indian conglomerates establishing global treasury operations in GIFT City can efficiently manage offshore subsidiaries' cash flows without triggering Indian tax drag. Conversely, setting up high-frequency trading desks or highly liquid hedge fund structures is premature due to the lack of secondary market depth and custodial agility. ### 10. What are the hidden risks and structural blind spots investors must monitor? Capital preservation requires identifying the risks that local promoters will not highlight. * **Regulatory Sunset Clauses:** The 10-year tax holiday is the bedrock of GIFT City. If the Indian government does not extend this beyond the current deadline, the fundamental thesis collapses. Investors must structure funds with clear termination or migration provisions if the tax regime alters. * **Regulatory Overreach:** IFSCA is a unified regulator, but it operates in the shadow of the RBI and SEBI. In times of systemic stress, the RBI has historically demonstrated a tendency to impose capital controls. The risk of sudden, retroactive regulatory tightening on repatriation remains non-zero. * **Liquidity Mismatch:** As the $22.5 billion allocation grows, the lack of deep, liquid offshore rupee markets (compared to ONDC in Singapore) means that in a crisis, funds may face severe markdowns when forced to sell assets to meet redemptions. ### Conclusion GIFT City in 2026 is a jurisdiction of immense potential constrained by operational reality. The 40% CAGR and the $22.5 billion in net allocations prove that the arbitrage is real and that capital is willing to test the waters. However, the $100 billion target achieved between FY20 and FY24 must be viewed through a critical lens. For the investment professional, GIFT City is not a universal solvent for Indian fund structuring. It is a highly specific tool—excellent for offshore-to-India capital flows, aircraft leasing, and tax-sheltered treasury operations, but fraught with friction for DTA repatriation and high-frequency liquidity. Structure with precision, enforce strict compliance with Circular No. 51 and CBDT Notifications 86 and 89, and always maintain a viable exit jurisdiction should the regulatory winds shift.
