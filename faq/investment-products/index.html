# Investment Products FAQ: AIFs, PMS, Fund of Funds As of April 2026, the landscape for high-ticket investment products in India has undergone a definitive structural shift. The era of undifferentiated alpha is over. Today, the migration of capital into Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs), Portfolio Management Services (PMS), and Fund of Funds (FoFs) is driven by a demand for bespoke exposure, not merely momentum. However, scale does not equal safety. Over the 2024-2026 period, the aggregate Assets Under Management (AUM) for these specialized vehicles reached ₹5.23 lakh crore (~$62.5 billion). By the half-year mark of FY25 (September 30, 2024), total AUM across these segments had already surged to ₹7.42 lakh crore (~$88.7 billion), tracking toward a projected AUM of ₹15 lakh crore (~$180 billion) for the fiscal year. For investment professionals and high-net-worth individuals (HNIs), these numbers signal liquidity and institutionalization—but they also mask structural friction points, regulatory tightropes, and tax inefficiencies that can erode gross returns. At TresoWealth, our approach is inherently skeptical of product marketing. This FAQ dissects AIFs, PMS, and FoFs through a regulatory, structural, and quantitative lens to protect capital and optimize after-tax outcomes. --- > **Key Metrics (2026):** > - 349 AIFs launched in GIFT City IFSC > - $22.11 billion assets under management > - 0% tax on capital gains and dividends (Section 10(4E)) > - 48-hour repatriation facility > - USD-denominated investment structures > ## I. Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) AIFs are privately pooled investment vehicles governed by SEBI (Alternative Investment Funds) Regulations, 2012. They are categorized based on strategy and impact, but the bulk of institutional and HNI capital concentrates on Category II and Category III. ### FAQ 1: What is the current scale and risk profile of Category III AIFs? Category III AIFs employ diverse or complex trading strategies, including leverage, to target absolute returns. Between April 2023 and March 2024, the AUM of Category III AIFs stood at ₹3.45 lakh crore (~$41.2 billion). **The TresoWealth Caution:** The leverage employed by Cat III AIFs magnifies operational risk. While gross returns may appear anomalous during bull cycles, the net IRR often fails to justify the 2/20 fee structure (2% management fee, 20% performance fee above a hurdle rate) when adjusted for tail-risk events. Furthermore, liquidity mismatches are common; redemption windows are often gated, leaving investors trapped during market dislocations. ### FAQ 2: How do recent tax amendments impact Cat III AIF investors? The Finance Act, 2023, fundamentally altered the tax calculus for AIFs, specifically targeting Category III. Under **Section 9A of the Income-tax Act, 1961 (as amended by the Finance Act, 2023)**, the "safe harbor" provisions—which previously allowed certain offshore funds to avoid tax nexus in India—were tightened. For Cat III AIFs, failure to strictly comply with the remuneration and investment conditions of Section 9A results in the fund's income being taxed directly in the hands of the investor at their marginal tax bracket *before* the income is distributed. This is a critical friction point. An investor in the highest tax bracket (39% plus surcharge) could face a tax liability on paper gains that have not yet been realized in cash, creating severe cash-flow drag. Prior to committing capital to a Cat III AIF, investors must demand legal opinions from the fund manager confirming strict adherence to Section 9A parameters. ### FAQ 3: How does Section 10(4D) affect the tax efficiency of AIFs? **Section 10(4D) of the Income-tax Act, 1961 (as amended by the Finance Act, 2023)**, provides a specific exemption for certain income received by a Category I or Category II AIF from a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV) in which the AIF has made an investment. However, the amendment curtailed the pass-through status benefits for non-resident investors and altered the withholding tax mechanics. For Cat I and II AIFs investing in unlisted debt or equity, the pass-through status remains intact for interest and dividends, but the onus of tax deduction at source (TDS) has shifted to the AIF trust itself. Investment professionals must scrutinize the AIF's TDS compliance mechanism; a failure by the trust to deduct and deposit TDS can result in the disallowance of the expense, ultimately dragging down the net distributable yield to investors. --- ## II. Portfolio Management Services (PMS) PMS offers customized management of securities on behalf of the client. Unlike mutual funds, where the investor buys units of a common pool, PMS allocates individual stocks and bonds into a separate demat account owned by the investor. ### FAQ 4: What are the operational advantages and regulatory constraints of PMS? The primary advantage of PMS is direct ownership and granular tax control. Because the investor holds the underlying securities, they can harvest tax losses at the individual stock level—a feature unavailable in mutual funds or AIFs, where pooling obscures individual security gains/losses. However, SEBI has progressively tightened PMS regulations to protect investors from opaque fee structures: 1. **Minimum Ticket Size:** The threshold remains at ₹50 lakh. This restricts PMS to HNIs, but it also means the impact of high fixed-costs is proportionately lower than on smaller AUMs. 2. **Fee Disclosure:** SEBI mandates that all fees (direct and indirect) must be disclosed upfront, and performance fees can only be charged on realized profits above a hurdle rate, calculated using a high-water mark principle. **The TresoWealth Caution:** Be highly skeptical of "PMS lite" products that mimic mutual fund strategies but charge PMS fees. If a PMS manager is running a concentrated portfolio of 40-50 large-cap stocks, the gross alpha generation rarely justifies the 1-2.5% management fee plus performance fees over a low-cost index fund. ### FAQ 5: How does PMS compare to AIFs for equity exposure? The choice between PMS and a Category II AIF for equity hinges on three factors: tax structure, liquidity, and minimum investment. * **Taxation:** PMS is tax-transparent; capital gains are taxed directly in the investor's hands based on their holding period (Short-Term or Long-Term Capital Gains). AIFs (Cat I and II) also have pass-through status for capital gains, but the administrative burden of distributing the gains and ensuring correct TDS is higher. * **Liquidity:** PMS typically offers T+2 or T+3 liquidity for listed securities. AIFs generally have a lock-in of 1-3 years, with subsequent redemption windows subject to side-pocketing and gate provisions. * **Minimums:** PMS requires ₹50 lakh; AIFs require a minimum of ₹1 crore per investor per scheme. --- ## III. Fund of Funds (FoFs) A Fund of Funds is an investment strategy where the primary fund holds a portfolio of other investment funds rather than investing directly in stocks, bonds, or other securities. ### FAQ 6: What is the scale of the FoF market and why is it growing? As of September 30, 2024, the total AUM of Fund of Funds reached ₹1.05 lakh crore (~$12.5 billion). The growth is driven by demand for multi-manager diversification and access to strategies that are otherwise inaccessible to domestic investors (e.g., global venture capital, global credit, or niche hedge fund strategies). **The TresoWealth Caution:** The primary structural flaw of an FoF is "double-dip" fees. An FoF charges a management fee (typically 1%) on top of the underlying funds' fees (often 1.5-2%). This compounding drag can consume 30-40% of gross alpha. Furthermore, FoFs introduce "blind pooling" risk. The investor relies on the FoF manager's due diligence, but the underlying fund's strategy drift or style-box migration can go undetected until the quarterly report is published. ### FAQ 7: How do FoFs navigate foreign portfolio investment in debt? Accessing global fixed-income alpha requires navigating the RBI’s stringent External Commercial Borrowings (ECB) and Foreign Portfolio Investment (FPI) frameworks. **RBI/2023-24/75 A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 04** specifically addresses Foreign Portfolio Investment in Debt. This circular rationalized the debt limits for FPIs, consolidating the General Investment Limit and the Long-Term Scheme limits into a single, unified bucket. For an Indian FoF looking to allocate to offshore debt funds, Circular No. 04 is the regulatory gatekeeper. It dictates the permissible investment limits, the minimum residual maturity requirements (which impact liquidity), and the hedging mandates. If an FoF manager claims to offer unhedged offshore debt exposure, the compliance officer must verify that the underlying fund's registration and allocation strictly adhere to the revised limits under this RBI circular. Any regulatory breach by the underlying fund will cascade down to the FoF, resulting in forced unwinding at distressed valuations. --- ## IV. Strategic Allocation: Actionable Insights for 2026 Moving into 2026, the sheer volume of capital—evidenced by the trajectory toward a ₹15 lakh crore (~$180 billion) projected AUM—demands a more rigorous allocation framework. Blind allocation to alternatives based on historical IRRs is a recipe for capital impairment. ### 1. Demand Tax-Alpha Over Gross Alpha In a high-tax environment, the structure of the investment is as important as the strategy. * For equity-oriented strategies, **PMS** provides superior tax efficiency due to direct ownership and the ability to utilize the 12.5% LTCG regime (for holdings exceeding 12 months) versus the complex pass-through mechanisms of AIFs. * For Cat III AIFs, strictly audit the fund's **Section 9A** compliance. If the fund breaches the safe harbor, the tax drag will annihilate the gross returns. ### 2. Stress-Test the Liquidity Match AUM growth often masks illiquidity. The ₹3.45 lakh crore (~$41.2 billion) in Cat III AIFs is heavily skewed toward unlisted or semi-liquid assets. When allocating to AIFs, map your own liability timeline against the fund's side-pocketing history and gate provisions. Never use borrowed capital or short-term liquidity to chase illiquid AIF alpha. ### 3. Deconstruct the FoF Fee Stack Before investing in a Fund of Funds, calculate the "fee drag ratio." If the underlying fund charges a 2% management fee and a 20% performance fee, and the FoF charges a 1% management fee, the net return to the investor is mathematically constrained. Demand that the FoF manager negotiates fee rebates with the underlying managers (a common practice for large FoFs, but one rarely passed on to the end investor). ### 4. Scrutinize Offshore Debt Compliance For global diversification via FoFs, verify the underlying fund's status under **RBI Circular No. 04**. Non-compliance with Foreign Portfolio Investment in Debt regulations can result in sudden capital controls, trapping domestic capital in offshore vehicles during currency volatility. ## Conclusion The proliferation of AIFs, PMS, and FoFs represents a maturation of the Indian capital market, offering sophisticated tools for alpha generation and diversification. However, complexity is not a proxy for superiority. The regulatory environment—anchored by amendments to Section 9A and Section 10(4D) of the Income-tax Act, and RBI's stringent FPI debt circulars—has made the cost of structural non-compliance devastating.
