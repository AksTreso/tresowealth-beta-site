# Indian Fund Managers in GIFT City: Top Picks and Regulatory Realities As of April 2026, the Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) has evolved from a regulatory experiment into a critical conduit for Indian fund managers navigating global capital flows. However, the narrative of unbridled success requires a skeptical examination. While the headline growth metrics are undeniably compelling, the underlying architecture of GIFT City remains heavily reliant on regulatory arbitrage and tax incentivization. For investment professionals, the central question is not whether GIFT City is growing, but where the sustainable, risk-adjusted alpha lies within its boundaries. This analysis dissects the current state of Indian fund managers operating out of GIFT City, evaluates the regulatory framework underpinning their operations, and identifies the top allocation picks for 2026—tempered by a rigorous assessment of structural and compliance risks. > **Key Metrics (2026):** > - 349 AIFs launched in GIFT City IFSC > - $22.11 billion assets under management > - 0% tax on capital gains and dividends (Section 10(4E)) > - 48-hour repatriation facility > - USD-denominated investment structures > ## The Growth Paradigm: Metrics Behind the Hype The quantitative trajectory of GIFT City’s International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) has been aggressive, driven primarily by the migration of alternative investment funds (AIFs) and portfolio management services (PMS) seeking operational and tax efficiencies. As of the 2024-2026 timeframe, Total Assets Under Management (AUM) and investor metrics for funds domiciled in GIFT City have reached **$10.5 billion**. This figure, while representing a fraction of the onshore AUM, marks a decisive shift in how Indian fund managers structure offshore capital. To understand the velocity of this migration, one must look to the prior fiscal year's momentum. As of Q3 2024 (October 2024), the IFSC reported a projected compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of **38%** for fund establishments. Furthermore, the projected market size for the hub stood at **$35 billion** as of March 2024. From a TresoWealth analytical perspective, a 38% CAGR transitioning toward a $35 billion projected market size signals rapid capital accumulation, but it also raises structural questions. Is this growth reflective of genuine economic value creation, or is it merely a jurisdictional reshuffling of existing capital? Current data suggests the latter is predominantly true. The $10.5 billion AUM is largely "re-domiciled" or "mirror-funded" capital that previously operated through Singapore or Mauritius. The sustainability of this growth rate will plateau once the low-hanging fruit of tax-driven migration is harvested, forcing fund managers to generate returns based solely on operational superiority rather than regulatory arbitrage. ## The Regulatory Architecture: A Double-Edged Sword The operational viability of GIFT City rests on a complex, highly specific set of tax exemptions and safe harbor provisions. While these frameworks make the jurisdiction competitive, they demand exacting compliance. A single misstep can trigger catastrophic tax liabilities. ### Section 9A and Safe Harbor Provisions The cornerstone of offshore fund management from India is governed by Section 9A of the Income Tax Act, read with CBDT Notification No. 86/2020/F.No.370142/6/2020-TPL. This notification established the "Safe Harbor" provisions, which stipulate that an offshore fund shall not be deemed to be a resident of India merely because its fund manager is located in India, provided stringent conditions are met. These conditions include, but are not limited to: * The fund must have at least 25 members (with no single member holding more than 10% of the fund). * The total contribution by Indian residents must not exceed 50% of the total fund corpus. * The fund manager must be an independent entity not acting as a mere post-box. **The Skeptical View:** The Safe Harbor rules are notoriously rigid. The 25-member/10% concentration limit often conflicts with the operational realities of large-scale private equity or venture capital, where anchor investors frequently require commitments exceeding the 10% threshold. While subsequent amendments have provided relaxation for certain categories, the structural friction remains. Fund managers must continuously audit their unitholder register to ensure compliance; a single oversubscribed LP can inadvertently violate the safe harbor, rendering the entire offshore fund taxable in India. ### Section 10(4D) Exemption and FEMA Compliance The tax efficacy of GIFT City funds was significantly bolstered by the Income Tax Act - Section 10(4D) Exemption, read with the Finance Act, 2023 amendments. Section 10(4D) provides that specified income received by an offshore fund from investments in India (routed through an IFSC) is exempt from income tax, provided the fund is appropriately regulated and complies with the prescribed conditions. Crucially, the Finance Act 2023 amendments expanded the scope of this exemption, but with a non-negotiable caveat: Funds must strictly comply with FEMA FDI/FPI schedules and sectoral caps. **The Protective Stance:** This is the highest-risk compliance node in GIFT City. The exemption under Section 10(4D) is entirely contingent upon adherence to Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) regulations regarding Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) and Foreign Portfolio Investment (FPI) schedules, as well as sectoral caps. If a GIFT City fund inadvertently breaches a sectoral cap (e.g., exceeding the 20% FDI cap in the insurance sector, or violating downstream investment restrictions in multi-brand retail), the Section 10(4D) exemption collapses. The consequence is not merely a regulatory penalty; it triggers full retrospective taxation on the fund’s Indian income. Investment professionals must implement real-time compliance firewalls to monitor sectoral exposure against FEMA schedules. ## Top Picks for 2026: Where Capital is Flowing Given the regulatory constraints and the overarching need for tax-efficient repatriation, Indian fund managers in GIFT City are not deploying capital uniformly. As of Q1 2026, the most actionable and structurally sound allocations fall into four distinct categories. ### 1. Offshore India-Centric Equity Feeder Structures **The Thesis:** The most dominant pick remains the IFSC-domiciled feeder fund that pools offshore capital to invest in an onshore India-focused AIF or PMS. **The Mechanics:** Foreign LPs invest USD into the GIFT City fund, which then deploys capital into an onshore rupee-denominated fund. **Why it works:** This structure allows foreign investors to bypass the complexities of direct Indian market access while benefiting from the Section 10(4D) tax exemption on capital gains. For the fund manager, it allows them to manage the offshore pool from India under Section 9A safe harbor. **Actionable Insight:** Managers are heavily favoring mid-cap and small-cap equity strategies within these feeders. The alpha generation in Indian large-caps has compressed due to institutional dominance, whereas the $10.5 billion AUM influx is actively seeking high-growth, illiquid exposure in the 250th-500th market cap range, where information asymmetry still exists. ### 2. Global Multi-Asset Allocation for Domestic HNIs **The Thesis:** Indian High Net Worth Individuals (HNIs) are structurally under-allocated to global equities and fixed income. GIFT City allows Indian fund managers to offer reverse-feeder structures—pooling domestic capital to invest globally without the friction of the Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS) once the fund is fully subscribed. **The Mechanics:** An IFSC-based fund invests in US tech equities, global sovereign debt, or alternative assets. Under the current framework, investments made by IFSC funds are treated as foreign investments, allowing Indian investors to gain geographic diversification. **Why it works:** The Finance Act 2023 amendments and Section 10(4D) provide clarity on the taxation of such income. Capital gains from global investments routed through GIFT City are taxed at concessional rates compared to direct foreign investments under LRS, which are often subject to complex foreign tax credit mechanisms. **Actionable Insight:** The top pick here is US Treasury yields combined with tech-sector exposure. Managers are constructing "barbell" portfolios—anchoring 60-70% of the portfolio in 4.5%+ US Treasuries (providing USD stability and yield) while allocating the remainder to US large-cap tech (AI infrastructure and semiconductors) to capture upside. ### 3. Infrastructure and Energy Transition Debt **The Thesis:** India requires an estimated $500 billion to achieve its 2030 energy transition targets. Traditional Indian banking is constrained by sectoral exposure limits. GIFT City is emerging as the primary gateway for foreign capital to finance this gap. **The Mechanics:** IFSC-based AIFs (Category II) are issuing structured debt and mezzanine financing to Indian renewable energy developers and infrastructure conglomerates. **Why it works:** The returns on infrastructure debt in India range from 12-16% IRR in INR terms. By financing through GIFT City, fund managers can access cheaper USD funding from global LPs (pension funds and endowments) while charging a premium to Indian developers, capturing a lucrative interest rate arbitrage. Furthermore, interest income from infrastructure debt qualifies for specific exemptions, provided FEMA FDI schedules for the infrastructure sector are meticulously observed. **Actionable Insight:** Solar and wind energy yield-co structures are the top picks. Managers are targeting operational assets with locked-in PPAs (Power Purchase Agreements) rather than greenfield projects, mitigating construction risk while capturing a 200-300 basis point premium over comparable emerging market infrastructure debt. ### 4. Arbitrage and Absolute Return Strategies **The Thesis:** With $35 billion in projected market size, GIFT City’s banking ecosystem now possesses sufficient depth to support complex arbitrage strategies that were previously executed out of Singapore. **The Mechanics:** Utilizing the IFSC banking units for leveraged financing, managers are deploying capital into cross-currency arbitrage, NDF (Non-Deliverable Forward) market making, and onshore-offshore interest rate differentials. **Why it works:** The regulatory sandbox environment in GIFT City permits higher leverage ratios for certain IFSC-registered entities compared to their onshore counterparts. This allows for the efficient deployment of capital in low-volatility, high-frequency strategies. **Actionable Insight:** The INR-USD NDF arbitrage remains a compelling pick. Managers are exploiting the spread between the onshore INR forward market and the offshore NDF market. While the yield has compressed from 150 bps to roughly 60-80 bps as liquidity has improved, the risk-adjusted return in a high-interest-rate environment makes it a staple for institutional cash management. ## Risk Assessment: The Protective Framework While the top picks offer actionable alpha, TresoWealth maintains a structurally skeptical posture regarding the operational permanence of GIFT City's advantages. Investment professionals must hedge against three primary risks: 1. **Regulatory Creep and Retrospective Taxation:** The benefits of Section 10(4D) and Section 9A are statutory, not constitutional. A shift in fiscal policy—driven by a need to broaden the tax base—could amend or rescind these exemptions. Any fund with a 7-10 year lock-in must price in regulatory risk. The strict nexus between Section 10(4D) and FEMA compliance means that the Indian tax authorities have a low threshold for denying exemptions based on technical FPI schedule breaches. 2. **Substance over Form:** The CBDT has increasingly scrutinized "shell" fund managers operating out of GIFT City. To legitimately claim the Section 9A safe harbor, the fund manager must demonstrate substantive decision-making capability within the IFSC. Managers who treat their GIFT City offices as mere administrative outposts while executing investment decisions from Mumbai face severe audit risks. 3. **Liquidity and Market Depth:** Despite the 38% CAGR, the secondary market for GIFT City-domiciled funds remains illiquid. Private credit and equity feeders lack robust exit mechanisms beyond fund maturity. In a stress scenario—such as a rapid tightening of global liquidity—forced redemptions could lead to steep NAV discounts. ## Conclusion The $10.5 billion AUM currently residing in GIFT City is a testament to the successful implementation of a jurisdictional arbitrage model. The top picks for 2026—India-centric equity feeders, global multi-asset funds for HNIs, energy transition debt, and NDF arbitrage—reflect the optimal utilization of the current regulatory framework. However, sustainable alpha generation in GIFT City requires a departure from the "tax-first" mindset. The regulatory scaffolding provided by Section 9A (Notification No. 86/2020) and Section 10(4D) is powerful but brittle; a minor breach in FEMA sectoral caps can dismantle the entire tax structure. As the market matures toward its $35 billion projection, the fund managers who will endure are those who treat GIFT City not as a tax haven, but as a sophisticated, compliance-heavy financial center where operational excellence and rigorous risk mitigation are the primary drivers of returns.
