# How to Invest in GIFT CITY AIFs: A Step-by-Step Guide As of April 2026, the Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) has evolved from an ambitious regulatory sandbox into a critical conduit for global capital flows. For investment professionals, family offices, and institutional allocators, Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs) domiciled in GIFT City’s International Financial Services Centre (IFSC) represent a compelling intersection of offshore capital deployment and tax arbitrage. However, the structural complexity of IFSC regulations demands a rigorous, skeptical approach. The allure of tax exemptions often obscures the operational friction, compliance overhead, and liquidity constraints inherent in these vehicles. At TresoWealth, we advocate for a framework where capital protection and regulatory adherence supersede yield-chasing. This guide provides a precise, step-by-step breakdown of how to invest in GIFT City AIFs, grounded in the current regulatory realities of 2026. --- > **Key Metrics (2026):** > - 349 AIFs launched in GIFT City IFSC > - $22.11 billion assets under management > - 0% tax on capital gains and dividends (Section 10(4E)) > - 48-hour repatriation facility > - USD-denominated investment structures > ## The Current Landscape: Scale and Scrutiny Before deploying capital, allocators must understand the scale and velocity of capital within GIFT City. According to recent regulatory data and industry reports, total Assets Under Management (AUM) for AIFs in GIFT City currently stand at **$7.2 billion**. To contextualize this growth, total committed capital reached **$10.5 billion** as of March 2024 (End of FY 2023-24). While the trajectory is undeniably steep, a skeptical lens requires us to note the delta between committed capital and deployed AUM. A significant portion of committed capital remains undeployed, highlighting the execution challenges fund managers face in sourcing viable offshore assets that satisfy both IFSCA mandates and investor return thresholds. ## The Regulatory and Tax Framework Investing in a GIFT City AIF is fundamentally an exercise in navigating regulatory architecture. The viability of these vehicles rests on three primary pillars of legislation: ### 1. The Tax Exemption: Section 10(4D) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 The primary driver of GIFT City AIF investments is the tax pass-through status. Introduced by the Finance Act, 2015, **Section 10(4D)** stipulates that specified income received by a Category III AIF (including all categories of AIFs registered in IFSC) from investments made in offshore assets is exempt from income tax. This allows foreign investors and Indian residents investing via the Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS) to access global markets without the burden of Indian capital gains tax, provided the structural prerequisites are met. ### 2. DTAA Claims: CBDT Notification No. 74/2020 For global allocators utilizing GIFT City as a holding jurisdiction, Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements (DTAA) are critical. **CBDT Notification No. 74/2020/F.No. 370142/15/2020-TPL** fundamentally altered the documentation requirements for entities in IFSC claiming DTAA benefits. The notification mandates stringent Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) requirements and supplemental declarations. Allocators cannot assume seamless DTAA access; the burden of proof for beneficial ownership and substance rests heavily on the GIFT City entity. ### 3. Operational Mandate: Regulation 15A (March 27, 2024) Operational compliance was permanently altered by the IFSCA circular dated March 27, 2024. The introduction of **Regulation 15A** mandated the compulsory dematerialization of AIF units and the mandatory recording of all transfers, pledges, and transmissions exclusively in dematerialized form. This effectively eliminated the opaque, over-the-counter transfer of physical unit certificates, introducing a layer of transparency that protects investors but also requires strict depository compliance. --- ## Step-by-Step Guide to Investing in GIFT City AIFs ### Step 1: Determine Eligibility and Investor Classification GIFT City AIFs are not accessible to retail investors. Under IFSCA guidelines, only eligible investors may participate: * **Foreign Investors:** Non-resident Indians (NRIs), Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs), and foreign companies. * **Indian Residents:** Indian residents can invest via the Reserve Bank of India’s Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS), subject to the annual threshold (currently $250,000 per individual per financial year). * **Institutional Investors:** Indian and global banks, insurance companies, and pension funds. *Actionable Insight:* Indian residents must strictly monitor their LRS limits. Over-utilization of the LRS limit for AIF subscriptions can trigger FEMA violations, resulting in capital repatriation issues. ### Step 2: Select the Appropriate AIF Category GIFT City AIFs are categorized based on their investment mandate: * **Category I:** Invests in start-ups, SMEs, or social ventures. Highly incentivized by IFSCA but carries the highest illiquidity risk. * **Category II:** Private equity and debt funds. These do not receive specific IFSCA incentives but leverage the Section 10(4D) tax pass-through. * **Category III:** Hedge funds and complex multi-strategy funds. These employ diverse trading strategies and are the primary beneficiaries of the GIFT City tax arbitrage. *Skeptical Take:* Category III funds offer the most immediate tax relief but often carry opaque fee structures (performance fees, management fees, and hidden operational costs). Demand a full fee tear-sheet before committing capital. ### Step 3: Execute Rigorous Due Diligence on the Fund Manager The IFSCA has lowered the barriers to entry for fund managers (minimum capital requirement of $0.7 million for certain categories), leading to a proliferation of managers. * Verify the manager’s track record *outside* of GIFT City. * Assess the "substance" requirement. Does the manager have a physical presence in GIFT City, or are they merely a shell operation? Lack of substance can invalidate DTAA claims under Notification No. 74/2020. * Review the Fund’s Private Placement Memorandum (PPM) with external legal counsel. Pay specific attention to the indemnification clauses and the manager's right to suspend redemptions. ### Step 4: Fulfill KYC, AML, and Demat Requirements The onboarding process is heavily scrutinized to prevent round-tripping and money laundering. * **KYC/AML:** Submit identity and address proof, source of wealth (SOW), and source of funds (SOF). For FPIs, this involves providing the SEBI registration certificate and beneficial ownership details. * **Dematerialization (Regulation 15A):** In accordance with the March 27, 2024 circular, you must open a Demat account with a depository participant operating within the IFSC. No physical unit certificates will be issued. All subsequent transactions—capital calls, distributions, and secondary transfers—will be executed via this Demat account. ### Step 5: Banking and Capital Remittance GIFT City operates as a jurisdiction outside the Indian mainland for financial purposes. Capital movement requires specific banking infrastructure: * **Foreign Investors:** Capital is wired directly from offshore bank accounts into the AIF’s IFSC Banking Unit (IBU) account. * **Indian Residents:** Capital must be remitted from an Indian bank via LRS to the investor’s own IBU account in GIFT City, and subsequently subscribed to the AIF. Direct remittance from an onshore Indian account to the AIF’s IBU account is prohibited. *Actionable Insight:* Factor in currency conversion spreads. LRS remittances are made in USD, incurring conversion costs on both the outward and inward legs. These frictional costs can erode the tax arbitrage on smaller ticket sizes. ### Step 6: Subscription and Capital Call Management AIF investments are rarely single-check deployments. * Upon signing the Subscription Agreement, you commit a total amount (e.g., $1,000,000). * The manager will issue Capital Calls based on deployment velocity. Ensure your IBU account maintains sufficient liquidity to meet capital call timelines (usually 10-15 business days). * Failure to honor a capital call triggers severe penalties, including forfeiture of prior capital, dilution of ownership, and potential legal action by the manager. ### Step 7: Tax Claim and Post-Investment Compliance The final, and most frequently mismanaged step, is securing the tax benefits. * Ensure the AIF files its tax returns correctly under Section 10(4D). The exemption is not automatic; it must be actively claimed by the fund. * If you are utilizing the GIFT City structure to claim DTAA benefits on underlying global assets, ensure strict adherence to CBDT Notification No. 74/2020. Maintain a documented paper trail proving that the GIFT City entity is the beneficial owner of the assets, not merely an agent or conduit. --- ## Critical Pitfalls and Risk Mitigation While the $7.2 billion AUM milestone indicates maturation, GIFT City remains a complex ecosystem. Allocators must protect their capital against the following risks: **1. Regulatory Volatility** Tax laws and IFSCA regulations are subject to retrospective amendments. The Section 10(4D) exemption is a statutory provision, not a constitutional right. A change in government fiscal policy could alter the tax pass-through structure. *Mitigation:* Model your IRR with and without the tax exemption. If the investment only makes sense with the tax break, the risk-reward ratio is fundamentally flawed. **2. Illiquidity Risk** Secondary markets for GIFT City AIFs are virtually non-existent. If you need to exit before the fund's tenure ends, you must find a buyer yourself, secure manager approval, and execute the transfer via the Demat route under Regulation 15A. *Mitigation:* Do not allocate capital you may need to access within the fund's lock-in period. **3. Operational Friction** The promise of seamless global investing often collides with the reality of IFSC banking. IBUs can be slow to process transactions, and forex conversions can be delayed. *Mitigation:* Maintain a buffer of uncommitted cash in your IBU account to cover administrative delays and unexpected capital calls. **4. The "Round-Tripping" Shadow** Indian residents using LRS to invest in GIFT City AIFs that subsequently invest back into Indian assets face intense scrutiny from the Enforcement Directorate (ED) and the Income Tax Department. While legally permissible under certain IFSCA frameworks, the regulatory optics are highly sensitive. *Mitigation:* Avoid funds with mandates that allow "on-shore" investments if you are an Indian resident deploying LRS capital. --- ## Conclusion Investing in GIFT City AIFs offers a structurally superior mechanism for tax-efficient global capital deployment, as evidenced by the robust $10.5 billion in committed capital and $7.2 billion in realized AUM. The introduction of Regulation 15A has brought necessary transparency to unit ownership, and Section 10(4D) remains a powerful incentive for Category III fund allocations. However, the architecture is demanding. Success requires more than capital; it requires an uncompromising attention to regulatory detail, from LRS limits and Demat mandates to the stringent DTAA documentation required by Notification No. 74/2020.
