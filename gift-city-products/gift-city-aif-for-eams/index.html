# GIFT City AIFs for EAMs: The Ideal Route As of April 2026, the operational landscape for External Asset Managers (EAMs) advising on Indian allocations has fundamentally bifurcated. The traditional reliance on Foreign Portfolio Investor (FPI) structures, while still prevalent, is being aggressively challenged by the structural and fiscal efficiencies of Gujarat International Finance Tec-City (GIFT City) Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs). For EAMs managing offshore capital seeking Indian exposure, the GIFT City AIF is no longer an experimental jurisdiction; it is rapidly becoming the ideal route—provided the regulatory and fiscal architecture is navigated with absolute precision. The thesis for GIFT City AIFs rests on three pillars: regulatory symmetry with global standards, concentrated capital deployment, and unparalleled tax neutrality. However, a skeptical lens is required. The jurisdiction is not a regulatory vacuum, and the tax exemptions are conditional, demanding rigorous compliance architectures from EAMs. Here is a comprehensive dissection of why GIFT City AIFs represent the optimal, albeit exacting, pathway for EAMs in 2026. > **Key Metrics (2026):** > - 349 AIFs launched in GIFT City IFSC > - $22.11 billion assets under management > - 0% tax on capital gains and dividends (Section 10(4E)) > - 48-hour repatriation facility > - USD-denominated investment structures > ## The Capital Influx: Following the 48% CAGR Trajectory The migration toward GIFT City is not speculative; it is backed by demonstrable capital velocity. According to SEBI data as of Q2 2024, AIF commitments in GIFT City recorded a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 48%. This exponential growth curve reflects a structural reallocation of offshore capital rather than a fleeting arbitrage play. To understand the scale, one must examine the aggregate commitments. In FY 2023-24, total commitments in GIFT City AIFs reached an estimated $14.8 billion (₹1.23 lakh crore). This figure is not merely a testament to the jurisdiction's viability; it represents a critical mass of institutional capital that has validated the IFSC (International Financial Services Centre) framework. For EAMs, a 48% CAGR signals deepening liquidity and a maturing service provider ecosystem. Custodians, fund administrators, and compliance vendors have scaled their operations within GIFT City to accommodate this influx, significantly reducing the operational friction that plagued early adopters. However, EAMs must remain skeptical of surface-level growth metrics; the true test of a jurisdiction is not capital commitment, but capital deployment and repatriation—areas where GIFT City has proven increasingly robust. ## Scale and EAM Relevance: The $109 Million vs. $25 Million Disparity A critical metric for EAMs evaluating fund structures is the average ticket size and fund scale, which directly impacts the economics of the management mandate. Here, GIFT City presents a stark contrast to onshore realities. As of Q2 2024, the average onshore Indian AIF averages approximately $25 million in size. While onshore AIFs have proliferated, their relatively diminutive scale often translates to higher proportional compliance costs and fragmented mandates for EAMs. Conversely, GIFT City AIFs are fundamentally institutional in scale. As of Q2 2024, AIFs with an India-focus operating out of GIFT City managed approximately $109 million (approx. ₹910 crore) in deployed capital within India. This larger scale is a natural fit for EAMs who typically manage consolidated mandates for foreign institutions and ultra-high-net-worth individuals (UHNIs). The $109 million average deployment size allows EAMs to justify the setup costs of a bespoke mandate, negotiate institutional-grade fee structures, and execute block trades that would be impossible in a $25 million onshore vehicle. Furthermore, of the broader capital flowing through GIFT City, approximately $4.9 billion (approx. ₹41,000 crore) is currently deployed as FPIs. This indicates that a substantial portion of offshore capital is still utilizing the traditional FPI route. The strategic advantage for EAMs setting up GIFT City AIFs today is the ability to capture the ongoing transition—converting FPI mandates into AIF structures to unlock tax efficiencies and strategic flexibility that FPIs simply cannot match. ## The Regulatory Architecture: The IFSCA-SEBI Bridge A common misconception among offshore practitioners is that GIFT City operates entirely outside the purview of Indian regulators. This is structurally false and operationally dangerous. The regulatory framework is a bridged architecture, demanding dual awareness. The base regulation governing AIFs remains the **SEBI AIF Regulations 2012**. Through **IFSCA Regulation 30**, the IFSCA has adopted the SEBI AIF Regulations 2012 as the baseline, overlaying them with IFSCA-specific modifications that cater to offshore capital. For EAMs, this regulatory symmetry is a double-edged sword. On one hand, it provides the comfort of a recognized, robust regulatory framework familiar to global allocators. On the other hand, it means that the fiduciary responsibilities, risk management mandates, and governance standards enforced by the EAM do not bypass IFSCA/SEBI fee and reporting structures. ### Actionable Insight for Regulatory Compliance: EAMs must establish compliance infrastructures that satisfy IFSCA’s reporting timelines while adhering to the substantive requirements of SEBI AIF Regulations 2012. This includes rigorous portfolio concentration limits, valuation guidelines, and disclosure requirements. Any attempt to use the IFSC overlay to dilute SEBI’s base protections will result in regulatory pushback. The ideal route requires treating GIFT City not as a loophole, but as a specialized economic zone with stringent regulatory oversight. ## The Fiscal Imperative: Section 10(4D) and the TRC Trap The single most compelling driver for EAMs to choose GIFT City AIFs over traditional FPI structures is the tax neutrality afforded under the Income Tax Act, 1961. However, this neutrality is highly conditional, and a failure to adhere strictly to the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT) notifications can result in catastrophic tax leakage for investors. ### Section 10(4D) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 Section 10(4D) provides the foundational tax exemption for GIFT City AIFs. It stipulates that specified income received by a GIFT City AIF from its investments in India is exempt from income tax, provided the fund complies with the prescribed conditions. This effectively creates a pass-through structure for offshore investors, ensuring that the fund vehicle itself is not subject to Indian taxation, and the tax liability flows directly to the non-resident investor (often resulting in zero or minimal tax under applicable DTAA rates). ### CBDT Notification 89/2020 / S.O. 3555(E) The conditions for availing the Section 10(4D) exemption are detailed in CBDT Notification Number 89/2020 / S.O. 3555(E). This notification mandates strict compliance regarding the nature of the fund, the residency of investors, and the reporting mechanisms. EAMs must ensure that the AIF's investment strategy does not breach the specified asset class restrictions outlined in the notification. ### The Section 10 TRC Requirement: A Critical Pitfall The most critical—and frequently underestimated—compliance requirement hinges on **Section 10** of the Income Tax Act, specifically concerning Tax Residency Certificates (TRCs). To avail the tax benefits, the AIF must secure TRCs from *all* Foreign HNIs and institutional investors. This is not a sampling exercise; it is an absolute statutory requirement. If a single investor in the AIF fails to provide a valid TRC from their jurisdiction of tax residence, the entire fund risks disqualification from the Section 10(4D) exemption. For EAMs, this represents a massive operational risk. During capital calls and subscription phases, EAMs and their fund administrators must implement a hard stop: no TRC, no admission. The protective stance here is non-negotiable. An EAM's onboarding workflow must be engineered to collect, verify, and archive TRCs prior to the acceptance of funds. Failure to enforce this will expose the EAM to severe liability from limited partners (LPs) who face unexpected withholding tax liabilities. ## Structuring the Mandate: Actionable Insights for EAMs Given the regulatory and fiscal realities of GIFT City, how should an EAM structure their practice to leverage this route effectively? ### 1. Transitioning from FPI to Category II/III AIFs With $4.9 billion still sitting in FPI structures within GIFT City, EAMs have a distinct opportunity to restructure these mandates. FPIs are subject to surcharges on short-term capital gains and are constrained by investment limits in unlisted securities. By transitioning an FPI mandate into a GIFT City Category II or III AIF, EAMs can access the Section 10(4D) pass-through, invest in unlisted debt and equity without FPI sectoral caps, and align the fee structure with the $109 million average deployment scale. ### 2. Implementing TRC-Centric Onboarding As emphasized, the Section 10 TRC requirement is the Achilles' heel of GIFT City AIFs. EAMs must mandate their legal counsel to draft subscription agreements that explicitly condition the drawdown of capital on the receipt of a valid, current-year TRC. Automated compliance dashboards should flag missing TRCs 30 days before fiscal year-end to prevent any disruption to the fund's tax-exempt status. ### 3. Navigating the IFSCA/SEBI Fee Architecture Do not assume that GIFT City offers a fee holiday. While the IFSCA has streamlined certain processes, EAMs must budget for SEBI registration fees, IFSCA annual fees, and the ongoing costs of compliance with SEBI AIF Regulations 2012 (via IFSCA Reg 30). The economic viability of the fund must be modeled assuming full compliance costs. Given the $109 million average deployment size, the economics remain highly favorable compared to the $25 million onshore average, but only if the fee architecture is accurately modeled from inception. ### 4. Substance and Governance The IFSCA requires genuine substance in GIFT City. EAMs cannot operate a "letterbox" fund. The fund must have a physical presence, at least one key managerial personnel residing in India, and a board of directors that meets the residency and independence criteria. EAMs must partner with local compliance officers who understand the nuances of IFSCA reporting, ensuring that the EAM's investment decisions are executed within the bounds of the fund's constitutional documents and IFSCA guidelines. ## The Skeptical Lens: Friction Points in 2026 Despite the compelling data, the GIFT City AIF route is not without friction. A precise and protective approach demands acknowledgment of the ongoing challenges: * **Banking Latency:** While improved since 2024, the repatriation of funds from Indian investee companies back to the GIFT City AIF, and subsequently to offshore LPs, can still face intermediary banking delays. EAMs must manage LP liquidity expectations accordingly. * **Regulatory Evolution:** The IFSCA is an active regulator, frequently issuing circulars that refine or alter operational requirements. EAMs must retain ongoing regulatory counsel; a structure established in 2024 may require recalibration in 2026 to remain compliant. * **KYC and AML Scrutiny:** Global standards for Anti-Money Laundering (AML) are tightening. GIFT City entities are under immense pressure to demonstrate pristine KYC protocols. EAMs must expect, and prepare for, intrusive source-of-wealth inquiries from GIFT City banking partners, particularly when onboarding LPs from complex holding structures. ## Conclusion: The Calculated Ideal The data is unambiguous. The 48% CAGR, the $14.8 billion in FY 2023-24 commitments, and the $109 million average deployment size collectively demonstrate that GIFT City AIFs have achieved escape velocity. For EAMs, the GIFT City AIF is the ideal route to access Indian alpha for offshore capital. However, "ideal" does not mean effortless. The jurisdiction demands a disciplined, precise, and skeptical approach. The regulatory bridge of IFSCA Regulation 30 tethering to SEBI AIF Regulations 2012 ensures that fiduciary standards remain high. More critically, the conditional tax exemption under Section 10(4D), governed by the strictures of CBDT Notification 89/2020 and the unforgiving Section 10 TRC requirements, means that operational sloppiness will be severely penalized. For the EAM who respects the regulatory framework, enforces rigorous TRC compliance, and structures their economics to account for the true costs of IFSCA substance, the GIFT City AIF offers an unparalleled combination of scale, tax efficiency, and strategic flexibility. In 2026, it is not just an alternative; it is the primary arena for sophisticated offshore capital entering India.
